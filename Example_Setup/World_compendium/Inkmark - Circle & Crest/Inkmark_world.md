# Inkmark — World Lore Compendium

## 1. WORLD OVERVIEW

### OVERVIEW -- Inkmark at a Glance
**Genre:** Medieval fantasy with Fire Emblem tactical energy and Witch Hat Atelier's grounded magic. Castles, kingdoms, swords, spell circles, crests, pegasi knights. No gunpowder, no technology. Magic is a craft, not a superpower. Warriors are deadly without magic. The world is colorful, pan-cultural, and war is coming.
**Tone:** GRAY MORALITY, TACTICAL DRAMA — serious stakes, larger-than-life personalities, moments of levity. People make terrible decisions for good reasons. Rivals become allies. The world is dangerous but the people in it burn bright.
**Year:** 847 of the Convergence Era
**Population:** ~35 million across three continents
**Continents:** Aethmark (European medieval — feudal kingdoms, knightly orders, cathedral universities), Ryuten (East Asian-inspired — shogunate provinces, temple complexes, honor-bound warrior traditions), Solmir (Middle Eastern/Mediterranean-inspired — desert empires, bazaar cities, faith-mandated magic traditions)
**The Divide:** Every living person has mana. It's as fundamental as breath. But only ~25% can shape it — pattern mages, born with the talent to channel mana through traced circles. The remaining 75% feel mana in their bones but can't control it. They're warriors, craftsmen, riders, ordinary people. There is no aura, no second path. A mage traces circles to cast. A warrior fights with steel, skill, and — if born lucky — a crest. A non-mage with a tattoo can cast that one spell. This makes tattoos the most coveted thing on Inkmark — they let anyone do magic.
**Magic:** Spell circles — specific geometric patterns that channel mana into effects. Everyone has mana; only ~25% can shape it through traced circles. Each spell is a unique pattern. No element locking — any mage can learn any pattern category, but mastery takes time. Casting speed depends on medium: mental trace (slow, mages only), book/scroll (fast, degrades), inscribed equipment (durable, anyone can use), tattoo (instant, anyone can use, 1-2 spells, requires three rare materials). The pattern is the spell. The circle is the container. Mana is the fuel — and everyone has it.
**Crests:** Rare bloodline gifts that grant warriors specific supernatural abilities. One crest = one power. Inherited through noble bloodlines or, rarely, discovered. Crests are politically significant — they define noble houses, fuel succession crises, and make their bearers strategic assets.
**The Convergence:** An island academy at the center of all three continents. The only place where magic tattoos can be inscribed — requiring three rare materials, one from each continent, each controlled by a royal monopoly. Treaty-bound. Competition-based intake. Pattern library. Combat training. Crest registry. Diplomatic hostage ground dressed up as a school. This is where the story starts.
**Core Conflict:** Three continents bound by an uneasy treaty. Aethmark's kingdoms scheme for territory. Ryuten's provinces fracture along honor and tradition. Solmir's empire enforces religious orthodoxy with fire. The Convergence Treaty holds — barely. Every nation sends heirs and candidates to the island, and every nation plots to gain advantage. The tattoo materials are leverage. The crests are currency. The patterns are power. And someone, somewhere, is tracing forbidden circles.
**Coin:** 1 gold sovereign = 10 silver marks = 100 copper pence. A peasant family lives on 20-30 copper/week. A trained warrior earns 3-5 gold/month. A battle mage with a full spellbook earns 5-10 gold/month. A spellbook of rare patterns is worth more than a horse.
**Mounts:** Pegasi (intelligent, prideful, mountain-born flying mounts — elite cavalry). Wyverns (aggressive, powerful, hard to tame — fewer in number, more feared). Horses (standard cavalry, still dominant on the ground). Dire wolves and great cats in Ryuten's frontier provinces.

## 2. POWER SYSTEM

### POWER_SYSTEM -- The Two Paths: Mage and Warrior
**Type:** Universal divide with crossover possibility
**The Split:** Every person on Inkmark has mana. It's universal — as fundamental as blood. But the ability to shape it varies wildly. Approximately 25% are born with the talent to channel mana through pattern circles — pattern mages. The remaining 75% have mana but can't control it. They feel it — a tingling warmth in stressful moments, a faint resonance near active patterns — but they can't direct it. They can't trace. They can't cast. They're not empty; they're just untrained in the one way that matters. A mage who never learned patterns has mana tingling through them with no way to shape it. A warrior with a tattoo has mana flowing through an inscribed pattern they couldn't draw themselves — and that changes everything.
**Mages:** Tracing spell circles to cast magic. Each spell is a specific geometric pattern. Mana flows through the pattern and produces an effect. Without a pattern, mana is useless. Without mana, a pattern is just geometry. Both are required. Mages are craftworkers — their power is in their knowledge (which patterns they know), their precision (how well they trace), their speed (how fast they cast), and their mana reserves (how much they can cast before exhaustion).
**Warriors:** Pure martial combatants. Steel, skill, training, and physical conditioning. A veteran warrior with a lance is deadly without any magic. Warriors are not lesser than mages — they are a different category. A mage who gets rushed in melee is in trouble. A warrior who faces a prepared mage at range is in trouble. Combined formations win wars. Solo duelists win duels. The rivalry between mages and warriors is real, mostly friendly, and occasionally bloody.
**Crest-Bearers:** Rare individuals born with — or who discover — a crest, a supernatural mark on their back that grants one specific power. Crests manifest on the back, between the shoulder blades, where the pattern geometry is largest and most complex. This is the same surface area needed for a back tattoo. You cannot have both a crest and a back tattoo — they occupy the same space. This is not a coincidence. One crest = one power. A Flame Crest-bearer's blade ignites. That's it. No resilience, no speed boost, just fire on their strikes. The power is specific, limited, and game-changing within its scope. Crests are politically explosive — noble houses are defined by them, marriages arranged around them, succession crises fought over them.
**Spellblades (Rare):** A mage who also trains as a warrior, or a warrior who discovers magical talent. Extremely difficult — splitting training between two disciplines means mastering neither as quickly as a specialist. But a mage who can fight in melee, inscribe patterns on their own blade, or tattoo combat-relevant spells becomes devastating. The path is rare because it requires both innate talent and obsessive dedication. Most spellblades are mediocre at both and excellent at neither. The ones who pull it off become legends.
**The Mutual Respect (Mostly):** Mages need warriors for protection, for melee combat, for holding ground. Warriors need mages for ranged support, healing, reconnaissance, and enchanted equipment. The best armies integrate both. The rivalry exists — mages think warriors are crude, warriors think mages are fragile — but in a real fight, both sides know they need each other. Tavern arguments about which path is "better" have started brawls in every city on Inkmark.

### POWER_SYSTEM -- Mana and Exhaustion
**Type:** Universal resource system (everyone has mana, not just mages)
**Mana:** The energy that flows through every living person on Inkmark. Every person has it. Every person feels it — a warmth in the blood, a tingling in stressful moments, a resonance near active patterns. But only the ~25% born with pattern talent can shape it voluntarily through traced circles. For the remaining 75%, mana is potential without direction — until a tattoo or crest gives it a channel. Mana depth — how much you can channel before exhaustion — varies by individual and grows with use. Trained mages have the deepest reserves through daily practice. Non-mages have smaller reserves. Crest-bearers have natural reserves appropriate to their conditioning.
**Exhaustion Stages:**
- **Fresh:** Full reserves. Normal casting (or tattoo/crest activation).
- **Strained:** Headache, trembling hands. Accuracy suffers. Patterns traced at this stage have higher failure rates. Mages feel their reserves thinning. Non-mages with tattoos feel a comparable drain.
- **Depleted:** Nosebleed, tunnel vision, loss of fine motor control. Casting at this stage is dangerous — patterns misfire, backfire, or flatline. Tattoo activation at this stage flickers weakly. Crest activation at this stage sputters. Wise users stop here.
- **Overdrawn:** Channeling past depletion. Internal damage. Mana burns through the body instead of through patterns. Permanent injury risk. Mages who overdraw frequently develop mana scars — visible lines where mana burned pathways through their flesh. Non-mages who overdraw through tattoos risk the tattoo itself destabilizing. Recovery takes weeks to months.
- **Critical Overdraw:** Fatal territory. The body cannot sustain the mana throughput. Death or permanent disability.
**Regeneration:** Passive regeneration with rest, sleep, and food. Active meditation roughly doubles recovery speed. Alchemical preparations accelerate further but with diminishing returns and eventual toxicity. There are no shortcuts. A depleted mage needs hours to recover. An overdrawn mage needs days to weeks. A non-mage who overdrew through a tattoo needs similar recovery time.
**Mana is Universal, Not Exclusive:** Everyone has mana. It's not a special resource that only mages possess — it's as fundamental as breath. The difference is control. Mages can shape mana through traced patterns. Non-mages can only channel mana through pre-made shapes: tattoos, crests, and inscribed equipment. A warrior with an inscribed flame-sword is using their own mana to ignite the blade. A non-mage with a healing tattoo is using their own mana to close a wound. The mana is theirs. The pattern just gives it direction.

### POWER_SYSTEM -- Casting Mediums
**Type:** The four ways to cast spells
**Mental Trace (No Medium):**
- Tracing a pattern in the air with a finger or visualizing it in the mind.
- Slowest casting method. Minimum several seconds for simple patterns. Complex patterns can take 30 seconds to minutes.
- Only works for patterns the mage has fully internalized through thousands of repetitions.
- No physical cost except mana. Always available.
- Master mages can mental-trace quickly because they've practiced each pattern thousands of times. Novice mages are functionally book-dependent.
- Mental tracing leaves no trace — the pattern vanishes after casting. Useful for subtlety.

**Book/Scroll/Card (Printed Medium):**
- Channeling mana through a pre-printed pattern on paper, parchment, or card.
- Fast casting. Seconds or near-instant for simple patterns. The mage activates the pattern by touching it and channelling mana.
- **Durability Degradation:** Each use degrades the medium. Narrative states: Fresh → Worn → Fading → Unreadable → Destroyed. Simple patterns barely degrade the page. Powerful patterns can destroy a page in 1-3 uses.
- A mage's spellbook is their most valuable possession. Losing your spellbook means relying on mental traces only — drastically limited.
- Spellbook quality matters. Ink quality, paper quality, and pattern precision all affect durability and casting reliability. Cheap spellbooks degrade faster. Masterwork spellbooks last through hundreds of casts.
- Cards and scrolls are disposable alternatives — single-purpose, less durable, cheaper. Military mages carry combat card decks for rapid casting and swap out destroyed cards between engagements.

**Inscribed (Equipment Medium):**
- Patterns carved or etched into weapons, armor, tools, and structures.
- Durable. Metal: decades with maintenance. Stone: centuries. Wood: years. Cloth/leather: months.
- Anyone can activate an inscribed item — the pattern provides the shape, the user's innate mana provides the fuel. A warrior swinging an inscribed flame-sword channels their own mana through the pattern to ignite the blade. A mage using the same sword gets more precise control due to pattern-tracing experience, but the activation itself works for anyone.
- Fixed effect. An inscribed flame-sword only does flame-sword. No versatility.
- Non-mages tire faster when using inscribed equipment because their mana reserves are smaller. A warrior can sustain an inscribed weapon through a battle but will feel the drain. Extended use causes the same exhaustion stages as spellcasting.
- Structural inscriptions (warded walls, bridge supports, warming circles in floors) are the backbone of civilized infrastructure in all three continents. Maintenance required — ward-keepers, circle-keepers, and enchanter-smiths are steady professions.

**Tattoo (Skin Medium):**
- Pattern tattooed directly onto the skin using a special ink composed of three rare materials — one from each continent.
- **Works for everyone.** This is the key. A tattoo channels the bearer's mana through the inscribed pattern. Since every person on Inkmark has mana, even the 75% who can't trace patterns can activate a tattoo. Touch the tattoo, will the mana through, effect fires. No tracing skill required. This is why tattoos are the most coveted magical advantage on Inkmark — they let anyone cast.
- Instant casting. Touch the tattoo, will mana through, effect fires. No tracing time. No degradation. Unlimited uses (limited only by mana — and everyone has mana).
- **Limited to 1-2 spells.** Only the back and stomach have enough flat surface area for complex pattern geometry. A single complex pattern takes most of the back. Two simple patterns (one back, one stomach) is the maximum.
- **Back slot conflict:** A crest manifests on the back. A tattoo occupies the back. You cannot have both. A crest-bearer cannot get a back tattoo because the crest pattern occupies the space. A mage with a back tattoo cannot manifest a crest in the same area. The body has room for one major pattern on the back. Choose wisely.
- **Requires three materials:** Lunite (Aethmark), Siltberry resin (Ryuten), Ashbloom extract (Solmir). All three must be combined to create the ink. The Convergence Academy is the only place where all three materials converge and the only facility authorized to inscribe tattoo patterns.
- **Painful and permanent.** Getting a tattoo takes 2-3 days. The ink bonds with mana channels under the skin. Removal is agonizing, risks permanent mana channel scarring, and takes weeks of recovery. Changing a tattoo is not practical.
- **Visible.** A tattoo on the back or stomach is visible when the bearer removes their shirt. Social implications vary — some cultures revere tattoo bearers, others distrust them. For non-mages, a tattoo is walking proof that you were chosen for inscription. For mages, it's proof of skill or connections.
- **Politically controlled.** The Convergence Treaty limits tattoo inscription to competition winners. Each nation controls their material supply. This makes tattoo bearers political assets — they are walking proof of national investment and Convergence success.
- **The prize.** A non-mage with a tattoo can cast one spell. A mage with a tattoo can cast one spell instantly, without a book, without tracing, forever. A warrior with a healing tattoo is a medic who never runs out of bandages. A scout with a Gale tattoo can move like wind once a day. This is why people kill for tattoo inscription slots.
- **Mana cost for non-mages:** A non-mage's mana reserves are smaller than a trained mage's. They can cast their tattoo spell a limited number of times before exhaustion — usually 2-5 casts depending on the pattern's complexity and the bearer's conditioning. A mage with the same tattoo can cast more times before exhaustion because their mana reserves are deeper. But even 2 casts of a healing pattern from a warrior who shouldn't be able to do magic at all? That changes battles.

### POWER_SYSTEM -- Progression
**Type:** Skill-based, descriptive, no numerical tiers
**Mages progress by:**
- Learning more patterns (breadth)
- Internalizing patterns for mental tracing (depth)
- Deepening mana reserves through daily practice (endurance)
- Improving tracing speed and precision (skill)
- No tiers, no ranks, no breakthroughs. A "master" fire mage is master because they know forty fire patterns, can mental-trace a dozen of them, and have mana reserves deep enough to cast for an extended engagement. A journeyman knows ten patterns, can mental-trace two, and tires after a few minutes of combat casting. The difference is practice, not power level.
- You cannot "break through" to mastery. You practice until you earn it.

**Warriors progress by:**
- Combat experience and technique refinement
- Physical conditioning — strength, speed, endurance
- Tactical knowledge and battlefield awareness
- Equipment quality — better steel, better armor, inscribed weapons
- Crest mastery (if crest-bearing) — understanding and maximizing their specific gift
- A 20-year veteran is better than a fresh recruit because they've fought more battles, learned more techniques, and have better instincts. No magic. No aura. Just skill.

**Crest-bearers progress by:**
- Understanding their crest's specific power
- Learning creative applications of their single gift
- Physical and tactical training (they're still warriors first, enhanced second)
- Some crests are straightforward (you're stronger). Some require exploration (your crest does something with fire — what exactly? Figure it out)
- A crest-bearer who has mastered their gift is significantly more capable than one who just discovered it. The power doesn't grow — the mastery does.

## 3. MAGIC SYSTEM

### MAGIC_SYSTEM -- Circle Patterns
**Type:** WHA-inspired spell pattern system
**Core Principle:** Every spell is a unique geometric pattern. The pattern is the spell. A fire bolt is one pattern. A fire wall is a different pattern. A fire lantern is a third. There is no "fire element" that you channel — there are specific patterns that produce specific fire effects. You learn patterns individually. You master them through repetition.
**Pattern Anatomy:**
- **The Outer Ring:** The container. Every pattern has at least one. Defines the boundary of the effect.
- **The Core Point:** Center where mana pools before being distributed through the pattern.
- **Conduit Lines:** Lines connecting elements within the pattern, directing mana flow.
- **Glyphs:** Symbolic elements within the pattern that modify and refine the effect. Not strictly necessary for basic spells, but essential for precision, power, and complex effects. Think of them as punctuation — the spell works without them, but not as well.
- **Modifiers:** Smaller patterns nested within or around the main pattern that adjust range, duration, area, or intensity. A fire bolt pattern with a range modifier hits further. The same pattern without is shorter range.
**Learning Patterns:**
- Patterns must be learned from a teacher, copied from a book, or discovered through research.
- No one is born knowing patterns. A mage who never learned a single pattern has mana but can't do anything with it.
- The first pattern a mage learns is usually simple — spark, light, warmth, push, pull. These are the alphabet of magic. Everyone learns the same basics.
- As mages advance, they learn more complex and specialized patterns. Military patterns (fire lance, barrier, healing) are restricted knowledge. Forbidden patterns are illegal to even copy.
- Pattern knowledge IS power. Knowing more patterns gives you more options. A mage with a hundred-pattern spellbook is exponentially more versatile than a mage with ten. But versatility isn't the same as mastery — knowing a hundred patterns poorly is less effective than knowing ten patterns perfectly.

**Pattern Categories (not domains, not elements — categories of effect):**
- **Ignition:** Fire, heat, light, warmth, cauterization, explosion. From lighting a candle to laying a fire line across a battlefield.
- **Flow:** Water, ice, healing (flesh is mostly water), currents, blood manipulation (forbidden at advanced levels). The most versatile category — everyone needs water.
- **Gale:** Wind, speed, flight, sound, breath, air pressure. Scouts, messengers, and anyone who wants to move fast.
- **Storm:** Lightning, paralysis, magnetism, ward-breaking. Poorly understood. Some patterns are easier for certain bloodlines, but anyone can learn them. A storm mage is feared even by allies.
- **Stone:** Earth, fortification, construction, terrain shift, mineral manipulation. The foundation of civilization — literally.
- **Radiance:** Light, purification, disease-healing, truth detection, memory manipulation (forbidden at advanced levels). Sacred in Solmir. Suspected everywhere else.
- **Veil:** Shadow, concealment, fear, mana absorption, necromancy (forbidden at advanced levels). Stigmatized. Veil mages hide their specialty in polite company.
- **Spirit:** Beast-bonding, empathy, ancestral communion, dominion/mind control (forbidden at advanced levels). Watched carefully. The peaceful applications are beautiful. The potential for abuse keeps spirit mages under scrutiny.
**Important:** These are categories of effect, not locked paths. Any mage can learn any category. A mage who studies ignition patterns for ten years will know more fire patterns than someone who just started. But the same mage can also learn flow patterns if they devote the time. There are no born affinities. You learn what you study.

### MAGIC_SYSTEM -- Pattern Progression and Backfire
**Type:** Skill-based casting progression (WHA/FE inspired)
**Core Principle:** If you can draw it, you can cast it. There is no "tier gate" preventing a novice from attempting a complex pattern. The only barriers are geometric precision and mana. A novice with a book open in front of them CAN attempt a master-tier pattern. The question is whether they can draw it correctly — and the consequences of failure scale with the pattern's complexity.

**Backfire Scale:**
- **Minor error, simple pattern:** Weakened or warped effect. A fire bolt becomes a spark. A healing pattern soothes instead of closes. Unpleasant but survivable.
- **Minor error, moderate pattern:** Painful failure. The pattern collapses and the mana discharges as uncontrolled energy. Nausea, headache, tingling extremities. The mage is rattled but functional.
- **Major error, complex pattern:** Explosive failure. Mana discharges violently. Burns, nerve damage, possible permanent injury. The pattern literally backfires — fire patterns burn the caster, stone patterns crush them, flow patterns drown them in their own body's water. This is how mages die in training accidents.
- **Catastrophic error, forbidden pattern:** Death or worse. Forbidden patterns are forbidden not just because of what they do when they work, but because the geometric complexity is so extreme that even attempting them without mastery courts disaster. An improperly drawn necromantic pattern can kill the caster and animate nothing. An improperly drawn dominion pattern can shatter the caster's own mind.

**Training Timelines:**
- **Learning a new simple pattern from a book:** Minimum 2 weeks of daily practice before the pattern is reliable. The mage traces it repeatedly until the geometry is internalized in their hand. Rushing this leads to backfire.
- **Learning a moderate pattern from a book:** 1-3 months. The geometry is more complex, the margin for error is thinner, and the mana channeling requirements are higher.
- **Learning a complex pattern from a book:** 6-12 months. These patterns have nested rings, multiple conduits, and precise glyph placement. A single misaligned line can cause catastrophic backfire.
- **Internalizing a pattern for mental tracing:** 6-12 months of daily repetition for a simple pattern. 2-5 years for a moderate pattern. 5+ years for a complex one. This is why spellbooks matter — you don't need to memorize if you have the page. Most mages never internalize more than a handful of patterns.
- **A master fire mage isn't master because they can cast bigger fire.** They're master because they know forty fire patterns, can mental-trace a dozen of them from memory, and can draw any of them with sufficient precision that backfire is a distant risk. Mastery is precision and breadth, not raw power.

**What This Means in Practice:**
- A novice with a book CAN cast any pattern in that book, but slowly, and with increasing risk as pattern complexity rises.
- A master without a book can only cast the patterns they've internalized — usually 5-15, depending on years of practice.
- A master WITH a book is the full package: speed, precision, and versatility. This is why spellbook destruction is a proven military tactic.
- The gap between novice and master is precision and speed, not power ceiling. A perfectly drawn fire bolt from a novice and a perfectly drawn fire bolt from a master produce the same effect. The master is just far more likely to draw it perfectly, and far faster at doing so.

**Pattern Difficulty Spectrum (for GM reference):**
- **Simple patterns (2 weeks to learn from book):** Spark, light, warmth, push, pull, minor healing, small barrier. Single ring, minimal glyphs. Most military mages know 10-20 of these.
- **Moderate patterns (1-3 months to learn):** Fire bolt, ice shard, water blade, wind push, stone spike, barrier wall, flesh healing. Single ring with conduits, 3-5 glyphs. This is journeyman level. Most active mages operate here.
- **Complex patterns (6-12 months to learn):** Fire lance, ice fortress, sustained flight, chain lightning, terrain shift, limb regeneration. Nested rings, multiple conduits, 6-12 glyphs. This is where specialists live.
- **Advanced patterns (years to master):** Firestorm, localized weather control, mass healing, shadow army, domain-level effects. Triple-nested rings, geometric amplification, 12+ glyphs. A mage who can reliably cast these from memory is considered a master by any standard.
- **Forbidden patterns (theoretical for most):** Necromantic animation, dominion override, blood weaponization. Extreme geometric complexity with zero margin for error. Even possessing the pattern is a crime. Attempting it without decades of experience is elaborate suicide.

### MAGIC_SYSTEM -- Spellbooks and Pattern Economy
**Type:** Knowledge economy
**The Spellbook Is Everything:** A mage without their spellbook is a mage who can only cast from memory — and most mages haven't internalized more than a handful of patterns well enough to mental-trace under pressure. Losing your spellbook is devastating. Protecting your spellbook is a cultural obsession.
**Spellbook Construction:**
- Hand-copied pattern pages bound into leather, vellum, or reinforced covers.
- Quality determines durability. Cheaper books — thinner paper, less precise ink, weaker binding — degrade faster.
- Masterwork spellbooks — thick vellum pages, precise ink, reinforced spines — can survive hundreds of castings per page before degradation becomes noticeable.
- A mage's spellbook is a reflection of their training, their teachers, and their access to pattern knowledge. Every spellbook is unique.

**The Pattern Trade:**
- Pattern pages are bought, sold, traded, stolen, and hoarded.
- Military patterns are classified. Universities guard their pattern libraries. Noble houses protect family patterns passed through generations.
- The black market in forbidden patterns is lucrative and lethal. Getting caught with necromancy patterns is a death sentence in most jurisdictions.
- Pattern scribes are a profession — skilled copyists who reproduce pattern pages for a fee. A good scribe is worth their weight in gold.
- Spellbook printing presses exist in major cities, producing standard pattern books for common spells. Cheap, functional, but fast-degrading pages. Every military mage starts with one.

**Combat Cards:**
- Disposable card-stock pages printed with single patterns. Carried in decks, shuffled, and discarded after use.
- Standard military issue. Cheap to produce, quick to deploy, and destroyed by the casting.
- A combat card deck might contain 20-50 cards of various patterns. A mage who burns through their deck is reduced to mental traces.
- Replacing decks between engagements is standard military logistics.

### MAGIC_SYSTEM -- Tattoo Inscription
**Type:** Permanent instant-cast medium
**The Three Materials:**
- **Lunite** (Aethmark): A pale silver mineral found only in deep mines beneath Aethmark's northern mountains. When refined, it becomes the base ink that bonds with skin and holds pattern geometry. Without lunite, the pattern cannot adhere to living tissue. The Aethmarkian crown controls all lunite mining and export.
- **Siltberry Resin** (Ryuten): Harvested from the siltberry bush, a plant that grows only in Ryuten's misted river valleys. The resin acts as the binding agent — it fuses the ink pattern with the mage's mana channels beneath the skin. Without siltberry, the pattern sits on the skin's surface and flakes off within days. The Ryutense shogunate controls all siltberry harvesting and export.
- **Ashbloom Extract** (Solmir): Distilled from the ashbloom flower, which grows only in Solmir's volcanic desert oases. The extract is the activation catalyst — it makes the bonded pattern responsive to mana. Without ashbloom, the tattoo is visible but inert; the mage can see it but can't channel through it. The Solmiran empire controls all ashbloom cultivation and export.

**The Inscription Process:**
- Takes 2-3 days per tattoo. Painful. The recipient must remain conscious and channel their own mana through the pattern as it's being inscribed, bonding the ink with their mana channels. Yes, even non-mages can do this — everyone has mana, and the inscribed pattern provides the shape. The recipient just needs to will the mana through.
- Performed only at the Convergence Academy by master inscribers.
- The pattern is chosen from the Academy's pattern library, the recipient's own spellbook (if a mage), or a certified pattern. Non-mage recipients choose from a curated list of combat-relevant and utility patterns — healing, shielding, and simple attack patterns are most common.
- Maximum: 1 complex pattern (full back) or 2 simple patterns (one back, one stomach). This is a physical limitation — there simply isn't enough flat skin surface elsewhere for functional pattern geometry.
- **Back slot conflict:** If the recipient already has a crest on their back, they cannot receive a back tattoo. If they receive a back tattoo, they cannot manifest a crest in that area. The mana channels in the back support one major pattern. Choose wisely.
- Removal is agonizing, risks permanent mana channel scarring, and takes weeks of recovery. Not done casually.

**Political Control:**
- The Convergence Treaty mandates that each nation contribute a limited quantity of their respective material each year.
- The Academy allocates tattoo inscription slots through competition — candidates from all three continents compete for a limited number of inscriptions annually.
- Nations that threaten to withhold their material face unified pressure from the other two. The treaty framework makes unilateral withdrawal economic and political suicide.
- Black market tattoo materials exist but are wildly unreliable. Inscriptions done with impure or substituted materials have high failure rates — the tattoo may not work, may work unpredictably, or may kill the recipient during inscription.

### MAGIC_SYSTEM -- Tattoo Culture and the Non-Mage
**Type:** Social and tactical implications of tattoo magic
**The Non-Mage Tattoo Warrior:** This is Inkmark's most distinctive tactical reality. A warrior with a Gale tattoo (short flight burst) is an elite knight who can jousted at 90 degrees — diving lance strikes from above that no ground force can counter. A warrior with a Flow tattoo (binding) can immobilize a target for the two or three casts their mana reserves allow — long enough for allies to finish the job. A warrior with a Stone tattoo (personal barrier) becomes an unbreakable shield bearer for those crucial seconds. These are strategic assets deployed at critical moments, not everyday soldiers.
**The Mage Tattoo Combo:** A mage with a tattoo can do something non-mages cannot — combo their instant-cast tattoo with their book-cast patterns. A mage with a Flow tattoo (binding) traces a binding pattern on their back, activates it instantly to lock a target in place, then follows with an Ignition pattern from their spellbook. The tattoo provides setup speed that no mental trace can match. A mage with a Stone tattoo (personal barrier) can pop their instant shield and keep casting from their book behind it. This two-layer casting — tattoo for instant setup, book for sustained output — is devastating in a duel and makes tattoo-bearing mages the most feared combatants on Inkmark.
**The Allocation Debate:** Every year at the Convergence, the same argument happens: should inscription slots go to mages (who can combo tattoo + book) or to warriors (who have no other magical capability)? Pro-mage faction: a mage with a tattoo is more versatile and more dangerous. Pro-warrior faction: a warrior with a tattoo walks proof that the system works for everyone, not just mages. A warrior with a tattoo is a symbol. A mage with a tattoo is already powerful — the tattoo is redundant. The debate is loud, recurring, and never resolved. Approximately 60% of slots historically go to mages, 40% to warriors. Every allocation is contested.
**Tattoo Bearers as Public Figures:** A tattoo bearer is visible. Back tattoos show when the bearer removes their shirt (training, bathing, medical treatment). Stomach tattoos show in certain clothing. A warrior with a glowing pattern on their back is immediately identifiable as someone who earned a Convergence inscription slot. They're celebrities. People know their names. They're in the tournament brackets. They're in the propaganda. Losing a tattoo bearer in battle is a political event, not just a military one.
**Social Stigma and Respect:** Cultural attitudes toward tattoo bearers vary by continent:
- **Aethmark:** Tattoos are respected marks of achievement. A warrior with a tattoo is admired. A mage with a tattoo is envied. Both are seen as having earned something extraordinary.
- **Ryuten:** Tattoos are viewed with more ambivalence. The Ryutense tradition emphasizes personal mastery — a warrior who needs a tattoo to cast magic is seen as... not lesser, but dependent. The Temple Circle teaches that true power comes from within, not from an inscribed pattern. A Ryutense tattoo bearer is respected but quietly pitied.
- **Solmir:** Tattoos are viewed through a religious lens. The Radiant Creed interprets tattoo inscription as a divine confirmation — if you were chosen for a slot, the Divine willed it. A Solmiran tattoo bearer is practically venerated. This makes the allocation debate even more fraught, because withdrawing Solmir's ashbloom is seen as denying the Divine's will.
**The Tattoo as a Visible Class Marker:** Because tattoos are visible and limited, they create a class division that transcends mage/non-mage lines. People with tattoos have proven themselves at the Convergence. People without tattoos haven't. This distinction cuts across wealth, birth, and talent. A hedge mage with a tattoo has credibility that a university-trained mage without one lacks. A common-born warrior with a tattoo commands respect that a crestless noble warrior doesn't. The tattoo is, in many ways, Inkmark's most honest meritocracy — and its most exclusive one.

### MAGIC_SYSTEM -- Forbidden Patterns
**Type:** Prohibited spell patterns
**Status:** Universally condemned. Not because the categories are evil — because specific patterns violate the fundamental rule: you don't use magic to destroy someone's soul, will, or the natural cycle of life and death.
**Necromancy (Veil, advanced):** Patterns that animate corpses, bind spirits, or create undead. The dead should stay dead. Necromancy patterns are the most heavily guarded forbidden knowledge — copying them is a capital crime everywhere.
**Dominion (Spirit, advanced):** Patterns that override another person's will. Mind control, forced servitude, personality erasure. The dominated person is aware but helpless. Long-term dominion causes permanent psychological damage.
**Blood Manipulation (Flow, advanced):** Patterns that weaponize another's blood while they live. Distinct from healing — blood manipulation for healing is sacred. Blood manipulation for harm is execution-worthy. Intent is the line, and intent is hard to prove.
**Soul Severance (Veil/Spirit, advanced):** Patterns that damage or sever the connection between body and spirit. Permanent damage no healing pattern can undo. Practitioners develop a visible taint — other mages can sense something wrong about them.
**Punishment:** Execution, exile, or mana branding — a pattern burned into the mage's hands that permanently disrupts their ability to channel mana. Considered worse than death by most mages. Aethmark prefers imprisonment or execution. Ryuten prefers exile. Solmir prefers burning.
**The Gray Area:** Not all Veil mages are necromancers. Not all Spirit mages are dominators. Pattern prejudice is real. Veil mages hide their specialty in polite society. Storm mages are feared. The pattern is not the crime. The application is. But the world rarely makes that distinction.

## 4. CREST SYSTEM

### CREST_SYSTEM -- Bloodline Gifts
**Type:** Rare supernatural warrior enhancements
**Core Principle:** Crests are supernatural marks that manifest on the bearer's back, between the shoulder blades. They grant one specific power. Crests are not pattern magic — they operate independently, using the bearer's innate mana as fuel but not requiring tracing, books, or tattoos. The crest activates on will, responds to conviction, and cannot be disrupted by pattern-breaking techniques. One crest = one power. A Flame Crest-bearer's blade ignites. No resilience, no speed, just fire. Specific, limited, game-changing within its scope.

**The Crest-Tattoo Conflict:** Crests and back tattoos occupy the same surface area — the back, between the shoulder blades, where the body has the largest flat plane for complex pattern geometry. You cannot have both. A crest-bearer cannot receive a back tattoo because the crest pattern occupies the space. A person with a back tattoo cannot manifest a crest in the same area. The body has room for one major back pattern. This is not cultural — it's physical. The mana channels in the back can sustain one major pattern at a time. Crest or tattoo. Choose wisely. This choice defines lives.

**Crest-Bearers Are Not Just Warriors:** While crests are most common among warrior bloodlines (the power amplifies combat), crests can manifest in anyone. A Healer Crest could appear in someone with no combat training. A Truth Crest might manifest in a merchant's child. The power doesn't care about your profession. But because noble houses have tracked and bred for crests over centuries, warrior houses have the highest concentration. A crest-bearer who can't fight still has a supernatural advantage — they just have to figure out how to use it.

### CREST_SYSTEM -- Crest Origins
**Type:** Theories of where crests come from

**Origin:** Crests are ancient. No one knows exactly where they came from. The prevailing theories:
- **Divine Gift (Solmir):** The Radiant Creed teaches that crests were granted to worthy warriors by the Divine. Each crest is a blessing, and the bloodlines that carry them are sacred.
- **Ancestral Achievement (Ryuten):** Ryuten tradition holds that the first crest-bearers earned their marks through extraordinary deeds — their conviction was so absolute that it manifested physically. The bloodline inherited the mark of that achievement.
- **Ancient Technology (Aethmark):** Aethmark's scholars believe crests are remnants of a more advanced civilization — pre-Convergence patterns inscribed not on paper but on the human body itself, passed down through blood. The implication that tattoo magic might have once been more widespread is uncomfortable for those who control the current system.
- **What Is Known:** Crests are real. They manifest on the back, between the shoulder blades, as visible glowing marks when activated. They are inherited through bloodlines — a crest-bearer's children have a chance of inheriting the crest, though it sometimes skips generations or manifests differently. Crests can also, very rarely, be discovered by individuals with no known bloodline connection — these "spontaneous" crest-bearers are the subject of intense interest and suspicion. A crest and a back tattoo cannot coexist — the mana channels in the back support one major pattern.

### CREST_SYSTEM -- Crest Characteristics & Known Crests
**Type:** How crests behave, and a catalog of known crests

**Crest Characteristics:**
- One crest = one power. A crest that grants flame strikes does not also grant resilience. The power is specific.
- **Crests are bloodline-locked.** A crest is tied to its bloodline. House Pendragon's Flame Crest produces Flame. Every Pendragon who manifests gets Flame. House Yoshitsune's Wind Crest produces Wind. Every Yoshitsune who manifests gets Wind. There is no variety within a pure bloodline — you inherit the family crest or you don't.
- **Crest marriages create new crests.** When two different crest bloodlines produce a child, that child may inherit either parent's crest OR manifest a new crest that blends the parents' powers. A Flame Crest parent and a Bastion Crest parent might produce a child with Flame, Bastion, or something entirely new — a Scorching Wall crest that ignites whatever the bearer blocks. This is the incentive. Crest marriages are a gamble with enormous upside. New crests are rare, powerful, and politically explosive — they create new bloodlines and new houses.
- **New crests are not guaranteed.** Most children of mixed crest bloodlines inherit one parent's crest. A new crest manifestation is uncommon but not unheard of. When it happens, it's a political event. Houses have gone to war over custody of a child with a new crest type.
- Crests manifest on the back and occupy the same surface area as a back tattoo. You cannot have both. This is a hard physical limitation.
- Crests use the bearer's innate mana as fuel, but not through pattern tracing. The crest channels mana automatically when willed. Everyone has mana, so crests work for anyone who bears one. A crest-bearer's mana reserves for their crest are limited — they tire like anyone else — but the power itself doesn't require concentration or skill to activate.
- Crests activate on the bearer's will and respond to emotional conviction — a crest-bearer who truly believes in their cause fights harder and more effectively. This isn't metaphor. A crest-bearer defending something they deeply care about is measurably more effective.
- Crest marks glow faintly when the power is active. Visible when activated, otherwise dormant and only visible upon close inspection of the back.
- Crests do not grant combat skill. A crest-bearer who has never held a sword still doesn't know how to swordfight. The power enhances — it doesn't replace training.
- Crests sometimes skip generations. A child of a crest-bearer might not manifest. A grandchild might. The inheritance is not guaranteed — it's probable. This uncertainty drives succession anxiety and genealogical obsession.

**Known Crest Examples (representative, not exhaustive):**
- **Flame Crest:** Weapons or strikes ignite with fire. The crest-bearer's blade burns. Simple, devastating, common in Aethmark's knightly houses.
- **Frost Crest:** Strikes freeze on contact. Armor becomes brittle. Ground turns to ice. Found primarily in Ryuten's northern provinces.
- **Bastion Crest:** Enhanced resilience. The bearer can survive blows that would kill a normal warrior. Not invincible — sustained damage still adds up. Valued in frontline soldiers across all continents.
- **Wind Crest:** Enhanced speed and agility. The bearer moves faster than should be possible. Short bursts of incredible speed, or sustained faster-than-normal movement. Favored by scouts and light cavalry.
- **Truth Crest:** The bearer can sense deception. Not mind-reading — an instinctive feeling when someone is lying. Valued in diplomacy, dangerous in politics. Several Solmiran inquisitors bear this crest.
- **Beast Crest:** Empathy with a specific type of creature. The bearer can understand and communicate with pegasi, wyverns, or other mounts at an intuitive level. The mount responds as if the bearer's will is its own. Essential for elite pegasus and wyvern riders.
- **Shadow Crest:** The bearer can step into shadow and emerge from another shadow nearby. Limited range, requires shadows, can't carry more than what they're wearing. Favored by assassins and spies. Rare and deeply distrusted.

### CREST_SYSTEM -- Crest Politics & New Crests
**Type:** Crest politics, new-crest manifestations, and the registry

**Crest Politics:**
- Noble houses are defined by their crests. House Pendragon IS the Flame Crest house. House Yoshitsune IS the Wind Crest house. The crest and the bloodline are inseparable — this is why houses fight so viciously over succession. A house without a crest-bearer is a house without its defining advantage.
- Crest inheritance is tracked obsessively. House genealogies are maintained with the same rigor as treasury accounts. A confirmed crest-bearer in the family line increases a house's standing dramatically. A generation skipped (no children manifested the crest) causes panic.
- Crest marriages are the most significant political tool on Inkmark — and the most dangerous. Two crest bloodlines producing a child creates three possible outcomes: the child inherits the mother's crest, the father's crest, or a new blended crest. The first two are common. The third is rare, powerful, and world-changing. A new crest means a new bloodline. A new bloodline means a new house. Houses have gone to war over custody of a child manifesting a new crest type. The gamble drives generations of political maneuvering — and it's the only way to create a new crest bloodline.
- **The New Crest Problem:** When a new crest manifests, the question of ownership is explosive. The heir carries a crest that belongs to neither parent's house. So who does it belong to? The mother's house claims it — she bore the child. The father's house claims it — the child carries their blood. The heir themselves might decide to found a new house, taking the new crest bloodline with them. A strong new crest — something like a Scorching Wall that combines Flame and Bastion — could make the bearer the foundation of a house more powerful than either parent's. This is the danger. The incentive is enormous. The fallout is catastrophic. Every new crest manifestation between two great houses is a succession crisis waiting to happen. Treaties, marriage contracts, and succession laws across all three continents have entire sections dedicated to new crest custody. The Convergence Registry keeps the authoritative records, but even they can't decide who a new crest belongs to — they just record who has it.
- **Practical outcome:** Most new-crest bearers either stay with one parent's house (creating internal tension about which bloodline the house now serves) or found a cadet branch (creating a new house with the new crest as its foundation). Both outcomes cause bloodshed. The bolder the new crest, the more bloodshed.
- Spontaneous crest-bearers — those with no known bloodline — are the system's greatest threat and greatest prize. A spontaneous Flame Crest-bearer could be claimed by any house to "confirm" a distant bloodline connection. Or they could be proof that crests aren't as bloodline-exclusive as the nobility claims. Houses react to spontaneous bearers with a mix of greed and terror.
- The Convergence Academy maintains the International Crest Registry, the only authoritative record of all known crest bloodlines across all three continents. Houses dispute entries constantly. The registry is political dynamite dressed up as scholarship.

### CREST_SYSTEM -- Historical Crest Events
**Type:** Three examples of new crest manifestations — one success, two catastrophes

**The Atlas-Zephyr Success (GOOD):** House Atlas (Bastion Crest) married into House Zephyr (Wind Crest) through a deliberate alliance 140 years ago. Their third-born daughter, Linnea, manifested a new crest — the Gale Bastion, a shield that deflects incoming attacks with bursts of pressurized air. Because Linnea was third-born, both parent houses were satisfied with their primary heirs inheriting the original crests. Linnea founded House Atlas-Zephyr as a cadet branch, taking marginal border territory in northern Aethmark that no one else wanted. The house still exists today, moderate in power, and Linnea's descendants still bear the Gale Bastion. It worked because the stakes were low and no one had anything to lose. The Convergence Registry records Gale Bastion as "Active — Cadet Bloodline, Established."

**The Fenrir-Cassandra Catastrophe (BAD):** House Fenrir (Frost Crest) and House Cassandra (Truth Crest) arranged a marriage 80 years ago specifically hoping for a new crest. Their son Erik manifested the Frosteye — a crest that froze deception on a person's tongue, making it impossible for them to lie while the crest was active. Both houses claimed Erik. House Fenrir marched on House Cassandra's keep. Cassandra hired mercenaries. Erik, 17 years old and the center of a war he didn't start, was killed in the crossfire during a siege that neither house won decisively. The Frosteye died with him. Both houses were weakened by the conflict. A third house — House Niflung, who had been watching from the north — moved into the territory both houses had bled over. The Convergence Registry records Frosteye as "Extinct — New Bloodline, Failed."

**The Dido-Hector Tragedy (BAD):** House Dido (Flame Crest) and House Hector (Bastion Crest) produced a daughter, Marienne, who manifested the Ashen Bastion — a shield that ignited anything it blocked against, turning defensive power into offensive heat. Both houses demanded custody. A three-year legal battle in the Convergence courts followed. Marienne grew up a pawn in a cage of lawyers, shuttled between houses, used as leverage in trade negotiations, and never given a moment of peace. At 19, she fled both houses, joined a mercenary company, and swore an oath never to bear children. The Ashen Bastion will die with her. Two great houses destroyed their alliance, both lost their most strategically valuable asset, and the Ashen Bastion — potentially one of the strongest defensive-offensive crests in Aethmark's history — will be recorded in the Registry as "Extinct — New Bloodline, Deliberately Ended." House Dido and House Hector still don't speak. Their territories border each other. The tension is permanent.

### CREST_SYSTEM -- Crest-Bearing Warriors in Combat
**Type:** Military role
**Crest-bearers are strategic assets.** A single Flame Crest-bearer in a cavalry charge changes the engagement. A Bastion Crest-bearer holding a wall section allows fewer soldiers to hold more ground. A Wind Crest-bearer scouting ahead provides intelligence no mundane scout can match.
**But they are not invincible.** A crest-bearer can be overwhelmed by numbers, outmaneuvered, exhausted, or killed by a lucky shot. The Fire Emblem principle applies: a powerful unit can sweep a battalion if fresh and careful, but chip damage and human wave tactics work. Crest or no crest, a sword through the throat kills.
**Anti-Crest Tactics:**
- Numerical superiority. Five warriors can bring down one crest-bearer through coordination.
- Exhaustion. Crests don't tire, but the bodies that bear them do.
- Ranged attacks. Arrows and thrown weapons don't care about melee enhancement.
- Mage support. A mage tracing a well-placed barrier or distraction pattern can neutralize a crest-bearer's advantage.
- Anti-crease ambush. Specific formations designed to isolate and overwhelm crest-bearers. Standard military doctrine in all three continents.

### HOUSES -- The Noble System
**Type:** The noble-house system across all three continents
**Core Principle:** Inkmark's nobility is organized into Houses, each descended — in claim or in truth — from a legendary founding ancestor. A House's identity IS its crest and its founding legend; the name alone tells you what the House is known for. Houses substitute for ordinary nobility: they hold land, command armies, fight succession wars, and obsess over crest lineage (see Crest System). Most claims of descent are exaggerated. Some are forged. A few are real. The Convergence Crest Registry is the only authority that can confirm a bloodline — and even it argues. Houses sit beneath the great governments: House Pendragon holds the Aethmark crown, the Ashikaga shogunate rules Ryuten's houses as daimyo-vassals, and Solmir's Radiant Empire sits above its houses as divine authority.
**Houses Are Not Everyone:** The great houses are the apex of nobility — a thin top layer, not the whole map. Most of Inkmark — including many of its deadliest individuals — are common-born, small-clan, or civilian, carrying ordinary family names tied to no founding legend: a smith's line, a frontier village clan, a single notable parent. A surname like Kurogane or Vance is just a family name, not a House. Talent does not check lineage: crests manifest spontaneously in people with no bloodline, the tattoo is a name-blind meritocracy, and the Convergence ranks candidates on performance alone. A commoner named for nothing but their grandfather's trade can hold rank #1 — and nothing rattles a great house more than a no-name prodigy who proves the bloodline myth optional. Small clans and common families produce heroes constantly; the houses simply own the genealogists and the propaganda.

## 5. CREATURES & MOUNTS

### CREATURES -- Pegasi
**Type:** Intelligent flying mounts
**Appearance:** Pegasi resemble large, powerful horses with feathered wings. Coat colors range from white and grey to dappled brown and rare midnight black. Wingspan 4-5 meters. Height at shoulder 1.6-1.8 meters. Eyes are large and notably intelligent.
**Temperament:** Pegasi are intelligent, prideful, and selective. They bond with one rider for life — choosing a rider is the pegasus's decision, not the rider's. A pegasus who hasn't chosen you will simply refuse to fly with you. Once bonded, the bond is absolute; the pegasus will fight for its rider, grieve their death, and refuse to accept another. Pegasi communicate through body language, ear position, vocalizations (whinnies, snorts, screams), and an empathic bond with their rider that deepens over time.
**Habitat:** Native to high mountain aeries across all three continents. Wild pegasi nest on cliff faces and mountain plateaus inaccessible to ground predators. Domesticated pegasi are bred in specialized programs maintained by military academies and noble houses.
**Combat Role:** Pegasus knights are elite aerial units. A pegasus-rider pair can scout, skirmish, flank ground formations, and deliver troops behind enemy lines. Pegasi are vulnerable to massed ranged fire — arrows and thrown patterns force them to stay mobile. In a pegasus-vs-wyvern engagement, pegasi rely on speed and agility; wyverns rely on brute strength and natural weapons.
**Cultural Status:** Vary by continent:
- **Aethmark:** Pegasi are military assets. Noble houses maintain pegasus stables. Pegasus knights are prestigious — the aerial arm of any lord's forces.
- **Ryuten:** Pegasi are respected partners. Ryutense riders emphasize the bond between rider and mount. The pegasus is not a tool; it is a companion. Choosing rituals involve meditation and mutual testing rather than Aethmark's training regimens.
- **Solmir:** Pegasi are considered semi-divine. The Radiant Creed teaches that pegasi were gifts from the Divine for the faithful to ride against darkness. Pegasus riders are always crest-bearers (Beast Crest preferred) or warriors of proven faith.

### CREATURES -- Wyverns
**Type:** Aggressive flying predators
**Appearance:** Wyverns are reptilian flying creatures — scaled, lean, and predatory. Two powerful wings, two clawed legs, a barbed tail. Size varies; the largest are 4 meters long with 6-meter wingspans. Coloration ranges from forest green and sandstone brown to volcanic red and rare ice-blue. Eyes are slitted and predatory. Their bite is venomous — painful but rarely lethal to a healthy adult; debilitating in combat.
**Temperament:** Wyverns are intelligent in a predatory sense — cunning, territorial, and aggressive. They do not bond the way pegasi do. A wyvern rider earns their mount's respect through dominance and skill, not affection. A rider who shows weakness is at risk of being challenged by their own mount. Successful wyvern riders are forceful, confident, and never show fear. The relationship is closer to a partnership between predators than a bond of love.
**Habitat:** Volcanic regions, arid highlands, and mountain territories with large game. Wyverns nest in cliff-side caves and mountain vents. They are rarer than pegasi — fewer habitable territories, lower birth rates, higher mortality. This scarcity makes them strategically valuable and culturally feared.
**Combat Role:** Wyvern riders are heavy aerial assault units. A wyvern can fight independently — biting, striking with tail, and using its venom — while the rider directs and strikes from above. Wyverns are slower and less agile than pegasi in tight maneuvers, but they are stronger, more durable, and outright dangerous without a rider. A wyvern attack on ground formations is devastating. In aerial combat, wyverns dominates in a straight fight; pegasi wins by outmaneuvering.
**Cultural Status:**
- **Aethmark:** Wyvern riders are rare and feared. Aethmark's military doctrine focuses on pegasus knights; wyvern units are small, specialized, and considered unpredictable. Several Aethmark houses refuse to stable wyverns, considering them unreliable.
- **Ryuten:** Wyverns are respected as symbols of strength and ferocity. Several Ryutense mountain provinces consider the wyvern a totem animal. Ryutense wyvern riders emphasize the predator partnership — the rider is the wyvern's alpha, and both hunt together.
- **Solmir:** Wyverns are tolerated but distrusted. The Radiant Creed doesn't condemn them, but they're not the "divine gift" that pegasi are. Solmiran wyvern riders tend to be provincial — desert and mountain dwellers who practicality overcame prejudice.

### CREATURES -- Other Mounts and Beasts
**Horses:** Standard cavalry. Still dominate ground warfare across all continents. Warhorses are bred for size, endurance, and aggression. No supernatural qualities — just excellent breeding and training.
**Dire Wolves:** Large, intelligent wolves bred in Ryuten's frontier provinces for patrol and skirmish roles. Bond with riders similarly to pegasi but to a lesser degree. Faster than horses in rough terrain. Cannot match pegasus or wyvern mobility but excel in forest and mountain operations.
**Great Cats:** Bred in Ryuten's southern provinces. Lion-sized felines with exceptional speed and reflexes. Used by elite skirmishers and scouts. Independent-minded; riders need Beast Crests or exceptional skill to maintain control in combat.
**Mountain Rams:** Aethmark's highland forces use specially bred rams for mountain and rough terrain transport. Not combat mounts — pack animals and transport for terrain where horses struggle. Stubborn, reliable, and capable of navigating slopes that would break a horse's legs.
**Desert Striders:** Solmir's elite cavalry ride long-legged desert horses bred for endurance in extreme heat. Faster over long distances than northern breeds, less armored, but capable of operating in conditions that would kill a normal horse in hours. Solmiran cavalry doctrine emphasizes speed and range over heavy armor.
**Sea Serpents:** Rumored in deep waters between the continents. Not domesticated. Occasionally attack shipping. The Convergence maintains ward-inscribed harbor defenses against them. Most sailors consider them bad luck to discuss. Whether they're real or just storm-mirage stories is a matter of faith versus experience — the ones who've seen them don't talk about it, and the ones who haven't don't believe it.

### CREATURES -- Wild and Dangerous
**Stonewyrms:** Armored burrowing predators found in Aethmark's mountain ranges. 2-3 meters long, stone-like hide, tunnel through rock. Dangerous to miners and mountain travelers. Stonewyrms are hunted for their hide (superior leather) and glands (alchemy reagent).
**Ashstalkers:** Volcanide predators native to Solmir's interior. Lizard-like, heat-resistant, pack hunters. Aggressive and territorial. Solmiran desert routes are planned around known ashstalker territories.
**Mist Weavers:** Large arachnid creatures in Ryuten's misted forests. Build nearly invisible web structures between trees. Venom causes paralysis. Ryutense rangers are trained to spot and avoid them. Their silk is valuable — light, strong, and mana-conductive.
**Thornbacks:** Massive herbivorous beasts in Ryuten's river deltas. 4-5 meters long, armored with bony plates, generally docile unless provoked. Domesticated as heavy labor animals. Thornback cavalry is not fast but almost impossible to stop — living siege engines.
**Hollows:** The worst. Undead remnants from the Convergence War (see Historical Events). Not natural creatures — necromantic residue that has persisted for centuries. Found in the dead zones where the worst necromancy was performed. Shambling, aggressive, and nearly immune to normal weaponry. Only pattern magic or crest-enhanced weapons reliably put them down. Every generation, some fool tries to clear a dead zone. Most don't come back.

## 6. THE CONVERGENCE

### CONVERGENCE -- The Academy
**Type:** Treaty-bound neutral academy and inscription center
**Leader:** The Triumvirate — one representative from each continent, appointed by their respective governments. Currently: Archscholar Miriel of Aethmark (academic head), Shogun-Representative Yoshitsune of Ryuten (administrative head), Oracle-Representative Amira of Solmir (diplomatic head).
**Location:** Convergence Island — a large island at the geographic center where sea routes from all three continents meet. Fortified, warded, politically untouchable. The treaty that established it is the only thing all three nations agree on.
**Political Neutrality:** The Convergence's neutrality is enforced by self-interest. Attacking it means losing pattern library access, losing tattoo inscription, losing the crest registry, and uniting two other nations against you. Every king, every shogun, every emperor has family or alumni inside those walls. Withdraw your materials and no one gets tattoos — but you also lose your own candidates' access. The mutual dependency is the peace.

### CONVERGENCE -- The Three Materials
**Type:** The three tattoo materials and annual inscription capacity

**The Three Materials:**
- **Lunite (Aethmark):** Pale silver mineral from deep mines in Aethmark's northern mountains. Base ink component — bonds pattern to skin. Controlled by the Aethmarkian crown. Annual shipment to the Convergence: 12 ounces (enough for approximately 24-30 tattoo inscriptions, depending on pattern complexity).
- **Siltberry Resin (Ryuten):** Harvested from siltberry bushes in Ryuten's misted river valleys. Binding agent — fuses ink with mana channels. Controlled by the Ryutense shogunate. Annual shipment: 8 vials (each vial sufficient for 3-4 inscriptions).
- **Ashbloom Extract (Solmir):** Distilled from ashbloom flowers in Solmir's volcanic desert oases. Activation catalyst — makes the bonded pattern responsive to mana. Controlled by the Solmiran empire. Annual shipment: 6 phials (each phial sufficient for 4-5 inscriptions).
- **Total annual inscription capacity:** ~24-30 tattoos, limited by the smallest supply. This is intentional. All three nations agreed to limit production. More inscriptions mean more tattoo mages, and more powerful mages with instant-cast capability destabilizes the balance.

### CONVERGENCE -- The Competition
**Type:** How tattoo inscription slots are won

**The Competition:** Each year, candidates from all three continents compete for tattoo inscription slots. The competition takes multiple forms:
- **Combat Tournament:** Warriors and spellblades compete in ranked duels. Winners earn inscription consideration.
- **Pattern Exhibition:** Mages demonstrate mastery of complex patterns. Speed, precision, creativity, and combat application are judged.
- **Strategic Trials:** Teams from each continent solve tactical problems — holding a position, extracting from a dangerous situation, achieving an objective against opposition. Tests leadership, cooperation, and practical skill.
- **Crest Demonstration:** Crest-bearers demonstrate mastery and creative application of their crest. New applications of known crests are particularly valued.
- Slots are allocated proportionally: 10 per continent per year, with additional slots awarded to the top overall performers regardless of origin. The allocation is a year-long political negotiation that every nation tries to game.

### CONVERGENCE -- Student Life & Houses
**Type:** Student experience, year structure, and the three Convergence houses

**The Student Experience:**
- Students enter the Convergence at age 15-16 and stay for 4 years, graduating as young adults (19-21). A 5th year of specialization is optional for those pursuing advanced pattern mastery, crest synchronization, or mount bonding.
- **Year Structure:**
  - **1st Year:** Foundations. Pattern theory for mages, combat drill for warriors, mount training for riders, crest exploration for crest-bearers. Everyone takes basic survival and fitness. The dropout rate is highest in the 1st year (~25%) — the academic pressure, physical demands, and cultural shock of living with students from three hostile continents breaks many.
  - **2nd Year:** Specialization begins. Mages choose pattern categories to focus on. Warriors choose combat disciplines. Crest-bearers begin exploring their specific power. Cross-training is encouraged but not required.
  - **3rd Year:** Practical application. Field exercises, tactical problems, ranked duels, mounted combat. Students begin competing for tattoo inscription candidacy slots. The pressure intensifies. Inter-house rivalry peaks.
  - **4th Year:** Examinations and the Tattoo Ceremony. Final exams, tournament rankings, and the allocation of inscription slots. The year ends with the formal ceremony where candidates receive their tattoos. This is the climax — families attend, dignitaries watch, and the recipients are formally recognized. People cry. People cheer. It's the biggest event of the Convergence calendar.
- **Attrition:** ~30% total dropout rate across all 4 years. Not from death (usually) but from academic failure, injury, inability to keep up, or — most commonly — the crushing pressure of living with students from rival continents while competing for limited slots. The Convergence is hard on purpose. The countries that send students want graduates who can handle pressure, not just talent.
- **Daily Life:** Morning classes (pattern theory, combat drill, crest study, riding), afternoon practical sessions (sparring, tracing practice, tactical exercises), evening study or free time. House rivalries play out in ranked exercises, dining hall arguments, and carefully timed pranks that the faculty pretends not to notice.
- **Dropout Consequence:** Leaving the Convergence isn't catastrophic like the old stories say — you don't lose your magic or your crest — but it's deeply embarrassing. A dropout returns home without the connections, the pattern library access, or the prestige of graduation. Many dropouts become hedge mages, sell-swords, or return to their home nations to face their families' disappointment.
- Students live in houses based on temperament and aptitude, not continent of origin. Three houses:
  - **House Talon** — Martial excellence, courage, strike first. Colors: iron grey and crimson. Best duelists, worst diplomats.
  - **House Scroll** — Knowledge, theory, mastery. Colors: midnight blue and silver. Brilliant theorists, chronic overthinkers.
  - **House Shield** — Duty, protection, service. Colors: forest green and gold. Most reliable, most stubborn, most likely to die protecting someone they just met.
- Inter-house rivalry is constant. Monthly rankings based on combat performance, pattern mastery, and tactical exercises. The annual Convergence Tournament determines house ranking for the year.

### CONVERGENCE -- Library, Training, Registry & Treaty
**Type:** Academy facilities, the crest registry, and the founding treaty

**The Pattern Library:** The most complete collection of spell patterns in the world. Seven floors of preserved patterns, from basic ignition sparks to advanced military combat patterns to restricted forbidden pattern copies (sealed, guarded, available only to authorized researchers under supervision). Students and scholars come to the Convergence for pattern access as much as for the combat training and tattoo inscription.
**Combat Training:** The Convergence trains warriors alongside mages. Sparring pits, riding grounds (pegasi and horses; wyverns are housed separately), pattern-tracing ranges, crest training facilities. The academy produces some of the finest warriors on Inkmark — not because of magic, but because of the quality of instruction and the intensity of competition.
**Crest Registry:** The authoritative record of all known crest bloodlines across Inkmark. Houses dispute entries constantly. A confirmed registry entry can legitimize a noble house; a removal can destroy one. The registry is maintained by a team of scholars who travel to verify claims, and the politics around it are intense.
**The Treaty:** The Convergence Treaty is the foundation. All three nations signed it after the Convergence War (see Historical Events). Key provisions:
- The island is neutral territory. No nation claims sovereignty.
- All three materials are shipped annually.
- Tattoo inscription is limited to treaty-mandated quantities.
- No military vessels within sight of the island. No armed forces beyond the Convergence's own guard.
- Attacking the Convergence means the other two nations unite against you. This threat has kept the peace for nearly 800 years.
- Every nation sends students and candidates. The intake process is competitive and overseen by the Triumvirate. Corruption has been attempted. Exposed attempts have nearly started wars.
**Key Institutions:**
- **The inscription chamber:** Where tattoo magic is performed. Underground, warded, temperature-controlled. The most secure room on the island.
- **The Grand Library:** Seven floors of pattern archives. The greatest collection of spell knowledge in existence.
- **The sparring pits:** Open-air arenas for warrior and mage training combined. Ranked duels, pattern-tracing exercises, mounted combat practice.
- **The rookery:** Pegasus and wyvern stables. Riding grounds. Aerial combat training.
- **The harbor:** Three major docks — one facing each continent. Ships arrive daily. Continental politics are visible here and only here on the island.
**Surprise Types:** INTER_HOUSE_RIVALRY, TOURNAMENT_UPSET, PATTERN_THEFT, INSCRIPTION_CEREMONY_GONE_WRONG, FORBIDDEN_PATTERN_DISCOVERY, CONTINENTAL_TENSION_IN_DORMS, CREST_REGISTRY_DISPUTE, TATTOO_CANDIDATE_SCANDAL, DIPLOMATIC_INCIDENT, MAGE_WARRIOR_SPARRING_ESCALATION

## 7. CONTINENTS & FACTIONS

### OVERVIEW -- Continent of Aethmark
**Type:** Continental region
**Geography:** Temperate to cold. Rolling plains, dense forests, mountain ranges, river valleys, rocky coastlines. Harsh winters in the north, mild in the south. Rich in iron, timber, and arable land. The northern mountains hold the lunite mines — the single most politically important resource on the continent.
**Culture Base:** European medieval — Germanic, Frankish, English influences. Stone castles, walled cities, feudal hierarchy, knightly orders. Cathedral universities teach pattern magic. Warrior houses define themselves by crests. Mage-Knight rivalry is a cultural pastime here — every tavern has an opinion.
**Major Powers:** Kingdom of Pendragon, The Hexen Council, The Pale Vow, The Free Cities of Velkath, The Convergence (sovereign island)
**Population:** ~15 million. Moderate density.
**Climate Threat:** Winter campaigns are brutal. Pattern tracing slows in extreme cold — mages' ink freezes, fingers stiffen, book pages become brittle. Cavalry is hampered by snow and mud. The worst battles in Aethmark's history were fought in winter.
**Cultural Flavor:** Feudal lords with crest-bearing lineages. Cathedral universities where mages learn pattern tracing under stained glass windows. Knightly orders that consider crest-less warriors equally noble if they've earned their spurs. Spellbook scribes in every major city. Pegasus knights as the flower of the aristocracy. A culture that respects both the sword and the pattern — but enjoys arguing about which one actually wins.
**Unique Feature:** The Lunite Mines. Deep beneath Aethmark's northern mountains, the pale silver mineral that forms the base of tattoo ink is extracted under tight crown control. The mines are guarded, dangerous, and essential. Control of lunite means control of one-third of the world's tattoo inscription supply. The Kingdom of Pendragon holds this leverage and wields it constantly.

### FACTION -- Kingdom of Pendragon
**Type:** Feudal monarchy
**Leader:** King Edmund Pendragon (aging, sharp, three heirs who hate each other)
**Location:** Central and northern Aethmark
**Structure:** Autocratic monarchy with a Royal Council of seven advisors — a mix of mages, warriors, and crest-bearers. Noble houses hold regional governance. Each house maintains its own forces, its own pattern library, and its own crest lineage. The King holds absolute authority in theory; in practice, the nobles' crest-houses limit royal power.
**Population:** ~5 million within borders
**Military:** The Pendragon Combined Guard — 8,000 professional soldiers (mix of warriors and combat mages), 2,000 pegasus knights, 500 wyvern riders (specialized mountain unit), and assorted feudal levies. Organized into mixed formations: warrior companies hold the line, mage units provide ranged pattern support, and pegasus knights scout, flank, and skirmish.
**Lunite Monopoly:** Pendragon controls all lunite mining and export. This gives them enormous leverage in Convergence politics. Threatening to restrict lunite shipments is Pendragon's favorite diplomatic tool — and everyone's least favorite negotiation tactic.
**Economy:** Iron mining, enchanted weapon forging, agriculture, spellbook printing. Pendragon's armories produce the finest inscribed weapons on the continent. Their spellbook presses turn out standard pattern books by the thousands.
**Relations:** Cold war with the Free Cities over trade routes. Alliance-by-convenience with the Pale Vow against Veranthos-border threats. Tension with the Hexen Council over university autonomy.
**Internal Tension:** The King is old. Three heirs — the eldest is competent but cruel, the middle is popular but naive, the youngest is a crest-bearer (Bastion Crest) with no political skill. The houses are positioning. Someone is going to make a move.

### FACTION -- The Hexen Council
**Type:** Mage university coalition
**Leader:** Grand Scholar Seren Calloway (elected by the Council of Six)
**Location:** Headquartered in Greywall; operates universities across Aethmark
**Structure:** Coalition of six major universities and dozens of smaller institutions. The Council of Six — one archscholar from each major university — governs collectively. The Grand Scholar is first among equals. The Concord is not a nation but wields power like one.
**University System:** Each university specializes:
- Embergarde Academy (military pattern casting and siege applications)
- Sylven College (healing patterns and medical magic)
- Ashmark Institute (inscription and enchantment craft)
- Greywall Academy (combat casting and defensive patterns)
- Greenfield College (agricultural and infrastructure applications)
- Deeplore Academy (divination and information gathering — the most secretive)
**Political Power:** The Concord trains Aethmark's mages and guards the second-largest pattern library on the continent (after the Convergence). Every noble court has Concord-trained advisors.
**The Warrior Question:** The Concord is mage-focused. They don't train warriors. The Pale Vow argues this produces mages who don't know how to fight alongside warriors. The Concord argues warriors have their own institutions. The Convergence, which trains both, watches this argument like someone eating popcorn at a bar fight.
**Pattern Secrecy:** The Concord's pattern libraries are their real power. Military patterns, advanced healing patterns, and cutting-edge research are all housed behind locked doors. Access requires enrollment, tuition, or very persuasive diplomacy. Stealing from a Concord library is a good way to disappear.

### FACTION -- The Pale Vow
**Type:** Chivalric military order (warrior-dominant, mage-integrated)
**Leader:** Commander Ryga the Unburnt (crest-bearer, Bastion Crest, 30-year veteran)
**Location:** Fortress-monastery at Thornwatch, northern Aethmark
**Structure:** A knightly order bound by oath to defend Aethmark's northern borders. Roughly 80% warrior, 20% mage. Recruitment by trial. Survivors join. Many do not survive.
**Philosophy:** Strength is earned, not given. Crest-bearers and crest-less warriors fight side by side. The Creed doesn't care if you have a crest — it cares if you can hold the line. Their mages specialize in combat casting alongside warriors, not behind them.
**Role:** Holding the Northern March against raids, wild creatures, and whatever comes out of the mountains. They are the wall between civilization and everything north of it.
**Key Members:** Commander Ryga the Unburnt (Bastion Crest, unkillable by reputation), Knight-Captain Iris Atalanta (Wind Crest, fastest rider in the Creed), War-Mage Caelen (no last name, specializes in rapid combat card deployment, has burned through more spellbook pages than anyone alive)

### FACTION -- The Free Cities of Velkath
**Type:** Mercantile confederation
**Leader:** The Coin Council (five elected Merchant-Princes)
**Location:** Coastal Aethmark, southeastern region
**Structure:** Five independent city-states bound by the Velkathish Trade Compact. Each city is governed by a Merchant-Prince elected by the merchant guilds. No central army — each city maintains its own guard and hires mercenary companies.
**Economy:** The wealthiest faction per capita in Aethmark. Velkathish ports connect Aethmark to Ryuten and Solmir. Everything flows through Velkathish harbors. They don't fight wars of conquest — they fund them.
**Spellbook Trade:** The Velkathish spellbook presses are the most prolific on the continent. Standard pattern books, combat card decks, specialized pattern collections — if it can be printed, Velkath prints it and sells it. They also run the largest black market for restricted and forbidden patterns, though no Coin Council member will admit this under oath.

### FACTION -- Minor Factions (Aethmark)
**The Northern March Lords:** Minor nobility holding the borderlands beyond the Pale Vow's protection. Tough, independent, frequently raided. Their crest-lines are some of the oldest and most battle-tested in Aethmark. They resent Pendragon for not sending more troops north.
**The Hedge Mages:** Unaffiliated mages who never attended a Concord university. Self-taught, trained by village elders, or educated by traveling tutors. Their pattern knowledge is limited but practical — healing, weather reading, crop patterns, basic combat. The Concord considers them dangerous amateurs. The hedge mages consider the Hexen Council expensive elitists.
**The Scribes' Guild:** Professional pattern copyists headquartered in Velkath but operating in every major city. They copy, bind, and repair spellbooks for a fee. The guild sets standards for ink quality, page durability, and pattern accuracy. A guild-certified scribe's work is guaranteed. A non-guild scribe's work is a gamble.

### LOCATION -- Aethmark Notable Locations
**The Pale Forest:** An ancient forest in central Aethmark where the trees grow in natural circle patterns. The bark of certain old trees forms geometric shapes that resemble pattern geometry — outer rings, core points, even conduit lines. Whether this is coincidence, ambient mana influence, or something older is a matter of debate. The Hexen Council sends researchers annually. The Pale Vow uses the forest for trials. Hedge mages pilgrimage here hoping the natural patterns will inspire their tracing. The forest is not dangerous in the conventional sense — no beasts, no bandits — but mages who trace patterns too close to the ancient trees sometimes report the patterns shifting beneath their fingers, as if the tree is correcting their geometry.
**Millford:** A village at the intersection of three trade routes in southern Aethmark. Known for two things: the largest spellbook printing press outside Velkath, and the annual Hedge Mage Gathering — an unofficial market where unaffiliated mages trade pattern knowledge, sell homemade spellbook copies, and argue about the Hexen Council's pattern monopoly. The Council tolerates it because it's useful to know what patterns are circulating in the wild. Velkath merchants attend because hedge mages buy cheap books and sell rare patterns. Millford is also where you go if you need a spellbook repaired by someone who doesn't ask questions about where you got it.
**The Lunite Depths:** The lunite mines beneath Aethmark's northern mountains. Not a single mine but a network of interconnected shafts, tunnels, and caverns that extend for miles underground. The walls glitter with pale silver lunite deposits. The air is thick with mana residue — mages report their pattern tracing improves down here, and non-mages report a constant tingling. The Depths are dangerous: tunnel collapses, lunite dust that causes mana overload in prolonged exposure, and something that lives in the deepest shafts that the miners won't talk about. The Pale Vow guards the main entrances. Smuggling lunite out is a capital offense. It happens anyway.

### OVERVIEW -- Continent of Ryuten
**Type:** Continental region
**Geography:** Mist-covered mountains, bamboo forests, river deltas, volcanic islands, temperate coastal plains. The landscape is beautiful and dangerous — mist that obscures vision, rivers that flood without warning, volcanic vents that the locals treat as sacred and everyone else treats as terrifying.
**Culture Base:** East Asian-inspired — shogunate provinces, temple complexes, honor-bound warrior traditions, clan-based governance. Pattern magic is practiced as an art form — Ryutense mages trace patterns with calligraphic precision, and their spellbooks are works of art. Warrior traditions emphasize personal mastery, discipline, and the bond between rider and mount.
**Major Powers:** The Shogunate of Ryuten (central authority), The Temple Circle (spiritual and magical authority), The Mountain Clans (independent frontier warriors), The River Provinces (agricultural and mercantile heartland)
**Population:** ~10 million. Spread across diverse terrain.
**Climate Threat:** Monsoon season. Flooding, landslides, and storms reshape the landscape annually. Ryutense architecture and infrastructure patterns are built to flex with the water, not resist it. Foreigners who try to build rigid stone walls watch them wash away every season.
**Cultural Flavor:** Clan loyalty above all. Siltberry groves as sacred sites guarded by armed retainers. Pattern scrolls painted on silk instead of paper — Ryutense spellbooks are beautiful, expensive, and surprisingly durable. Wyvern riders as symbols of power and ferocity. Honor codes that govern everything from dueling to commerce. Mist weaver silk as a luxury export. Dueling traditions that are more ceremony than combat — unless honor is genuinely at stake.
**Unique Feature:** The siltberry groves. Guarded by shogunate soldiers, tended by temple botanists, and the source of Ryuten's leverage in Convergence politics. The siltberry bush grows nowhere else on Inkmark. The resin it produces is essential for tattoo inscription. Control of the siltberry supply means control of one-third of the world's tattoo magic.

### FACTION -- The Shogunate of Ryuten
**Type:** Centralized military government
**Leader:** Shogun Ryoma Ashikaga (iron-willed, aging, maintains control through personal reputation and strategic marriages)
**Location:** Central Ryuten, capital at Kyougoku
**Structure:** A shogunate governing through provincial daimyo. Each daimyo controls a province and owes military service and tax to the shogun. The balance of power between shogun and daimyo is constant negotiation. Daimyo who grow too powerful are curbed. Daimyo who grow too weak are absorbed.
**Population:** ~7 million within shogunate-controlled territory
**Military:** The Shogunate Army — disciplined, hierarchical, and integrated. Warrior companies form the backbone. Mage units provide pattern support. Pegasus and wyvern riders serve as elite aerial forces. The shogunate's military doctrine emphasizes combined arms — warrior, mage, and rider operating in concert.
**Siltberry Monopoly:** The shogunate controls all siltberry harvesting and export. The groves are guarded by elite retainers. Trespassing in a siltberry grove is a capital offense. Foreign merchants buy resin through tightly controlled trade agreements. The shogunate uses siltberry access the way Pendragon uses lunite — as diplomatic leverage.
**Internal Tension:** Provincial daimyo chafe under shogunate authority. The mountain clans want autonomy. The river provinces want lower taxes. The shogun is aging and his designated heir is politically weak. The other daimyo are circling.

### FACTION -- The Temple Circle
**Type:** Spiritual and magical authority
**Leader:** Abbot Seikan (centenarian, allegedly, and still tracing patterns with steady hands)
**Location:** Temple complex at Ryousen, central Ryuten
**Structure:** A council of temple abbots governing the spiritual and magical education of Ryuten. The Temple Circle operates the most prestigious pattern schools on the continent, maintains sacred siltberry groves, and provides spiritual guidance rooted in ancestral veneration and balance with nature.
**Pattern Philosophy:** Ryutense pattern tracing is an art. Where Aethmark mages treat pattern precision as engineering, Ryutense mages treat it as calligraphy — each stroke meaningful, each pattern an expression of the mage's character. A Ryutense spellbook is a work of art. The patterns are painted on silk with meticulous care. They cost ten times what a standard Aethmark spellbook costs, and they last five times as long.
**The Grove Guardians:** Elite warrior-monks who protect the siltberry groves. Every guardian has taken vows of silence except when reciting pattern activation phrases. They are among the most disciplined fighters on Inkmark. Their wyvern mounts are raised in the temple compounds — a sight that unnerves even experienced riders.

### FACTION -- The Mountain Clans
**Type:** Independent frontier warriors
**Leader:** Clan Elder Yuki of the Frost Fang (oldest clan, most respected, least likely to take orders from anyone)
**Location:** Ryuten's northern mountain provinces
**Structure:** Semi-autonomous clans who acknowledge the shogun's nominal authority but govern themselves. Each clan holds mountain territory, maintains its own forces, and settles its own disputes. The shogun tolerates this because the mountain clans are a buffer against northern threats and because suppressing them would cost more than it's worth.
**Culture:** Wyvern riders, dire wolf breeders, and some of the hardest warriors on Inkmark. The mountain clans consider hardship a virtue and comfort a weakness. Their crests tend toward the martial — Flame, Frost, and Wind crests are common among their bloodlines. Their mages are self-trained, practical, and focused on combat patterns.
**Internal Politics:** The clans are not one family. They are seven, and they hate each other almost as much as they hate outsiders. Clan alliances shift every season — a Frost Fang daughter marries into the Red Talon to seal a mining dispute, two months later Red Talon raiders steal a Frost Fang siltberry grove, the marriage is annulled by combat (the husband lost, he's dead, the daughter came home), and now the Red Talon and Frost Fang are in a blood feud again. The shogunate watches this with weary resignation. The clans call it "the natural order." The five major clans:
- **Frost Fang** — Oldest, largest, most territorial. Wyvern riders and dire wolf breeders. Their crest line runs strong (Frost). Elder Yuki is 73 and has outlived four challenges to her leadership. She killed two challengers personally. The other two withdrew when they saw her reach for her wyvern's harness.
- **Red Talon** — Aggressive, expansionist, always pushing into Frost Fang and Iron Wheel territory. Their crest runs to Flame. They breed the fastest wyverns and the worst tempers. Their current leader, Hideo, is 28 and has already started three border disputes this year.
- **Iron Wheel** — Strategists. Where other clans charge, the Iron Wheel plans. They hold the mountain passes and charge tolls. Their crest line runs to Stone — fortified skin, structural intuition. They built the mountain roads everyone else uses and they have never let anyone forget it.
- **Pale Feather** — The pegasus clan. The only mountain clan that rides pegasi instead of wyverns. The other clans consider this deeply suspicious. Pale Feather considers the other clans' wyvern-obsession equally baffling. Their crest line runs to Wind. They control the highest mountain paths — the ones wyverns can't navigate but pegasi can.
- **Silk Fang** — The political clan. They don't fight the other clans directly — they marry into them, trade with them, and slowly absorb their resources. Every other clan knows what Silk Fang is doing. No one can prove it. Their crest line runs to Beast (hawk affinity — their scouts see everything). The current matriarch, Rin, is married to a Red Talon war leader, has a sister married to an Iron Wheel road-master, and her eldest son is studying at the Convergence. She is playing a game none of the other clans fully understand.
**Blood Feuds and Resolutions:** The clans have a formal system for settling disputes — the Challenge Circle. Two disputants enter a marked circle, a neutral elder adjudicates, and the fight continues until one yields, is incapacitated, or dies. Deaths are rare but not unheard of. Yielding is common and carries only moderate shame — the mountain clans respect strength but also pragmatism. Refusing a Challenge Circle is the real disgrace.
**Relations with Shogunate:** Complicated. The clans provide border defense, wyvern riders, and siltberry access (mountain groves produce the highest-quality resin). The shogunate provides trade goods, pattern knowledge, and a market for clan exports. Neither side fully trusts the other. The clans want more autonomy. The shogun wants more control. The current arrangement holds through inertia and mutual benefit.

### FACTION -- The River Provinces
**Type:** Mercantile coalition under shogunate authority
**Leader:** Provisional Governor Isoru Tanaka (elected by Provincial Council, constantly scheming against the other four governors)
**Location:** Ryuten's central and southern river deltas
**Structure:** Five provinces, each governed by a daimyo who owes the shogun tax and troops. The river provinces are the breadbasket of Ryuten — rice, fish, silk, and siltberry processing all flow through their ports. They are wealthy, soft, and absolutely ruthless in trade. Every governor maintains a network of spies in every other governor's household. Assassinations are rare but always suspected. Poison is preferred over the sword — deniable, quiet, and the River Provinces produce the best poisons on Inkmark.
**Economy:** Rice, fish, silk, siltberry processing, and the trade routes that connect Ryuten's interior to the coast. The River Provinces don't produce siltberry — that's the mountain clans and temple groves — but they process, package, and ship it. Control of the trade routes gives them leverage that their military strength alone wouldn't justify.
**Culture:** Polite, formal, and deadly. The River Provinces practice the art of the insult wrapped in a compliment. A governor's dinner is a minefield of veiled threats and strategic gossip. Honor codes are observed in letter, not spirit — a River Province daimyo would never strike you, but they would arrange for your siltberry shipment to be delayed by three days, costing you a fortune in spoiled resin, and then apologize profusely for the "logistical difficulty."
**Relations with Shogunate:** The River Provinces pay their taxes on time and provide their troops as required. This is the minimum acceptable behavior. Behind the scenes, they lobby for lower taxes, greater trade autonomy, and reduced shogunate oversight. They are the money behind Ryuten's economy and they know it. The shogun knows it too. The relationship is transactional and suspicious on both sides.
**Key Members:** Governor Isoru Tanaka (Frost Crest, calm, calculating, has survived three assassination attempts and doesn't bother investigating who ordered them — he already knows), Trade Minister Sato (no crest, runs the siltberry export logistics with terrifying efficiency), Lady Yuki of the Pearl River Province (Beast Crest — pegasus affinity, uses her aerial scouts for trade intelligence)

### LOCATION -- Ryuten Notable Locations
**The Mistweald:** A vast forest in central Ryuten where the mist never fully clears. The trees here are ancient — some predating human settlement — and their bark grows in natural circle patterns that resemble geometric spell structures. The Temple Circle considers the Mistweald sacred; this is where the first Ryutense mages are said to have learned pattern tracing by copying the trees. Travelers report that the mist seems to respond to mana — mages find their pattern tracing improves near the Mistweald, and non-mages report a persistent tingling. The forest is home to mist weavers (the spider creatures), rare herbs used in Ryutense alchemy, and a silence that feels intentional. The Temple Circle maintains a monastery at the forest's edge where monks meditate among the pattern-bark trees.
**Kitsuhara:** A river village in the southern delta, built on stilts over the water. Known for three things: siltberry cultivation (the groves surround the village), silk spellbook production (the finest silk pages on Inkmark are made here), and the annual Kitsuhara Pattern Festival — a gathering where mages from across Ryuten come to trace patterns on the river's surface using the mist as a medium. The festival is part competition, part celebration, and part religious observance. Pattern-tracing on water is considered the highest form of Ryutense magical art — the pattern lasts only moments before the current takes it, so precision and beauty are everything. The village survives on the siltberry trade and silk production. It is quiet, beautiful, and significantly wealthier than it looks.
**The Mirror Pools:** Volcanic hot springs in Ryuten's mountain interior where the water reflects patterns that aren't there. Mages who meditate at the Mirror Pools report seeing pattern geometry in the water's surface — patterns they've never seen before, half-formed, like the water is trying to show them something. The Temple Circle sends researchers annually. Some return with new pattern insights. Some return changed — quieter, more precise, occasionally speaking in geometric shapes that don't correspond to any known pattern. The Temple Circle advises caution at the Mirror Pools. They do not forbid visits. They do not explain why.

### OVERVIEW -- Continent of Solmir
**Type:** Continental region
**Geography:** Arid to Mediterranean. Desert expanses, rocky highlands, fertile river valleys, sun-baked coastlines. Extreme heat in the interior, moderate on the coasts. Minimal forest. Ashbloom oases are the most valuable real estate on the continent.
**Culture Base:** Middle Eastern/Mediterranean medieval — desert empires, bazaar cities, faith-mandated magic traditions, temple complexes. Pattern magic is viewed through a religious lens. Certain categories are sacred (Radiance, Spirit), others are tolerated (Ignition, Flow, Stone), and one is heretical (Veil). Crests are considered divine blessings.
**Major Powers:** The Radiant Empire, The Order of the Burning Light, The Sand Caravans, The Oasis Cities
**Population:** ~10 million. Concentrated in river valleys and coastal cities. Sparse in the desert interior.
**Climate Threat:** The Sun's Judgment — mana storms in the desert interior where concentrated solar mana creates fire tornadoes, heat mirages that manifest physically, and patches of ground that spontaneously combust. Unpredictable and deadly. Caravans plan routes around known storm zones.
**Cultural Flavor:** Bazaars where pattern scrolls are sold alongside spices. Temple complexes where Radiance mages heal the faithful as an act of devotion. The Radiant Creed as both religion and law. Crest-bearers as the chosen of the Divine. Veil mages hiding their craft or facing the pyre. Ashbloom gardens guarded like fortresses. The smell of incense and the sound of calligraphy brushes on silk spell scrolls.
**Unique Feature:** The ashbloom gardens. Solmir's only source of the flower that produces the activation catalyst for tattoo inscription. Grown in volcanic desert oases, tended by temple botanists, and harvested under armed guard. The Solmiran empire controls all ashbloom cultivation and export. Withholding ashbloom extract means no tattoo magic anywhere on Inkmark — a leverage tool Solmir uses with devastating precision.

### FACTION -- The Radiant Empire
**Type:** Theocratic empire
**Leader:** The Radiant Sovereign (divine monarch, chosen by the Council of Imams)
**Location:** Solmir, capital city of Solharis
**Structure:** Divine monarchy where the Sovereign heads both state and faith. The Council of Imams advises and administers. Civil law and religious law are identical. To violate one is to violate both.
**Population:** ~8 million within imperial territory
**Military:** The Radiant Army — 12,000 soldiers organized into mixed warrior-mage companies. 3,000 pegasus knights (the Radiant Wings — always crest-bearers, always devout). 1,000 desert cavalry (light, fast, endurance-trained). Mage units organized by pattern category — Radiance companies for healing and purification, Ignition companies for assault, Stone companies for fortification.
**Faith Doctrine:** The Radiant Creed. Mana is the Divine's gift. Patterns are sacred geometry — the language of creation. Crests are the Divine's mark upon the worthy. Radiance and Spirit categories are sacred. Veil is heresy. The Burning Light enforces this doctrine with fire.
**Political Position:** The dominant power in Solmir. Claims spiritual authority over all pattern mages everywhere. Considers Aethmark's secular approach spiritually bankrupt. Considers Ryuten's philosophical approach respectful but misguided. Veil mages are to be found and purified. The ashbloom gardens are Solmir's most sacred sites after the temples themselves.
**Internal Tension:** The Sovereign's health is failing. The Council of Imams is divided between hardliners (who want to expand the Burning Light's authority) and reformists (who want to restrain it). Schismatic movements challenge the Creed's monopoly on truth. The ashbloom supply is being targeted by smugglers. Something is going to burn.

### FACTION -- The Order of the Burning Light
**Type:** Inquisitorial military order
**Leader:** Grand Inquisitor Tarek Voss (Veil mage. Yes, the irony kills him. No, you're not allowed to mention it.)
**Location:** Headquartered in Solharis, operates across all Solmir and covertly beyond
**Structure:** Specialized order of pattern-mages and warrior-inquisitors tasked with detecting, pursuing, and eliminating forbidden pattern practitioners. ~2,000 members organized into Hunt Teams of five — divination mage, combat mage, warrior-warden, interrogator, and pattern-breaker (specialist in disrupting active patterns).
**Methods:** Divination patterns to detect forbidden pattern residue. Interrogation under truth-patterns (flirting with dominion — a gray area they don't discuss). Warrior-wardens handle physical apprehension. Combat mages handle magical threats. Public executions by burning — the Order's signature.
**Reputation:** Feared. Even innocent mages avoid drawing the Burning Light's attention. The Order's zeal is genuine — they truly believe forbidden patterns will destroy the world. Their methods are brutal but effective.

### FACTION -- The Sand Caravans
**Type:** Nomadic trade network
**Leader:** Caravan Master Rasheed al-Bashir (survived three assassination attempts, still leading caravans across the worst desert routes — earned the title "the Unbroken" among caravan crews, refuses to use it himself)
**Location:** Solmir's interior — desert trade routes between oasis cities and coastal ports
**Structure:** A loose confederation of nomadic trading families who operate the only reliable transport across Solmir's hostile interior. They know the safe routes, the water caches, and the patterns that keep caravans alive in conditions that kill everyone else.
**Economy:** Everything flows through the Sand Caravans. Pattern scrolls, reagents, textiles, spices, metals, information. They're the only reason Solmir's interior is accessible at all.
**Relevance:** They see everything. They know which oasis has been corrupted by ashstalkers. They know which noble is shipping illegal patterns. They know which Burning Light patrol is where. Information is their second currency. The smart factions pay for it. The dumb ones ignore it.

### FACTION -- The Oasis Cities
**Type:** Confederation of independent city-states
**Leader:** Rotating council of city governors (the Oasis Council)
**Location:** Solmir's scattered oases — each city built around a water source and ashbloom garden
**Structure:** Independent cities bound by mutual defense pacts and trade agreements. Each city governs itself. The Oasis Council coordinates response to external threats and resolves inter-city disputes.
**Economy:** Ashbloom cultivation and export. Water trade. Pattern scroll copying (the desert cities' scriptoria produce some of the most durable scrolls on Inkmark — desert-pressed parchment lasts centuries). Caravanserai services for Sand Caravan routes.
**Relations with Empire:** Complicated. The Oasis Cities are technically imperial territory, but their remote locations and control of ashbloom production give them significant autonomy. The Empire needs their ashbloom. The Oasis Cities need imperial military protection against ashstalkers and desert raiders. Neither side fully trusts the other.

### LOCATION -- Solmir Notable Locations
**The Glass Dunes:** A stretch of desert where ancient pattern residue fused the sand into glass. The dunes stretch for thirty miles — smooth, reflective, and humming faintly with residual mana. Walking on the Glass Dunes is treacherous (the surface is slippery and the glass shards are sharp) but traversable with proper footwear. The glass formations catch light in ways that produce geometric refractions — pattern-like shapes that appear and vanish as the sun moves. Mages report improved tracing accuracy after spending time near the dunes, though the effect fades within hours of leaving. The Burning Light considers the dunes sacred (the Divine's fire made manifest) and patrols them regularly. Schismatics consider them proof that pattern magic predates the Creed. The Sand Caravans consider them a navigational hazard and route around them unless a client pays extra.
**Ashbloom Village (Miriya):** The largest permanent settlement near the primary ashbloom cultivation sites. Built around a volcanic oasis with natural hot springs. Miriya exists to grow, harvest, and process ashbloom. Every resident either works the gardens, guards the supply, or serves someone who does. The village is fortified — not against raiders (the Empire handles that), but against smugglers who try to extract ashbloom extract outside imperial channels. The hot springs are a perk of the job — ashbloom workers soak in naturally mineral-rich water that the Empire claims has no magical properties but which every resident swears improves their mana circulation.
**The Radiant Spire:** A tower in Solharis, separate from the Radiant Temple, built on the site where the First Circle is said to have been drawn. Whether the story is true or not, the Spire radiates a constant, warm mana pulse that every mage and even non-mages in the city can feel. The pulse changes subtly depending on the viewer — Radiance mages feel warmth, Veil mages feel pressure, everyone else feels a gentle hum. Pilgrims come from across Solmir to meditate in the Spire's shadow. The Burning Light maintains a permanent presence to ensure no one uses the Spire for forbidden purposes. The Spire is not the temple's property — it predates the Creed by centuries — but the Creed claims it as sacred ground anyway. The Schismatics claim it as proof that all pattern categories share a single source. The Aethmark scholars claim it as evidence of pre-Creed magical achievement. Everyone wants it. The Spire doesn't care.

**Key Locations:**

- **The Artisan Quarter:** Spellbook shops, inscription services, pattern-copying scribes, combat card printers, mount equipment, weapon smiths. Students spend too much money here.

### OVERVIEW -- Intercontinental Relations
**Aethmark-Ryuten:** Trade partners with cultural friction. Aethmark buys siltberry resin and Ryutense silk spellbooks. Ryuten buys Aethmark iron and inscribed weapons. Both sides consider the other's culture bizarre but profitable. The lunite-for-siltberry trade is the backbone of their relationship.

Attacking the Convergence means losing pattern access, tattoo inscription, and the crest registry, plus uniting two other nations against you. The treaty is held together by mutual interest, not goodwill. It works because breaking it is worse than keeping it.

## 8. ECONOMY

### ECONOMY -- The Magic-Lite Medieval Economy
**Type:** Economic system
**Currency:** 1 gold sovereign = 10 silver marks = 100 copper pence. A peasant family lives on 20-30 copper/week. A trained warrior earns 3-5 gold/month. A battle mage with a full spellbook earns 5-10 gold/month. An inscribed weapon from a master smith costs 50-500 gold depending on the pattern and quality. A tattoo inscription is priceless — you can't buy one; you earn it through competition.

**Spellbook Trade — The Knowledge Economy:**
- Standard pattern books: 5-20 silver. Printed in Velkath, sold everywhere. Basic combat patterns, everyday utility patterns. Pages degrade after moderate use. Good for beginners and battlefield mages who burn through pages.
- Scholarly spellbooks: 1-5 gold. Hand-copied by guild scribes. Better ink, thicker vellum, lasts longer. Each one unique — the mage chooses which patterns to include.
- Ryutense silk spellbooks: 10-50 gold. Patterns painted on silk with specialized ink. Beautiful, durable, and worth every copper. A status symbol and a practical investment. They last five times as long as paper spellbooks.
- Military combat card decks: 2-5 silver per deck of 20-50 cards. Disposable, fast, standard issue. Mages carry multiple decks into battle and discard destroyed cards.
- Restricted pattern pages: Priceless on the black market. Possession is a crime. The Burning Light will kill you for having them. The knowledge they contain could be worth a kingdom.

**Inscribed Equipment:**
- Weapons and armor with carved pattern circles. Anyone can use inscribed equipment — a warrior activates the pattern with a touch and channel of intent (or ambient mana for non-mages). The effect is fixed: a flame-sword always produces flame, a ward-shield always produces a barrier.
- Common inscriptions: light patterns on lanterns, warmth patterns on winter cloaks, sharpness patterns on blades, barrier patterns on shields. These are the backbone of magical infrastructure.
- Masterwork inscriptions: Complex patterns on weapons and armor. A blade that produces flame and cuts through metal. A shield that generates a barrier against pattern magic. Expensive, rare, and worth more than a horse.
- Structural inscriptions: Ward patterns on city walls, support patterns on bridges, warmth patterns in floors. Centuries-old inscriptions still function in many cities. Maintenance is a steady profession — circle-keepers walk city walls daily checking patterns.

**Crest Economy:**
- Crests cannot be bought or sold. They are inherited or spontaneously manifested.
- Crest-bearing houses command economic and political premiums. A house with a Flame Crest in its lineage commands higher fees for its warriors, more favorable trade terms, and more prestigious marriage offers.
- The Convergence Crest Registry is the authoritative record. Disputed entries are arbitrated by the Triumvirate. A confirmed entry can make a house; a removal can break one.
- Crest marriages are arranged around lineage maximization. If a Flame Crest house marries a Bastion Crest house, their children might inherit either crest. The gamble drives generations of political maneuvering.

**Mount Economy:**
- **Horses:** Standard. 5-50 gold depending on quality and training. Warhorses 50-200 gold.
- **Pegasi:** 200-500 gold for a trained, bonded mount. Actually impossible to buy a bonded pegasus — you buy the foal and hope it bonds with you. Unbonded pegasi are difficult and dangerous. Pegasus breeding programs are military assets maintained by major factions.
- **Wyverns:** Not sold through normal markets. Wyverns are captured from the wild and trained, or bred in specialized programs. A trained wyvern is worth a small estate. Most wyverns belong to military units, mountain clans, or very wealthy very determined individuals.
- **Dire wolves:** 30-80 gold. Bred in Ryuten's frontier provinces. Not common outside Ryuten but occasionally exported.
- **Great cats:** 50-150 gold. Bred in Ryuten's southern provinces. Rare exports. Only experienced handlers need apply.

**The Three Materials Economy:**
- **Lunite:** Traded primarily through Pendragon's state monopoly. Annual shipment to the Convergence is treaty-mandated. Surplus is sold at enormous markup. Black market lunite exists but is often adulterated — useless for inscription, dangerous if used.
- **Siltberry resin:** Traded through Ryuten's shogunate monopoly. Same treaty framework. Same black market problems. Siltberry resin that hasn't been properly refined degrades tattoo quality and risks inscription failure.
- **Ashbloom extract:** Traded through Solmir's imperial monopoly. Same deal. Same problems. Ashbloom extract that's been diluted or improperly stored loses catalytic potency.
- The three-material trade is the single most politically sensitive commerce on Inkmark. Disrupting any one supply chain means no tattoo inscriptions anywhere. Nations have gone to war over less.

**Resource Control by Continent:**
- **Aethmark:** Iron, timber, arable land, lunite mines. The continent's wealth comes from mineral extraction, enchanted weapon crafting, and the lunite monopoly.
- **Ryuten:** Siltberry resin, mist weaver silk, rare pattern ingredients, exotic mounts. The continent's wealth comes from controlled exports, luxury goods, and the siltberry monopoly.
- **Solmir:** Ashbloom extract, desert minerals (sun-crystals, volcanic glass), textiles, spices. The continent's wealth comes from the ashbloom monopoly, desert trade routes, and the faith economy (pilgrimage, tithes, temple services).

## 9. COMBAT DOCTRINE

### COMBAT -- Combined Arms and Tactical Reality
**Type:** How magic and steel actually fight together
**Standard Formation Doctrine:** Warrior companies hold the line. Mage units provide ranged pattern support from behind the line. Pegasus knights scout, flank, and harass. Wyvern riders deliver heavy aerial assault. This is basic military doctrine across all three continents, taught at the Convergence and in every military academy. Variations exist — Ryutense doctrine emphasizes mobility and precision over brute force, Solmiran doctrine integrates faith-pattern casting with cavalry charges — but the core principle is universal: combined arms win wars. Solo specialists win duels.

**Inscribed Weapon Tactics:**
- Any warrior can use an inscribed weapon — the pattern provides the shape, the warrior's mana provides the fuel. But inscribed weapons drain mana, and non-mages have smaller reserves. Standard doctrine: the inscribed weapon is your opening move. Activate it for the first critical strike, then fight unenhanced while your mana recovers. A warrior who keeps their inscribed weapon active for an entire battle will collapse from mana exhaustion.
- Common inscribed weapons in combat: flame-swords (ignition on cutting edge), frost-lances (freeze on impact), barrier-shields (Stone pattern that blocks one heavy hit before needing mana recharge), light-helms (Radiance pattern that illuminates dark areas — cheap and common).
- Wealthy warriors carry two inscribed weapons — one for the opening strike, one for emergencies. Poorer warriors have one and guard its mana carefully.

**Tattoo Tactics in Combat:**
- **Non-mage with Gale tattoo (flight burst):** The most feared tattoo configuration for warriors. A knight who can launch from a pegasus and then re-launch mid-engagement is a tactical nightmare. Dive lance strikes at 90 degrees from above. No ground combatant can counter it. These warriors are deployed at critical moments — not sustained combat, because 2-5 flight bursts is all a non-mage's mana reserves allow.
- **Non-mage with Flow tattoo (binding):** Immobilize a target for the duration of the cast (2-3 activations for a non-mage). Long enough for allies to finish the job. Invaluable for capturing enemy commanders or crest-bearers.
- **Non-mage with Stone tattoo (personal barrier):** Become an unbreakable shield for crucial seconds. A warrior with a Stone tattoo tanking the breach point while mages set up patterns behind them is a battle-winning formation. 2-3 barriers from a non-mage, then they're on raw steel like everyone else.
- **Non-mage with Ignition tattoo (fire strike):** The simplest and most common warrior tattoo. A flame punch or flaming blade for 2-3 activations. Doesn't win a battle alone, but the surprise factor — a warrior who shouldn't be able to do magic suddenly igniting — has broken formations.
- **Mage with tattoo (combo potential):** This is what makes tattoo-bearing mages terrifying. The tattoo provides instant setup that no book or mental trace can match. A mage with a Flow tattoo (binding) activates instantly to lock a target, then follows with an Ignition pattern from their book. A mage with a Stone tattoo pops their instant barrier and keeps casting from behind it. The two-layer casting — tattoo for instant action, book for sustained output — is the deadliest single-combatant configuration on Inkmark.

**Anti-Mage Tactics:**
- Rush them. Get inside their tracing distance before they finish a pattern.
- Destroy their book. A mage without a book is reduced to mental traces only — drastically limited.
- Use cover and terrain. Patterns require line of sight for most effects. Break line of sight, break the mage's advantage.
- Overwhelm with numbers. A mage can cast 5-10 patterns before exhaustion in combat conditions. Five warriors rushing from different angles is enough.
- Anti-pattern formations. Specific warrior formations designed to close distance and disrupt casting. Trained in every military academy.

**Anti-Crest Tactics:**
- Numerical superiority. Five warriors can bring down one crest-bearer through coordination.
- Exhaustion. Crests use the bearer's mana. They tire like anyone else. Sustained pressure works.
- Ranged attacks. Arrows, thrown patterns, and thrown weapons don't care about melee enhancement.
- Pattern support. A mage tracing a well-placed barrier or distraction can neutralize a crest-bearer's advantage.

**Duel Culture:**
- Formal duels happen at the Convergence and in every major city. They have rules: a marked circle, a judge, surrender conditions, and prohibited tactics (no forbidden patterns, no killing a surrendered opponent). Prize money and ranking points are awarded.
- Tavern duels happen everywhere. They have no rules. They start over arguments about mages vs warriors, whose house is stronger, whether the Convergence allocation is fair, or who looked at who wrong. They end when someone is unconscious, the pattern backfires, or the owner throws both parties out.
- Mage vs warrior duels are a persistent cultural phenomenon everywhere on Inkmark. In open terrain with preparation time, a mage usually wins. In close quarters with no warning, a warrior usually wins. In a tavern with three feet of space, it depends on who throws the first punch and whether the mage has combat cards accessible.
- Pegasus jousting is a formal sport at the Convergence. Riders compete in aerial lance combat, judged on skill, precision, and style. It's the most popular event at the annual tournament. Crest-bearers with Beast Crests dominate the sport. Non-crest riders compete in a separate bracket.
- Cultural attitudes: Aethmark treats dueling as a pastime and a legitimate way to settle disputes. Ryuten treats it as an honor ritual — refusing a duel is a serious insult that can only be wiped away through the duel itself. Solmir treats it as a test of faith — the Radiant Creed teaches that the Divine favors the righteous, so duel outcomes are taken as signs of divine judgment. This means Solmiran duels carry religious weight that makes them much more dangerous than Aethmark's casual bar fights.

## 10. RULES / GM KERNEL

### RULES -- Core Magic Rules
**Type:** Immutable system rules for AI GM reference
**Rule 1 — Everyone Has Mana:** Every person on Inkmark has mana. It's universal. ~25% are born with the talent to shape it through pattern circles (mages). The remaining 75% have mana but can't direct it voluntarily — they feel it, but can't trace. A non-mage with a tattoo can cast that one spell using their own mana. A non-mage without a tattoo feels mana tingling but can't do anything with it.
**Rule 2 — Pattern Is Required (for Mages):** All magic requires a pattern. A specific geometric design that channels mana into an effect. Without a pattern, a mage has mana but can't shape it. Without mana, a pattern is just geometry. Both are required.
**Rule 3 — No Element Locking:** Any mage can learn any pattern category. Ignition, Flow, Gale, Storm, Stone, Radiance, Veil, Spirit — all available. No born affinity. You learn what you study. Most mages specialize because mastery takes time, but cross-category study is possible.
**Rule 4 — Medium Determines Speed:** Mental trace (slow, mages only), book/scroll (fast, pages degrade), inscribed equipment (durable, anyone can activate, fixed effect), tattoo (instant, anyone can use, 1-2 spells, back/stomach only, requires three rare materials and Convergence inscription).
**Rule 5 — Mana Is Finite (Everyone's):** All magic costs mana. Every person's mana is finite. Exhausted people cannot cast (or activate tattoos/crests). Overcasting past exhaustion causes damage, then death. No shortcuts to recovery — rest, sleep, food. Non-mages have smaller mana reserves than trained mages, so they get fewer tattoo activations before tiring.
**Rule 6 — Tattoos Work for Everyone:** A tattoo channels the bearer's mana through the inscribed pattern. Since everyone has mana, everyone can use a tattoo — mages and non-mages alike. Non-mages get fewer casts before exhaustion (smaller reserves) but can still cast. A warrior with a healing tattoo is a medic who never runs out of bandages. This is why tattoos are the most coveted thing on Inkmark.
**Rule 7 — Crests Are on the Back:** Crests manifest between the shoulder blades. Back tattoos go in the same area. You cannot have both a crest and a back tattoo — the mana channels in the back support one major pattern. Crest or tattoo. One choice. Permanent.
**Rule 8 — One Crest = One Power:** A crest grants one specific supernatural ability. Not a suite. Not scalable. Flame Crest = flame strikes. Nothing else. The power doesn't grow — mastery of the power does.
**Rule 9 — Progression Is Mastery, Not Level:** No tiers, no ranks, no breakthroughs. A mage is as good as the patterns they know, the speed they trace, and the mana they can channel before exhaustion. A warrior is as good as their training, experience, and equipment. A crest-bearer is as good as their understanding of their specific gift. You practice. You improve. There are no shortcuts.
**Rule 10 — Forbidden Patterns Have Minimum Complexity:** Necromancy (Veil, advanced), Dominion (Spirit, advanced), Blood Manipulation (Flow, advanced), Soul Severance (Veil/Spirit, advanced). These are specific patterns, not categories. Attempting them without sufficient skill produces nothing or catastrophic backfire.
**Rule 11 — Enchantments Require Maintenance:** Inscribed patterns degrade over time. Stone: centuries. Metal: decades. Wood: years. Cloth: months. Neglected inscriptions fail. Maintenance extends lifespan indefinitely.
**Rule 12 — Back Slot Conflict:** Crest and back tattoo occupy the same physical space. Choose one. This is a hard rule, not a cultural preference. The body cannot sustain two major mana-channel patterns in the same area.

### RULES -- Combat Reference
**Type:** Core combat principles for AI GM
**Mages in Combat:**
- Without a book or tattoo, a mage can only mental-trace — slow, limited to internalized patterns, vulnerable in melee.
- With a spellbook, a mage is versatile but their book is their life. Destroy the book and the mage is reduced to mental traces only.
- With combat cards, a mage is fast but disposable. Cards burn quickly. A mage who runs out of cards and hasn't memorized patterns is helpless.
- A tattoo mage is never helpless — but they only have 1-2 spells. Versatile they are not.
- Mages are ranged threats. In melee, they need warrior protection or they die.

**Warriors in Combat:**
- No magic. No aura. Steel, skill, and training.
- A veteran warrior is a lethal combatant without any supernatural enhancement.
- Inscribed weapons and armor provide fixed magical effects. A flame-sword is always a flame-sword.
- Crest-bearers are force multipliers — one specific supernatural ability on top of full warrior training.

**Combined Doctrine:**
- Warrior companies hold the line. Mage units provide ranged pattern support. Pegasus knights scout and flank. Wyvern riders provide heavy aerial assault. This is standard military doctrine across all three continents.
- Mages without warriors get overrun at close range. Warriors without mages get shredded at range. Combined arms win wars.
- The duel between mage and warrior is a persistent cultural phenomenon. In open terrain, a prepared mage usually wins. In close quarters, a warrior usually wins. In a tavern, it depends on who throws the first punch.

### RULES -- What Can a Non-Mage Do?

## 11. HISTORICAL EVENTS

### ARC SUMMARY -- The First Patterns
**Type:** Origin myth / historical event
**Date:** Pre-history (approximately 2,000+ years before the Convergence Era)
**Summary:** No one knows where pattern magic came from. Every culture has an origin story, none can prove it.
**The Aethmark Version:** A philosopher in the northern mountains discovered that certain geometric shapes, when traced with specific pigments, produced effects. The first pattern was drawn in charcoal on cave stone. It made light. The philosopher spent the rest of their life drawing more, mapping the relationship between shape and effect. Universities are built on this legacy.
**The Ryutense Version:** The patterns were always there — written in the flow of rivers, the rings of trees, the orbits of celestial bodies. The first mage didn't invent patterns; they learned to read them. Pattern magic is not creation but translation — copying the language of nature into ink and action. The temple complexes preserve this understanding.
**The Solmiran Version:** The Divine granted the first pattern to a worthy supplicant as a gift. The circle is sacred geometry — the shape of perfection. All subsequent patterns are echoes of that first gift. This is why Radiance patterns are considered most holy — they are closest to the original light. The Radiant Creed builds its authority on this narrative.
**What Is Known:** Pattern magic has existed for at least 2,000 years. The earliest surviving inscriptions and pattern fragments date back that far. The first tattoo inscription is lost to history — whether it was discovered, invented, or granted is unknown. The Convergence Treaty was signed 800 years ago. Everything before that is legend, archaeology, and ideology.

### ARC SUMMARY -- The Convergence War
**Type:** Historical catastrophe
**Date:** Approximately 800 years before current era (0-15 CE in the Convergence Era calendar)
**Summary:** The most devastating conflict in Inkmark's history. A coalition of necromancers, blood mages, and dominion practitioners — collectively called the Hollow Pattern — attempted to establish a nation where forbidden patterns were legal and practiced openly. They raised armies of hollows (animated corpses), used blood manipulation to power war patterns beyond normal limits, and applied dominion patterns to force captives into fighting against their will.
**The Scale:** The war consumed all three continents for fifteen years. Estimated 2-3 million dead. Entire regions rendered uninhabitable by pattern residue. Several "dead zones" still exist — areas where necromantic patterns left permanent scars on the landscape. Nothing grows there. Nothing lives there. Travelers avoid them.
**The Alliance:** The war forced an unprecedented alliance between Aethmark kingdoms, Ryutense shogunates, and Solmiran theocracies. Enemies who had fought for centuries stood shoulder to shoulder. Mages and warriors fought side by side in a way that redefined military doctrine.
**The Aftermath:** Universal prohibition of forbidden patterns was codified. Enforcement mechanisms — the Burning Light in Solmir, Concord investigators in Aethmark, Temple wardens in Ryuten — were created or strengthened. The Hollow Pattern's leader was never confirmed killed. The body was never found. This haunts every authority on Inkmark.
**The Convergence Treaty:** Signed in the war's aftermath. The three nations agreed to establish a neutral island where pattern knowledge would be preserved, tattoo inscription would be regulated, and future generations would be trained together. The Convergence Treaty has held for 800 years. It is the foundation of intercontinental peace. Removing any one nation's contribution (lunite, siltberry, or ashbloom) collapses the system. This mutual dependency is by design.

### ARC SUMMARY -- The Solmiran Schism
**Type:** Religious division
**Date:** ~200 years ago in the Convergence Era
**Summary:** A Solmiran theologian named Danya published a treatise arguing that all pattern categories — including Veil — originate from the same source, and that the Radiant Creed's distinction between "sacred" and "heretical" patterns is a political construct designed to maintain theocratic power.
**The Treatise:** "On the Unity of Patterns" circulated secretly for years before the Creed discovered it. The argument: if mana is the Divine's gift, and if patterns channel mana, then all pattern categories are equally divine. The category is a tool. The caster's intent is what makes a pattern sacred or profane. Burning someone alive with an Ignition pattern is just as evil as animating a corpse with a Veil pattern.
**The Creed's Response:** Immediate condemnation. Danya was declared a heretic. The Burning Light was deployed to find and execute everyone who possessed the treatise. Danya vanished and has evaded capture for 200 years — either through exceptional cunning or, as the Schismatics believe, divine protection.
**Current State:** The Schismatic movement operates underground across all three continents. They don't practice forbidden patterns (publicly) — they argue for the right to study them. The Creed considers the distinction meaningless. The Schism is the defining internal conflict of Solmiran politics and a growing philosophical movement in Aethmark and Ryuten.

### ARC SUMMARY -- Current State of Inkmark (847 CE)
**Aethmark:** The Pendragon succession crisis simmers. Three heirs, none ideal. The Hexen Council pushes for autonomy. The Pale Vow holds the north against increasing raids from mountain predators and something worse — organized hollows sighted in the deep tunnels, which shouldn't be possible 800 years after the Convergence War. The Free Cities profit from everyone's tensions. The Hedge Mages are organizing for the first time in generations, demanding pattern library access from the Hexen Council.
**Ryuten:** The Shogun is aging. His designated heir is politically weak. Provincial daimyo are consolidating power. The mountain clans are restless and demanding more autonomy. The Temple Circle has noticed a disturbing trend — more spontaneous crest manifestations than usual, in bloodlines with no known crest history. Something is changing.
**Solmir:** The Radiant Sovereign is dying. The Council of Imams is divided between hardliners and reformists. The Burning Light is stretched thin between hunting Schismatics, pursuing forbidden practitioners, and maintaining order during the succession crisis. The Schismatic movement has released a new treatise — one that argues the original Convergence Treaty was a political compromise that suppressed legitimate pattern research. The Oasis Cities are pushing for more autonomy, and someone has been smuggling ashbloom extract out of the imperial gardens.
**The Unanswered Question:** 2,000 years after the first patterns were drawn, no one knows where mana comes from. No one knows why 25% of people are born with the talent. No one knows where crests originated. No one knows who — or what — created the first tattoo inscription ink. No one knows if the Hollow Pattern's leader is still alive. No one knows what the dead zones are actually producing after 800 years of magical residue accumulation. Every tradition claims to know. None can prove it. The mystery persists. And somewhere, a young warrior is discovering a crest mark on their hand that doesn't match any known bloodline, a hedge mage is tracing a pattern that produces an effect no book records, and an old scribe in the Convergence Library has found a fragment of a pattern that pre-dates every known origin story.
