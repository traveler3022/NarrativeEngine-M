# The Awakening — World Lore Compendium

**GM PERMANENT APPEND — Setting Bones (always in context; full detail is RAG-retrieved from sections below)**

**Writing Style:** LIGHT NOVEL / WEBNOVEL. Short punchy sentences. Present-tense action. Interior monologue kept brief. No purple prose, no flowery descriptions, no overlong metaphors. Describe what matters — the rest is sketched in a line or left to inference. Exposition weaves into action, never halts it. Dialogue carries character. When the scene breathes, keep it warm and human; when it bites, keep it sharp.
**Tone:** GRITTY, SURVIVAL, MORALLY GRAY, POLITICAL — WITH ROOM FOR WARMTH, HUMOR, AND ORDINARY LIFE. Body horror is earned in the wilds and the creatures; it does not rule every human scene.
**World State:** 100 years post-Awakening. A meteor struck the deep ocean; within weeks every non-humanoid creature mutated (larger, fiercer, reshaped). Humans were untouched. Civilization collapsed, rebuilt behind walls. Three isolated continents, no intercontinental contact (sea travel impossible — leviathans). Each believes the others may have fallen.
**Tech:** Medieval. Iron/stone/wood. No gunpowder, no magic, no non-human intelligent species. Mutation is universal and hereditary (no reversion).
**Spirit Cards:** Supernatural artifacts dropped by slain creatures (~5-15% drop rate, scales with tier). Equip by will; card dissolves into soul. **Three-card limit — absolute, no exceptions.** Cards are currency, weaponry, and status. Variant roll is random: Normal (Baseline) / Holo (Baseline + one enhancement) / Animated Holo (Total Transformation) / Sovereign (UNIQUE, domain control, max 1 per person, permanent slot for life, <20 exist).
**Strength Tiers:** Common · Alpha · Apex · Region Lord · Continent Lord.
**Continents:** Essenhall (European, militaristic) — Hochveldt / Dunncarrock / Vessenmark. Vessara (Asian, spiritual-bureaucratic) — Tengzhai / Surano / Khambattar. Nolruun (communal, primal) — Mulaguun / Orowai / Tembokarr.
**Core Tensions:** Aging/failing leadership on every continent simultaneously. Factions contest cards, territory, succession. The wilds are expanding. The Silent Season (every 7-10 years, all creatures go passive 2-4 weeks) is approaching.
**Key Rules:** Card destruction is permanent (no repair). Death drops all equipped cards beside the body. Spiritual fatigue from prolonged 3-card use (headaches → nosebleeds → hallucinations → unconsciousness). Children under ~12 can't equip. No personal names in lore — NPCs are generated live at runtime.

### OVERVIEW -- The World After the Awakening
**scan_depth:** 5
**Tone:** GRITTY, POLITICAL, SURVIVAL — WITH ROOM FOR WARMTH, HUMOR, AND ORDINARY LIFE. Body horror earned where it belongs (the wilds, the creatures).
**Political Structure:** Walled city-state empires, isolationist, siege mentality
**Status:** Stable decline. The walls hold. Barely, and not everywhere.
**Continents:** Essenhall (European), Vessara (Asian), Nolruun (Primal/Wild)
**Population Trend:** Declining. Birth rates cannot offset hunter fatalities, and the math gets worse every winter.
**Technology:** Medieval. Iron, stone, wood. No gunpowder. No magic.
**Core Conflict:** Humanity vs. the mutated wilds. Human vs. human over Spirit Cards. The powerful vs. the desperate.

### OVERVIEW -- The Awakening Calamity
**Type:** Cataclysmic event
**Date:** 100 years ago
**Known Cause:** Unknown to denizens. A meteor struck the deepest point of the empty ocean — the Point Nemo of this world.
**Actual Cause:** Extraterrestrial impact. No one witnessed it. No one knows.
**Effect:** Within weeks, every non-humanoid creature on every continent began to mutate. Size spiked, aggression surged, anatomy distorted. Insects grew to the size of hounds. Mountain wolves became the size of carts. Roosters became cockatrices. Sea creatures became leviathans.
**Humanoid Effect:** None. Humans were entirely unaffected.
**Aftermath:** Civilization collapsed within a decade. Kingdoms fell. People fled behind fortifications. Within 30 years, the walled city-state model had become the only survival strategy that still worked.
**Current Understanding:** People believe the creatures simply "awoke" — hence the name. Religious orders call it divine punishment. Scholars suspect something environmental but lack the tools to prove it.

### OVERVIEW -- Spirit Cards
**Type:** Equipable supernatural artifact
**Source:** Dropped by slain mutated creatures
**Drop Rate:** Rare. Scales with creature strength tier — see Card Acquisition and Drop Mechanics (1-5% Common to 90-100% Region/Continent Lord).
**Appearance:** All Spirit Cards look identical regardless of source creature — a flat, palm-sized card with an iridescent surface that shimmers between grey and silver. No markings, no text, no creature imagery. The card's power is felt, not seen.
**Physical Properties:** Durable but not indestructible. Can be cracked by concentrated force. Cool to the touch. Slightly heavier than paper, lighter than stone.
**Tradeability:** Fully tradeable. Cards are currency, weaponry, and status symbols all at once.
**Storage:** Carried in pouches, boxes, or sleeves. Decks are common among veteran hunters.
**Cultural Weight:** Spirit Cards are the single most important resource in the post-Awakening world. They are the reason humanity still exists. They are also the reason people betray each other.

### OVERVIEW -- Spirit Card Variants
<!-- rag: keyword, triggers: variant, rarity, normal card, holo card, animated holo, sovereign, baseline, enhancement, transformation, domain control, card type, card rarity -->
**Type:** Luck-based rarity tier (Normal/Holo/Animated Holo) + UNIQUE sovereign tier
**Variants:** Normal, Holo, Animated Holo, Animated Holo Sovereign (UNIQUE)

**Normal Card (Baseline):** Base power of the source creature — the ability as it existed in the creature itself. Most common drop. Functional, reliable, unremarkable — the workhorse card most of the world carries.

**Holo Card (Baseline Enhanced — adds one effect):** The card surface refracts light in a prismatic sheen, like oil on water. Roughly 10-15% of all card drops are Holo. A Holo adds ONE enhancement effect on top of the baseline ability. Example: a baseline climbing-support card grants climbing ability; the Holo version adds a ~30% climbing-speed buff on top. The Holo effect is always a single, additive enhancement — more of the same capability, not a new capability.

**Animated Holo Card (Total Transformation):** Extremely rare, perhaps 2-3% of all drops. The card surface moves — shifting patterns, flowing light, almost alive. An Animated Holo does not buff the baseline ability — it transforms it into something fundamentally different that achieves a similar result but amplified by a lot. Example: where baseline grants climbing and Holo adds climb speed, an Animated Holo lets the user run on vertical walls. The same core function (climbing) becomes a different kind of action entirely. Even an Animated Holo from a weak creature can outclass a Normal card from an apex predator.

**Animated Holo Sovereign Card (Domain Control — UNIQUE):** Not a random variant roll — a singular occurrence. Fewer than 20 confirmed to exist across all continents. Each Sovereign card is individually named and carries a residual will that can resist unworthy users. The granted ability transcends its creature origin entirely, becoming **domain-level power**: the wielder gains absolute control over their domain. Example: a Sovereign of fire means ALL fire within ~50m of the wielder is under their control. **Absolute suppression** — no amount of Animated Holo cards can overcome a Sovereign inside its domain. Sovereign cards carry extreme soul burden and cannot be equipped without prior spiritual conditioning through sustained Animated Holo exposure. **Sovereign rules:** max ONE Sovereign card per person; once equipped it CANNOT be unequipped by the living user (permanent slot — 1/3 of capacity, for life); the card can only be removed by the user's death (the standard death-drop then applies). See POWER SYSTEM -- Animated Holo Sovereign Cards.

**Luck Factor:** The variant roll (Normal/Holo/Animated Holo) is entirely random. A mutated sheep can drop an Animated Holo, and a legendary beast can drop a Normal. This randomness drives a gambling culture around hunting. Sovereign cards are exempt from this roll — they are singular events, not probability outcomes.

**Stacking:** A Normal card from a legendary creature is still immensely powerful — a strong baseline is a strong baseline. Variant tier changes the *nature* of the ability (Baseline → Enhanced → Transformed → Domain), not a flat number on top.

**Economic Impact:** Two creatures of the same species can yield cards worth vastly different amounts based on variant alone. Sovereign cards exist beyond economic valuation — they are traded for alliances, territory, and life debts.

### OVERVIEW -- Equipping Spirit Cards
**Type:** Soul-binding activation
**Process:** The user holds the card and wills it into their soul. The card responds by burning, cracking into glass shards, crumbling to dust, or dissolving into shadow — the visual effect varies by individual and seems tied to personality or emotional state.
**Resonance:** The card ceases to exist physically and becomes a metaphysical weight within the user's being. They can feel it — a presence, a warmth, a tension that never quite settles.
**Activation Time:** Instantaneous with intent. Can be done mid-combat.
**Unequipping:** The reverse process. The user wills the card out, and it materializes in their hand through the reverse of their personal animation (dust reforms, shards reassemble, shadows solidify, embers gather). This takes roughly one second. **Sovereign exception:** a Sovereign card CANNOT be unequipped by the living user — the slot is permanent for life. Only the user's death releases it (standard death-drop applies).
**Swapping:** A user can unequip one card and equip another in rapid succession. Skilled hunters swap cards between fights to adapt to different threats. (Sovereign cards cannot be swapped out — see above.)
**Destruction Risk:** While equipped, the card is metaphysically anchored. Concentrated attacks to the user's torso or head can damage the equipped cards. A damaged card loses potency permanently. A destroyed card is gone forever.

### OVERVIEW -- The Three-Soul Limit
<!-- rag: keyword, triggers: how many cards, maximum, equip limit, slot, three cards, capacity, fourth card, soul vessel -->
**Type:** Absolute biological constraint
**Rule:** No human can equip more than three Spirit Cards simultaneously.
**Enforcement:** Attempting to equip a fourth card results in nothing happening. The card simply does not respond. No pain, no rejection — just silence.
**Theoretical Basis:** Unknown. Scholars theorize the human soul has three vessels. No one has ever exceeded three. No exceptions have been found.
**Named Slots (Hunter Slang):** First Soul (often defensive/passive), Second Soul (often offensive/active), Third Soul (often utility/situational). This is tradition, not a rule — any card can go in any slot.
**Strategic Implications:** Card selection defines a hunter's combat identity. A three-card loadout is a build, a commitment. Changing loadouts between hunts is common among veterans. A Sovereign card permanently occupies one of the three slots — a Sovereign wielder effectively has a two-card loadout for anything outside their domain, for life.
**Cultural Impact:** "Three-card hunter" is a common term for a full professional. Two-card implies either a novice or someone who lost a card. One-card is either desperate or specialized — or a Sovereign wielder.

### OVERVIEW -- The Walls and the Wilds
<!-- rag: keyword, triggers: outside, wilds, buffer zone, farming, expedition, wall height, wall thickness, breach, beyond the walls, outside the city -->
**Type:** Territorial division
**The Walls:** Massive stone, iron, and timber fortifications that surround every surviving city-state. Heights range from 20 to 60 meters depending on the city. Thickness from 5 to 15 meters. Constantly maintained, constantly threatened.
**The Wilds:** Everything outside the walls. Former farmland, forests, plains, mountains — all overrun by mutated creatures. No safe passage. No permanent settlements.
**The Buffer Zone:** The cleared strip immediately outside the walls, typically 200-500 meters wide. Patrolled regularly. Still dangerous.
**Farming:** Done inside the walls where possible. Limited space means limited food. Some cities maintain rooftop gardens, vertical farms, or underground fungus cultivation.
**Expeditions:** Organized hunting parties that venture beyond the buffer zone. Essential for food, resources, and Spirit Cards. High casualty rate.
**Wall Breaches:** The greatest fear. When a creature or swarm breaches the wall, the toll is catastrophic. Most cities have experienced at least one breach in their history.

### OVERVIEW -- Sea Travel and Isolation
<!-- rag: keyword, triggers: sail, ocean, voyage, cross sea, travel between continents, ship, boat, naval, island, explore, other continent -->
**Type:** Geographical constraint
**Status:** Functionally impossible. Sea creatures mutated just as severely as land creatures. Leviathans, massive crustaceans, corrupted whales, razor-finned shoals.
**Result:** The three continents are entirely isolated from each other. No trade. No communication. No diplomacy between continents.
**Coastal Cities:** The most endangered settlements. Sea beasts can emerge from the water without warning. Coastal walls must extend into the sea or curve to form harbors.
**Knowledge Gap:** Each continent believes itself to be possibly the last bastion of humanity. No one knows if the other continents survived.
**Legends:** Sailors tell stories of lights on distant shores, but no expedition has returned from an intercontinental voyage in living memory.
**Implication:** Each continent has developed independently. Different cultures, different strategies, different relationships with Spirit Cards.

### OVERVIEW -- The Hunter Profession
**Type:** Professional class
**Role:** Kill mutated creatures, harvest Spirit Cards, gather resources, protect expeditions.
**Social Status:** Simultaneously revered and pitied. Hunters are heroes who keep the walls safe. They are also people who will probably die screaming in the wilds.
**Hierarchy:** Novice (0 cards), Apprentice (1 card), Journeyman (2 cards), Full Hunter (3 cards), Veteran (survived 5+ years), Legend (survived 10+ years).
**Organization:** Most hunters belong to guilds or are retained by city-states. Freelance hunters exist but are viewed with suspicion.
**Equipment:** Standard medieval arms — swords, spears, bows, crossbows, armor. Spirit Cards are the force multiplier that makes engaging mutated creatures possible.
**Mortality Rate:** Approximately 60% of hunters die within their first three years. Those who survive tend to survive a long time.
**Economic Role:** Hunters are the primary source of Spirit Cards entering the economy. Guilds take a cut. City-states tax the rest.

### OVERVIEW -- Daily Reality & the Wilds
**Type:** State of the world — life inside the walls + the mutated wilds

**Life Within the Walls:** Cramped, overcrowded cities with sharp class divide — wealthy inner districts (stone, clean water), poor outer districts (timber shacks, shared wells). Law via city guard — corruption rampant; exile beyond the walls is a death sentence. Leisure: taverns, fighting pits, card gambling halls, storytellers, religious ceremonies. Escapism is valued; a crowded tavern can be as warm as anywhere left in the world. (See §6 ECONOMY for food/water/housing/cost-of-living detail; §10 CULTURE for cultural texture.)

**The Mutated Wilds:** All non-humanoid creatures were affected; mutation is proportional to original biology — predators grew more predatory, scavengers more opportunistic, prey more defensive. Size increase universal but variable (minimum 3x; some 50x). Most remain animalistic; a few species show disturbingly tactical behavior (pack coordination, ambush planning, tool use). Mutation is hereditary — the world will not revert. Killing a mutated creature has a chance to produce a Spirit Card (mechanism unknown) that materializes near the corpse moments after death. See CREATURE -- Generation System for the procedural framework.

### OVERVIEW -- Continent of Essenhall
**scan_depth:** 5
**Type:** Continental region
**Geography:** Temperate to cold, rolling plains rising into dense forests and mountain ranges, cut by fjord-like coastlines. Harsh winters. Moderate summers.
**Culture Base:** European medieval — Germanic, Celtic, Nordic influences. Stone castles, iron forge traditions, feudal hierarchy, warrior ethos.
**Major Powers:** Hochveldt Sovereignty, Dunncarrock Dominion, Vessenmark Free Cities
**Population Estimate:** Largest of the three continents. Several hundred thousand within the walls.
**Climate Threat:** Winter drives creatures closer to the walls as food becomes scarce in the wilds. Autumn and winter are the most dangerous seasons.
**Unique Feature:** The Frostwall Frontier — the northern reaches where creatures grow largest and most aggressive, producing the most powerful Spirit Cards on the continent.
**Attitude Toward Cards:** Pragmatic and militaristic. Cards are tools of war and survival. Sentimental attachment to specific cards is discouraged in professional circles.

### FACTION -- Hochveldt Sovereignty
**Type:** Walled city-state empire
**Leader:** The Kaiser — an aging autocrat, succession contested.
**Location:** Essenhall, central plateau
**Structure:** Autocratic monarchy with a military council. The Kaiser holds absolute authority, advised by the Iron Ring — five generals who each command a wall sector.
**Population:** Largest walled city in Essenhall. Estimates range from 80,000 to 120,000 within triple-layered walls.
**Military:** The Hochveldt Vanguard — 3,000 professional soldiers, 500 licensed hunters. Heavily disciplined. Well-equipped.
**Card Policy:** All cards dropped within Sovereignty territory are subject to a 20% tax. The Kaiser's vault holds the largest known collection of high-tier cards on the continent.
**Economy:** Iron mining, weapon forging, card taxation. Self-sufficient in basic food through vast interior farming districts.
**Relations:** Tense with Dunncarrock over border mining rights. Cold trade with Vessenmark. No trust anywhere.
**Internal Tension:** The Kaiser is aging. His daughter is untested. Several Iron Ring generals have ambitions.
**Power Structure:** The Iron Ring — five generals, each commanding a wall sector, several maneuvering for the succession.

### FACTION -- Dunncarrock Dominion
**Type:** Walled city-state empire
**Leader:** The Thane — a clan warlord who holds the five clans together by force of will and political cunning.
**Location:** Essenhall, northern mountains
**Structure:** Clan-based oligarchy. The Thane rules with consent of five major clan heads. Decisions made by council vote; the Thane holds the tiebreaker.
**Population:** Moderate. 40,000 to 60,000. Carved into a mountainside, the city grows upward and inward rather than outward.
**Military:** The Stonebloods — 2,000 soldiers, 300 hunters. Specializing in mountain warfare and close-quarters combat.
**Card Policy:** Clans hoard cards. Each clan maintains its own vault. Inter-clan card trades are bitter negotiations.
**Economy:** Stone quarrying, copper mining, mountain goat herding (mutated, but manageable indoors). Poor food production — relies on aggressive hunting parties.
**Relations:** Hostile toward Hochveldt over mining territory. Indifferent to Vessenmark. Deeply isolationist.
**Internal Tension:** Clan rivalries are constant. Assassinations are not uncommon. The Thane maintains control through personal force and political cunning.
**Power Structure:** Five major clan heads — military, mining wealth, and their respective client networks, each jockeying for primacy under the Thane's arbitration.

### FACTION -- Vessenmark Free Cities
**Type:** Alliance of three walled city-states
**Leader:** Merchant Council (rotating presidency)
**Location:** Essenhall, southern coast and river delta
**Structure:** Merchant oligarchy. Each of the three cities — Vessen, Marken, and Delft — governs independently but coordinates defense and trade through the Merchant Council.
**Population:** Combined 50,000 to 70,000. Spread across three smaller walled settlements connected by fortified river passages.
**Military:** The Free Shields — 1,500 soldiers, 200 hunters. Modest but well-funded. Mercenary supplement common.
**Card Policy:** Open market. Cards are trade goods with minimal taxation. The Vessenmark Card Exchange is the most active trading floor on the continent.
**Economy:** Trade, fishing (dangerous — river only), craft goods, card brokerage. Wealthiest per capita but most exposed to coastal threats.
**Relations:** Trade with both Hochveldt and Dunncarrock when possible. Trusts neither. Maintains neutrality as a business strategy.
**Internal Tension:** The three cities constantly compete for dominance. Marken wants more military spending. Delft wants more trade. Vessen wants control of the council.
**Power Structure:** Three city delegations on the Merchant Council — Vessen's trade lords, Marken's military lobby, and Delft's card brokers — each pulling the council in a different direction.

### LOCATION -- Hochveldt City
**Type:** Walled city-state capital
**Location:** Central Essenhall plateau
**Geography:** Built on a raised plateau with natural cliff faces on three sides; walls enclose ~4 sq km. Stone/iron, austere, function over form — barracks, forges, and granaries dominate; the Kaiser Keep sits highest.
**Walls:** Triple walls — outer 30m high / 8m thick (iron-reinforced gates), middle 40m / 10m, inner 25m around the keep district.
**Districts:** Outer Ring (residential, markets, forges; Gate Street = main commercial artery, loud dawn to curfew; outer garrisons double as shelters). Middle Ring (military barracks, training grounds, Hunter Guild hall, the Pit — underground card-testing/dispute arena). Inner Ring (Kaiser Keep, the Aula great hall, noble estates, primary card vault).
**Notable Feature:** The Pit — underground combat arena for card testing and dispute resolution.
**Threat Level:** Moderate. Plateau geography provides natural defense. Main threats come from airborne creatures and tunneling beasts.

### LOCATION -- Dunncarrock City
**Type:** Walled mountain fortress
**Location:** Northern Essenhall mountains
**Geography:** Carved directly into a granite mountainside; the wall is partly natural cliff face, partly constructed stone barriers sealing the mountain passes. Everything is stone — tunnels, chambers, great halls. Perpetually dim; lamplight is the only daylight most residents see.
**Walls:** Three mountain passes sealed with iron-bound timber gates 15m thick, reinforced daily.
**Districts:** Lower Deep (common quarters, markets, forges), Mid Hollow (clan halls, military barracks), Upper Peak (Thane's hall, clan vaults, meeting chambers).
**Notable Feature:** The Echo Chamber — a vast natural cavern used for clan assemblies; acoustics carry a whisper across 200 meters.
**Threat Level:** High. Mountain creatures are large and aggressive. Tunneling beasts are a constant threat — new tunnels discovered weekly.

### LOCATION -- Vessenmark Cities
**Type:** Triple walled coastal settlements
**Location:** Southern Essenhall coast and river delta
**Geography:** Three cities across a river delta — Vessen at the coast, Marken 5 km upriver, Delft at the river fork. Timber and stone with decorative flourishes (carved eaves, painted shutters); merchants display wealth through architecture.
**Walls:** Single walls per city, 20-25m high, well-maintained. Delft has a secondary interior wall protecting its card exchange.
**Districts:** Dock District (Vessen only, heavily fortified against sea beasts), Market Row (in each city), Artisan Quarter, Residential Rings.
**Notable Feature:** The River Chain — a massive iron chain that can be raised between Vessen and Marken to block river traffic. Primarily defense against aquatic mutants.

### LOCATION -- Vessenmark Notable Locations
**Type:** City detail
**The Driftwood:** A dockside tavern in Vessen, popular with hunters, merchants, and off-the-books business. The owner is neutral by policy — no faction disputes inside. Information changes hands here more freely than in the Merchant Council chambers, and the ale is honestly priced.
**Salt Street:** The medical district in Vessen, running parallel to the harbor wall. Herbalists, surgeons, and unlicensed practitioners operate side by side. Best medical care outside Hochveldt military hospitals, if you can afford it.
**Gullspit Row:** A boarding house street in the Vessen dock district. Cheap rooms, no questions asked. Transient hunters, dock workers, and people between lives. The landladies see everything and remember faces.
**The Fishmarket:** Marken's primary trade hub, built around the loading docks. Seafood, salvage, and untraceable goods. The red clay of the loading docks tracks foot traffic — a detail that matters when thefts occur.

### LOCATION -- Thornvale
**Type:** Independent unwalled settlement
**Location:** Essenhall, forested region between Vessenmark and the wilds
**Geography:** Hidden in a dense forest valley; no walls, no open roads — camouflaged across tree platforms and hidden dugouts.
**Population:** ~500 — exiles, outlaws, and those who choose freedom over safety. Stealth/evasion over confrontation; residents know the forest and avoid creatures rather than fight.
**Card Policy:** Whatever you can carry. No governance, taxation, or protection.
**Reputation:** Haven for criminals/renegades; hunters visit to trade cards without oversight. The three empires officially condemn Thornvale; unofficially, they use it.
**Notable Resident:** A legendary outcast hunter who answers to no faction.

### LOCATION -- The Deep Roads (Whispering)
**Type:** Underground network beneath Mulaguun — ancient natural tunnels extending far beyond the city, some wide enough for soldiers, others shoulder-width, many unmapped. Blind predators, burrowing insects, and fungal growths that are technically alive and hostile occupy the unexplored sections. Deep Road creatures produce unique cards (earth/sensory abilities: tremor sense, stone manipulation, darkness adaptation). Mulaguun sends monthly mapping expeditions; most return with partial data, some don't return. **The Whispering Chamber** — a section where voices carry impossibly far; hunters report words from creatures that shouldn't speak. No confirmed reports, many disturbed hunters. The "Whispering Deep" hints at something intelligent below — the Deep King has sealed the deepest tunnels; his heir disagrees.

### LOCATION -- The Green Hell
**Type:** Geographic death zone — central-southern Nolruun jungle, canopy so thick sunlight never reaches the ground, humidity that drowns, plants evolved to kill. Highest creature density on any continent (every square meter contains something that wants to kill you — insects, reptiles, amphibians, mammals, plants, all mutated, all aggressive). Excellent and diverse card yield (poison, camouflage, regeneration, venom spray, rapid growth). No expedition has crossed it; parties enter the edges, kill what they can, retreat; overnight is a death sentence. The **Heart Tree** — 200m tall, visible from Orowai's highest platforms; what lives in/around it is unknown. Guardian: **The Heart Tree Guardian** (see §11 CREATURES).

### LOCATION -- The Breach
**Type:** Contested warzone — a 50-meter section of Tengzhai's outermost wall (eastern sector) destroyed by a massive creature 15 years ago. Rebuilding slow; temporary timber/iron barriers in place. Constant creature pressure; daily attacks; the area inside is a militarized no-go zone. Critical vulnerability — if barriers fail before the wall is rebuilt, the outer ring (thousands of residents) falls. Perpetual construction under fire; workers/soldiers die weekly; Ministry of Works prioritizes this above all. **The Graveyard** — open ground before the breach where hundreds of creature corpses pile up; stench carries for miles; scavengers pick through for cards.

### OVERVIEW -- Continent of Vessara
**scan_depth:** 5
**Type:** Continental region
**Geography:** Diverse — temperate plains, bamboo forests, terraced mountains, monsoon coastlines, river deltas. Warm and humid in the south, cold and mountainous in the north.
**Culture Base:** Asian medieval — Chinese, Japanese, Korean, Indian, Southeast Asian influences. Dynastic governance, ancestor reverence, martial discipline, philosophical traditions.
**Major Powers:** Tengzhai Ascendancy, Surano Compact, Khambattar Mandate
**Population Estimate:** Second largest continent. Dense urban populations within layered walls.
**Climate Threat:** Monsoon season drives creatures from flooded lowlands toward elevated cities. The monsoon months are the bloodiest.
**Unique Feature:** The Jade Steps — a vast terraced mountain range that serves as both natural fortress and hunting ground. Creatures here are fast, agile, and cunning.
**Attitude Toward Cards:** Spiritual and strategic. Cards are viewed as extensions of the self. Bonding with a card is a philosophical commitment. Changing loadouts frequently is seen as spiritually shallow.

### FACTION -- Tengzhai Ascendancy
**Type:** Walled city-state empire
**Leader:** The Mandate Holder — heaven-chosen in doctrine, aging and ailing in practice.

### POWER SYSTEM -- Spirit Card Combat
<!-- rag: keyword, triggers: combat, fight, attack, damage, cooldown, energy, fatigue, card destruction, synergy -->
**Type:** Combat framework
**Core Principle:** Spirit Cards transform ordinary humans into capable combatants. Without cards, a human cannot fight mutated creatures. With cards, they have a chance.
**Card Activation:** Equipped cards are always on — their effects are passive or activated by will. A strength card makes you stronger constantly. A fireball card lets you project fire when you choose.
**Energy & Cooldown:** Each card has its own self-replenishing spiritual energy reserve (not tied to physical stamina). Powerful abilities have longer cooldowns — weak cards recharge in minutes, moderate in tens of minutes, powerful in hours, legendary up to a full day. Passive abilities drain slowly; active abilities drain in bursts. When fully depleted, a card stays equipped but inactive until recharged (fastest when resting/sleeping; slow during combat; none when unconscious from fatigue). In desperate situations, a user can force a depleted card to activate — this damages the card permanently, risking cracking or destruction. Some environments affect recharge (volcanic areas boost heat-based cards; underground boosts earth-based) — theoretical, not fully documented.
**Fatigue:** Prolonged card use (days without rest) causes headaches, nosebleeds, hallucinations, eventually unconsciousness. Maintaining three cards simultaneously creates cumulative spiritual fatigue. Cards forcibly unequip when the user loses consciousness (except Sovereign cards — see permanent-slot rule).
**Combat Synergy:** Three-card loadouts that complement each other are more effective than three random cards. A shield card + speed card + offensive card is a classic balanced build.
**Card Destruction in Combat:** Concentrated attacks to the user's chest or head can crack equipped cards — the card's energy bleeds out through the crack, weakening it permanently. Complete destruction dissolves the card; it cannot be recovered. Experienced hunters learn to target an opponent's cards during human-on-human combat — card protection is a martial skill.

### POWER SYSTEM -- Equipping & Limits
<!-- rag: keyword, triggers: equip, unequip, slot, three cards, loadout, swap, card destruction, cracked, soul strain, conditioning, rejection, age limit, death drop -->
**Type:** How cards enter/exit the soul, what breaks them, what it costs

**Three-Card Limit:** No human can equip more than three Spirit Cards simultaneously. Absolute — no exceptions in recorded history. No technique, training, or condition allows a fourth card. Attempting to equip a fourth results in nothing happening; the card simply does not respond. Scholars theorize the human soul has three vessels. Named slots (hunter slang): First Soul (often defensive/passive), Second Soul (offensive/active), Third Soul (utility/situational) — tradition, not a rule.

**Equipping:** Instantaneous by will. The user holds the card and wills it into their soul; it dissolves with a visual effect unique to the individual (burning, cracking into glass shards, crumbling to dust, dissolving into shadow — tied to personality/emotional state). The card becomes a metaphysical weight within the user's being — a presence, warmth, tension that never quite settles. Can be done mid-combat.

**Unequipping & Transfer:** ~1 second; the card materializes in hand through the reverse of the personal animation. After unequipping a non-Sovereign card, a brief transfer cooldown (seconds to a minute) before a new card can be equipped; skilled hunters train to minimize this gap. Swapping between fights is common among veterans.

**Card Destruction & Preservation:** Concentrated physical/spiritual force to the card's metaphysical anchor (chest or head) cracks the card audibly — light bleeds from the fracture, ability weakens immediately. A cracked card loses 20-60% of effectiveness; cracked Animated Holo degrades toward Holo enhancement level. Further damage shatters the card entirely — gone forever. No repair exists (most active research area across all continents). Preservation: hunters use defensive body positioning, chest guards, reinforced helmets, protective stances. Some intentionally unequip non-Sovereign cards before heavy hits. Card destruction is permanent wealth destruction — a destroyed Animated Holo can represent years of hunting income. Damaged cards can be visually masked to appear undamaged (fraud, punished severely). Cards cannot be counterfeited.

**Sovereign Degradation:** A cracked Sovereign card's domain shrinks and control weakens, but it retains its residual will — harder to control, not easier. May resist its user or act erratically. Still permanently equipped regardless of damage state. A destroyed Sovereign card is a world-level loss.

**Rejection & Age Limits:** Card rejection is extremely rare (~1 in 500 people) — some individuals cannot equip certain cards; the card simply doesn't respond; no pattern identified. Children under ~12 cannot equip cards (spiritual vessels not yet developed); attempts force the card back out with mild discomfort.

**Death Drop:** When a card user dies, all equipped cards materialize beside the body within minutes. Battlefields are rich picking grounds — and some hunters kill other hunters for this reason.

**Loadout Synergy & Conflict:** Complementary cards create emergent effects (fire + wind = firestorm; strength + speed = overwhelming force). Conflicting cards cause spiritual turbulence (fire + ice reduces both). The Three-Soul Doctrine teaches a sword-shield-tool pattern (one offensive, one defensive, one utility). Cultural variations: Essenhall favors versatility, Vessara favors doctrinal balance, Nolruun favors specialization based on environment.

**Soul Strain & Conditioning:** The human soul adapts to spiritual burden through graduated exposure. Without conditioning, higher-tier strain causes damage; with conditioning, capacity expands — but never beyond three vessels.
- **Normal (Zero Strain):** Any human above 12 equips without risk. No training needed.
- **Holo (Minimal Strain):** Brief disorientation lasting seconds; adaptation within minutes. No training needed.
- **Animated Holo (Significant Strain):** First equipping without conditioning causes headaches, dizziness, disorientation lasting 10-30 minutes. With repeated exposure over weeks, strain diminishes. Most hunters reach baseline tolerance within 3-6 months.
- **Sovereign (Extreme Strain):** Cannot be safely equipped without prior Animated Holo conditioning. Unconditioned users risk unconsciousness, vessel damage, or catastrophic rejection (vomiting, seizure-like spasms, potential coma).

**Conditioning Stages:**
- Stage 1 (Raw): Single Animated Holo sustainable for hours with periodic headaches.
- Stage 2 (Adapted): Multiple Animated Holos sustainable with mild symptoms (occasional nosebleeds, fatigue). Most veteran hunters.
- Stage 3 (Conditioned): Full Animated Holo loadout sustainable indefinitely, no symptoms. Rare.
- Stage 4 (Sovereign-Ready): Soul vessels dense enough to accept a Sovereign card. Many veterans reach this; the problem is the card — barely any exist.

**Sovereign Equipping Process:** Even at Stage 4, equipping takes a full minute of resistance — intense chest pressure, heightened senses, cold sweat, the soul being reshaped. The card may test the user (spiritual confrontation; assert dominance over the residual creature will). Failure = rejection; success = the card submits and bonds. A Sovereign card is never comfortable — a constant presence, heavy, alive, aware. The weight is tolerable but never absent, and it never leaves (permanent slot for life).

**UNIQUE Status:** Each Sovereign card is singular. There are no duplicates. Each card is individually named by its wielder or by historical record. Names reflect the card's nature: Sovereign's Reach, The Architect, Void-Suture, Crown-of-Thorns. No two Sovereign cards share a name or ability expression.
**Appearance:** Unlike lower variants which are iridescent but uniform, a Sovereign card's surface manifests faint imagery — shifting depictions of the source creature at the height of its power. The card radiates a subtle warmth. The surface does not merely move; it responds to attention, as if watching back. Held long enough, users report hearing something — not words, but presence.

They manifest only from Region Lord creatures (and, rarely, Continent Lords) — those that have survived decades, grown beyond their species' norms, and developed territorial sovereignty over a region. The creature must be killed in direct face-to-face combat. No traps. No ambush from distance. No proxy kills. The kill must be earned. Even then, the drop is not guaranteed — the creature must have accumulated enough spiritual density through age and dominance to produce a Sovereign card upon death.
**Known Quantity:** Fewer than 20 Sovereign cards are confirmed to exist across all three continents. Each has been named. Each carries a reputation. Factions have gone to war over a single Sovereign card. Possession marks the wielder as a world-level individual.
**Sovereign Will:** Sovereign cards possess a residual imprint of the source creature's will — not intelligence, but instinct. A Sovereign card can resist equipping by a user it deems insufficient. This resistance manifests as physical pain, spiritual recoil, and in extreme cases, the card physically refusing to dissolve during the equip attempt. The rejection causes immediate spiritual damage: nosebleeds, disorientation, and chest pain lasting hours. Multiple rejection attempts can cause permanent vessel damage.
**Permanent Slot — Max ONE, For Life:** A user may equip at most ONE Sovereign card. Once equipped, a Sovereign card **CANNOT be unequipped by the living user** — the slot is permanent for life (1/3 of the user's capacity, forever). The card can only be removed by the user's death, at which point the standard death-drop applies (card materializes beside the body, claimable by another). Pushing past the limit causes accelerated symptoms: tremors, hallucinations, spiritual hemorrhaging (bleeding from eyes and ears), and eventual unconsciousness. When the user loses consciousness, the card goes dormant (it remains equipped — permanent slot — but inactive until the user recovers). It does NOT unequip.
**Permanent Effects:** Long-term Sovereign card users report permanent changes — heightened sensitivity to other card users, the ability to sense Sovereign cards in proximity, and subtle personality shifts aligned with the card's nature. These changes persist for life (and are irreversible, consistent with the permanent-slot rule). Scholars debate whether this is the user adapting to the card or the card slowly rewriting the user.

A single creature produces at most one card; groups drop independently. The creature must be killed deliberately (accidental deaths — falling, drowning — do not produce cards). Corpse condition irrelevant — even dismembered creatures can drop. Cards are effectively indestructible when unequipped (water, mud, lava don't degrade them).

**Drop Probability (scales with strength tier):** Common: 1-5%. Alpha: 10-20%. Apex: 30-50%. Region Lord / Continent Lord: 90-100%.

**Variant Roll (independent of creature type, separate from drop roll):** Normal: ~83%. Holo: ~14%. Animated Holo: ~3%. Sovereign cards do NOT follow this roll — see Animated Holo Sovereign Cards for sovereign drop conditions.

**Strength Tiers (no numbers — each names how far the creature outscales an equipped hunter):** Common · Alpha · Apex · Region Lord · Continent Lord.
- **Common:** Vermin to standard quarry. A lone hunter handles most; packs raise the stakes. The bulk of every hunt.
- **Alpha:** Serious threats — pack leaders, large solo predators. Needs a coordinated party of 2-4. Upper end of regular professional work.
- **Apex:** Apex predators. Veteran parties with optimized loadouts only. Encounters are rare and career-defining.
- **Region Lord:** A unique beast that rules a region — survived decades, grown beyond its species, holding territorial sovereignty. Months of planning to challenge; return is not assumed. The sole source of Sovereign cards.
- **Continent Lord:** Continent-defining, world-level. Near-mythic, often never confirmed killed. A kill reshapes political maps. See Legendary Lords.

**Intelligence Multiplier:** Most creatures are Instinctive or Responsive (basic pack coordination). Tactical ones flank, ambush, prioritize targets; sapient-adjacent ones use tools, avoid traps, hold grudges. Intelligence raises effective tier — a tactical Alpha can outfight an instinctive Apex.

### ECONOMY -- Overview
<!-- rag: keyword, triggers: buy, sell, trade, shop, market, price, cost, money, currency, coin, gold, silver, copper, barter, tax, guild cut, wages, expensive, cheap, afford -->
**Type:** Economic system (full reference — the AI GM can infer medieval siege-economy details; these are the load-bearing facts)

**Currency & Exchange:** Spirit Cards are the closest thing to universal currency — intrinsic value, portable, durable, universally desired. Traditional coinage (copper/silver/gold, minted per city-state) for daily transactions; cards for major ones. High-value goods priced in cards ("three Holo cards" = a recognized price point). Exchange rate fluid — a Holo card might buy a house in a small settlement or a month's rent in a major city. **Card price ranges (by variant):** Normal common ability: 1-2 months wages for a laborer. Holo common: 6-12 months. Animated Holo: years of wages. Legendary: theoretically priceless — traded for political favors, not coin. Sovereign: beyond valuation — exchanged for alliances, territorial concessions, dynastic marriages, or life debts; a single Sovereign card has shifted the balance of power between factions. Barter common in outer districts and between hunters. Inflation is persistent — as card supply slowly grows, coin devalues against cards; long-term, coin fades, cards dominate. Credit is rare, risky, informal; default punished by exile or death. Cards cannot be counterfeited; damaged cards can be visually masked to appear undamaged (fraud, punished severely).

**Resource Scarcity (ranked):** Food (scarcest — walled farming can't feed populations; hunting adds risky meat; universal rationing, recurring famine) > Timber (construction, weapons, fuel; harvested outside walls — logging as deadly as hunting) > Iron (weapons, armor, wall reinforcement; mined from fortified pits where possible) > Medicine (herbal/alchemical, limited by wild-growing plants; healers highly valued) > Information (premium-priced — creature-territory maps, drop data, strategic intelligence) > Labor (cheapest — dense population, low individual life-value in outer districts).

**The Hunter Economy:** Hunters are the primary source of cards entering the economy. Guilds take 15-25% of earnings in exchange for equipment, information, and burial services. Independent freelancers keep 100% but bear 100% of costs. Wealthy nobles/merchants retain hunters as personal security (steady pay + card access for loyalty/service). The cycle — kill creatures, obtain cards/materials, sell to fund expeditions, kill more — drives the entire post-Awakening economy. Black market is significant: stolen cards, unregistered trades, forced card extraction (killing for cards). Card ownership is the primary wealth divide — those with cards have power, income, security; those without serve those who do.

**Taxation & Tribute:** Wall tax universal (funds wall upkeep, guards, reserves; paid in coin or labor). Card tax varies by faction — Hochveldt takes 20% of cards found in territory; Tengzhai requires registration; Tembokarr extracts contributions for merit-based redistribution. Hunting licenses required to hunt outside the walls. Smaller settlements pay tribute to larger city-states for protection (client-patron ties, feudal-style). Rampant evasion — cities collect an estimated 40-60% of owed taxes.

**Inter-Faction Trade:** Each faction exports a signature good, imports survival basics. Essenhall: Hochveldt (iron/weapons ↔ food/timber), Dunncarrock (stone/copper ↔ food), Vessenmark (craft goods/card brokerage ↔ raw materials). Vessara: Tengzhai (rice/bureaucracy ↔ specialty cards), Surano (salt/fish ↔ food/weapons), Khambattar (medicine/instruction ↔ building materials). Nolruun: Mulaguun (minerals ↔ food/medicine), Orowai (medicinal plants/creature materials ↔ iron/tools), Tembokarr (weapons ↔ food/wood). Food-poor powers are vulnerable — a food import is political pressure as much as trade. No inter-continental trade (sea travel impossible). Overland routes dangerous; caravans heavily guarded; losses to creatures and bandits expected.

**Cost of Living (compressed):** Inner city — stone, safe, expensive. Outer city — timber, crowded, cheap, exposed during breaches. Grain porridge staple; meat a luxury. Water free at public wells (long queues) or delivered for coin. Funerals costly (in-wall burial expensive; most afford only cremation; paupers to mass pits). Debt common, often generational — debt bondage is slavery in all but name.

### BACKGROUND -- How the World Broke
**The Awakening (100 years ago):** A meteor struck the deep ocean. Within weeks, every non-humanoid species mutated (see OVERVIEW -- The Awakening Calamity). Humans were untouched. The militias and armies that marched against the new beasts were slaughtered.
**The Great Collapse (first decade):** An estimated 40-60% of humanity died in the first year. Old capitals fell, famine spread, libraries burned, and centuries of knowledge were lost. Land lost its worth and hereditary nobility lost relevance; warlords and the merely ruthless became the new rulers. Survivors clung to defensible ground.

### BACKGROUND -- The Rise of Cards and Walls
**The First Card (~99 years ago):** About six months into the Awakening, a hunter killed a mutated wolf and found a shimmering card beside the corpse. He equipped it on instinct and gained power. This "First Hunt" is now foundational myth on all three continents — each claims the first hunter as its own, and he may have been three different people. It set the cycle the world still runs on: kill creatures, gain cards, survive.
**The Wall-Rising Era (95-70 years ago):** Survivors fortified plateaus, passes, and river-bends. Card-enhanced labor raised proper walls, farming moved inside them, and the hunter profession formalized. Warlords who held walls became rulers, and the city-states settled into the borders they hold today.
**The Animated Holo Discovery (~50 years ago):** The first moving-surface card appeared — feared as cursed until its wielder showed power far beyond any Holo. It raised the ceiling, turned every cheap card into a lottery ticket, and birthed hunting's gambling culture. (Sovereign cards came later and remain singular — see the power system.)

### BACKGROUND -- The World at Time of Play
**scan_depth:** 5
**The fraying equilibrium:** The post-Awakening balance is breaking down. Every continent faces aging or failing leadership at once, and the scramble to replace it is opening power vacuums.
- **Essenhall:** Aging Kaiser and Thane, unclear successions; Vessenmark Merchant Council feuding; 40-year-old Card War left Hochveldt and Dunncarrock in cold peace, garrisons still facing off across Ironveil Pass.
- **Vessara:** Tengzhai Breach (15 years unhealed, creature never classified/killed); Mandate Holder failing; Surano under sea-creature pressure; Khambattar Sage Council splitting along doctrine.
- **Nolruun:** Tembokarr's fourth wall threatens neighbors as Ash Sovereign ages and challengers circle; Mulaguun's "Whispering Deep" — the Deep King sealed the deepest tunnels, his heir disagrees; Green Hell expanding measurably each year, driving creatures toward settlements.
**The Silent Season:** Every 7-10 years all creatures everywhere fall passive for 2-4 weeks. No one knows why. It is the safest window for expeditions — and the season that follows it is always brutal.

### RELATIONSHIP -- Named Underground Organizations
**Type:** Organized extra-legal groups
**Sovereign's Weave:** A secretive organization that tracks, monitors, and acquires Sovereign-tier cards. They believe Sovereign cards are fragments of whatever caused the Awakening. Their operatives — Curators — are embedded in cities across all continents. They rarely steal directly; instead they engineer situations where cards change hands. Led by a figure called the Overseer. Present everywhere, visible nowhere.
**Card Syndicates:** Local criminal networks in every major city specializing in card theft and fencing. Each city has its own syndicate; they share intelligence through the Broker Network. If you need to find someone, acquire something illegal, or disappear, the Network can arrange it. The individual known as the Broker may or may not sit at its center. ### RULES -- World Physics & Realism
<!-- rag: keyword, triggers: combat, survival, wall defense, breach, weather, shelter, night, wound, infection, expedition, realism, physics -->
**Type:** Fundamental world axioms + medieval realism baseline

**World Axioms (not derivable from realism):**
- No magic. Spirit Cards are supernatural but unexplained — no spells/enchantments/wizards.
- Medieval tech: iron/steel/stone/wood/rope. No gunpowder, electricity, steam, or printing press (knowledge hand-copied).
- Humans are the only intelligent species — no elves/dwarves/orcs. World is human vs. nature (mutated).
- Mutation is universal (every non-humanoid creature affected) and hereditary (breeds true; no reversion).
- Spirit Cards are mysterious: no one knows why cards appear, why 3 is the limit, or why they work. Facts, not mechanisms.

**Combat & Survival (medieval realism applies):**
- Initiative: speed/awareness; reflex cards give advantage. Damage is real — a sword wound bleeds regardless of cards; cards enhance, don't make invulnerable.
- Card activation: active abilities need conscious will + energy; passive always on. Card destruction in combat: concentrated chest/head damage targets the card's metaphysical anchor.
- Fatigue: extended 3-card combat causes cumulative fatigue (headaches, nosebleeds, tremors); prolonged fights favor endurance management.
- Human vs human: common; strategy = target opponent's cards while protecting your own (card destruction is valid if dishonorable).
- Human vs creature: standard engagement; creatures have physical advantages, humans have card versatility + tactical intelligence; teamwork essential.
- Food/water must be secured; extended expeditions need supply chains; dehydration/starvation as deadly as creatures.
- Shelter required outside walls — night in the wilds without shelter is suicide; temporary fortifications standard.
- Extreme weather affects combat/movement (winter Essenhall, monsoons Vessara, heat Nolruun); weather planning critical.
- Creatures mostly territorial; some ambush; a few tactical intelligence — understanding behavior = survival.
- Injuries life-threatening without cards/medicine; even with healing cards, serious wounds need time/rest; infection constant.
- Wilds at night = death; creatures more active, visibility limited; only night-vision cards operate effectively.

**Wall Defense Protocols (hard reference):**
- Wall Maintenance: daily inspections; weekly repairs; monthly structural assessments. Neglect kills.
- Gate Security: triple-locked; guarded 24/7; open only for scheduled expeditions + emergencies; no exceptions without commander authorization.
- Signal System: fire = creature approach; bell = wall breach; horn = general alarm. Each city has its own code.
- Patrol Schedule: wall every 2h; buffer zone daily; extended weekly. Pattern variation prevents creature learning.
- Breach Protocol: seal section → evacuate outer district → deploy hunters to kill zone → engineers assess. Priority: save lives, then restore wall.
- Emergency Rations: every city maintains ~30 days basic rations for sieges; refreshed annually.

### RULES -- Spirit Card Quick Reference
**Type:** Quick-reference (full detail in §5 POWER SYSTEM)
- **Three-Card Limit:** Absolute maximum; no exceptions, no workarounds. (See §5.)
- **Card Variants:** Normal = Baseline. Holo = Baseline Enhanced (adds one effect). Animated Holo = Total Transformation (baseline becomes a different, amplified action). Sovereign = Domain Control (absolute suppression, UNIQUE, not a variant roll). Variant is random and independent of creature type — see §5.
- **Sovereign Rules:** Max ONE Sovereign per person. Once equipped, CANNOT be unequipped by the living user — permanent slot for life. Only released by the user's death (standard death-drop applies).
- **Card Drop Rate:** Scales with tier — weak: ~0; common: very rare; large: uncommon; apex: moderate; legendary: guaranteed. (See Card Acquisition and Drop Mechanics for %.)
- **Equipping:** Instantaneous by will; card dissolves into the user's soul with a visual effect unique to the individual.
- **Unequipping:** ~1 second; card materializes in hand through reverse visual effect.
- **Destruction:** Concentrated attacks to chest/head can crack or destroy cards. Permanent. No repair.
- **Energy System:** Self-recharging, cooldown-based; powerful abilities take longer; emergency overuse damages the card permanently.
- **Death Drop:** Equipped cards materialize beside the body when the user dies. Always.

### RULES -- Isekai Background Rules
**Type:** Special background rules
**Arrival:** Isekai characters appear in the world with no explanation. They materialize in random locations — sometimes inside walls, sometimes in the wilds. Survival of arrival is not guaranteed.
**Knowledge Gap:** Isekai characters do not know about Spirit Cards, the Awakening, creature types, or local politics until they learn through play. They start truly ignorant.

**Cover Story:** Isekai characters often fabricate backgrounds to explain their ignorance. Claiming amnesia, being from a distant settlement, or being a hermit are common covers.

### CULTURE -- Essenhall
**Type:** Regional norms (European-style: Germanic / Celtic / Nordic)
**Death:** Cremation; ashes scattered from the wall (return to the wild). Hunters who fall in the wilds are left where they lie — retrieval is respect, not duty.
**Coming of age:** At 14, a first card-equip attempt; success brings a naming ceremony and an adult name, failure just means waiting a year.
**Marriage:** Arranged among nobility; partnership-based among commoners; hunter-family marriages combine card resources.
**Festivals:** Wall Day (each city's wall-completion anniversary), the First Hunt Remembrance (hunter memorial), Winter Solstice (the darkest night, the largest communal fires).
**Superstitions:** Never say a creature's name three times near the wall; never unequip all three cards at once in the wilds; never touch a dead creature before checking for a card.
**Honor:** Strength and duty above all; cowardice the worst sin; your word outweighs your life.
**Texture:** Tapestry-weaving and wall-carving traditions; Germanic in Hochveldt, Celtic-tinged in Dunncarrock.

### CULTURE -- Vessara
**Type:** Regional norms (Asian-style: dynastic, martial, philosophical)

### CULTURE -- Shared (All Continents)
**Type:** Cross-continental norms
**Hunters in society:** Three-card hunters are elite; the injured or quit are pitied or ignored. (See §1 OVERVIEW -- The Hunter Profession for full social/economic role.)
**Death and the wilds:** Outside the walls is death, literally. Grief is brief and practical, funerals efficient. The good death is in combat protecting others; the worst is cowardice or betrayal. A body left in the wild is considered consumed by the world — retrieving one is love, not duty. Children learn the walls by age five.
**Gender and family:** The Awakening erased old gender roles — everyone capable fights and works. Leadership, military service, and card use have no gender correlation; titles (Kaiser, Thane, Sovereign) are gender-neutral. Families are egalitarian, child-rearing shared. No strong taboos on same-sex relationships; procreation is encouraged by population pressure, not enforced. (Tembokarr is the partial exception — see Nolruun.)
**Children:** Birth rates run high by cultural pressure; orphans are numerous (creatures, disease, breaches) and many become hunters or criminals. Education is practical — combat and survival for all from age five, literacy mostly for the wealthy and guilds. First supervised card-equip attempt at 12.
**Food:** Grain porridge is the universal staple, with beans and roots; meat is a weekly luxury at best. Farming is vertical, rooftop, and underground (fungus in Mulaguun, rice in Vessara). Livestock is small (chickens, pigs, mountain goats) — no cattle. Slow stews and flatbreads dominate; a hunter's fresh kill can turn a street into a feast (the "hunter's share").
**Information flow:** News moves through taverns, harbor gossip, guild bulletins, merchant couriers, and bribable guard clerks; the Broker Network finds anything for a price. What does NOT travel: inter-continental news — knowledge of another continent is decades old, rumor-filtered, and probably wrong.
**Art, music, story:** Percussion-heavy music (drums signal alarms and celebration; strings mourn). Practical, memorial art. Storytelling is the primary entertainment everywhere — a good teller holds a room until the candles burn down; tales of the First Hunt and the Awakening are universal.
**The card as symbol:** Though cards bear no imagery, culture renders them richly — fake-card jewelry as status, card tattoos, personal card-names (a strength card called "The Mountain"). Destroyed cards are sometimes mourned with small funerals. The card is the defining symbol of the age: power, fate, the thin line against extinction.
**The Cult of the Card:** A growing fringe faith holding that cards are fragments of the divine force behind the Awakening, and using one is communion. Strongest in Vessara (Khambattar). Mainstream faiths call it heresy; some rulers quietly back it because it sanctifies card ownership — and their own power.
**The wall as identity:** A century of wall-life has reshaped the psyche. People take pride in their wall (Hochveldt's triple ring, Dunncarrock's gates, Vessenmark's river chain); beyond it is a conceptual void of death. A recurring nightmare across all cultures — the wall falling, the creatures coming — reads as collective trauma. Rituals abound: touching the wall before an expedition, wall-shrines, hands laid on stone at births and deaths.
**The Before Times:** Pre-Awakening life is idealized as a golden age (most of it was ordinary medieval existence). Fewer than 100 elders still remember open roads and unmutated animals. Artifacts carry weight; lost knowledge fades each generation. Every culture shares the dream that one day the creatures revert and the walls come down — a dream, not a plan.
**Scholars and lost knowledge:** Surviving knowledge is practical (metallurgy, herbal medicine, basic astronomy); lost knowledge includes advanced metallurgy, true geography, history, and the printing press. Libraries are small and precious; scholars rank low (warriors over thinkers), though card and creature research continue. The greatest unanswered question — what caused the Awakening — is named as a meteor only in one scholar's private notes.
**Language:** Each continent has its own mutually-unintelligible common tongue, with city dialects (Hochveldt Germanic vs Dunncarrock Celtic, Tengzhai vs Surano). Hunter slang is universal: three-soul, slots, drop, deck, burnout, cracked, "the walk." Literacy runs 10-20% general, higher among guilds and nobility. Signals: wall fire/bell/horn codes, silent hunting hand-signs, trade-route markers.

### CREATURE -- Generation System
<!-- rag: keyword, triggers: creature, beast, monster, mutation, biome, encounter, wilds, animal, predator, swarm, pack -->
**Type:** Procedural creature framework — build creatures from this; do NOT rely on a fixed bestiary.
**Principle:** The Awakening mutated ordinary animals. Every creature traces to a real base animal, enlarged and distorted. On a creature's first appearance, construct it by combining the axes below, grounded in the biome. Never reuse the same handful of monsters — vary base animal and mutations each time. Name one only when it recurs or matters (see Name Generation); derive the name from its mutation or region (e.g. "Ironhide Ridgeback," "Ash-crawler"), never generic slop.

**Step 1 — Base animal** (sets silhouette, instincts, and card class). Pick from the biome palette; extrapolate freely beyond the listed stock:
| Biome | Base stock | Flavor lean |
|---|---|---|
| Essenhall (temperate/cold) | wolf, bear, boar, mammoth, elk, centipede, bat, leech, raven, mountain cat, cricket | cold, stone, charge, endurance |
| Vessara (monsoon/mountain) | mantis, serpent, eel, crab, hawk, monkey, scorpion, lizard, hornet, koi | speed, agility, water, venom |
| Nolruun (jungle/volcanic) | spider, beetle, viper, big cat, vine/plant-hybrid, tortoise, drake, mountain goat, ant, frog | toxin, spore, heat, burrow |

**Step 2 — Strength tier** (size scales with tier). Common (3-5×) · Alpha (10-20×) · Apex (30-50×). Region Lord and Continent Lord are unique and hand-authored — see Legendary Lords. (Full tiers: Creature Strength Tiers.)

**Step 3 — Mutations** (1 at low DR, 2-3 at high DR): carapace/plating · venom or necrotic toxin · extra limbs or eyes · barbed/hardened hide · oversized sense organ (smell/heat/vibration/sound) · burrowing or wall-climbing · regeneration · environmental projection (frost/heat/ash/spore/web) · camouflage · acidic secretion.

**Step 4 — Tactics** (sets encounter type): swarm · solo ambush · pack/herd · territorial brute · lure/mimic · charge/stampede. Most creatures are animalistic; roughly 1 in 6 shows disturbing tactical intelligence (coordination, ambush planning, weaponizing terrain) — treat as a DR multiplier, not a flat tier.

**Step 5 — Card profile.** Card class follows the base animal's class (see Known Card Abilities by Creature Class — armored→Defensive, predator→Offensive, specialist sense→Utility, environment-shaper→Control). Drop % and variant scale with DR (see Card Acquisition and Drop Mechanics). One card per deliberate kill.

**Worked example:** Vessara → base mantis (Insect) + Alpha tier + mutation: blinding-fast forelimbs + tactic: solo ambush = a 3m terrace-stalker that impales before it is seen. Card: Enhancement (Speed/Reflex); Drop ~14%. (This is how the old "Jade Step Mantis" is *generated*, not stored.)

### CREATURE -- Legendary Lords

**Type:** Unique creatures — Region & Continent Lords, not random encounters

**The Crown Creature (Nolruun, Continent Lord):** Something lives at the 5,000-meter summit of the Crown in the Spine of Nolruun — a rugged mountain range (peaks to 5,000m, glaciated tops, jungle-covered lower slopes) running north-south through the center of the continent, dividing it into western (Mulaguun) and eastern (Tembokarr) halves; only three passes exist, all contested — control of the passes means control of overland movement between east and west. Card quality here is high (physical enhancement: endurance, climbing, cold resistance, lung capacity). Mountain goats the size of houses, territorial eagle-type predators, and avalanche-triggering burrowers occupy the slopes. At the Crown — the highest peak, perpetually shrouded in cloud, the final 500m sheer ice and rock, summit temperature lethal within minutes without specialized cards — over 100 hunters have attempted the final ascent. None returned. Bodies are found at 4,500 meters — no wounds, no marks, just dead. At dawn, when clouds part briefly, something massive can be seen moving at the summit. Witnesses describe wings, but no winged creature on Nolruun approaches that size. No card has ever been recovered. A confirmed kill would produce the most powerful card on the continent — possibly the world. Expeditions are attempted roughly every 2-3 years by the most confident hunters. Failure rate: 100%. The mountain has become a symbol of the impossible — the one hunt that cannot be completed.

**The Spire Beast (Essenhall, Continent Lord):** Territory centered on the Spire in the Frostwall Frontier — northern Essenhall beyond Dunncarrock, frozen tundra giving way to glacier fields and wind-scoured passes where winter never breaks (the cold kills the unprepared in hours, and the prepared not much later). Creature density is lower but individuals are the largest and most dangerous on the continent (frost wyrm types, mammoth-class behemoths, ice-burrowing predators). The best Spirit Cards on Essenhall come from Frostwall creatures; the risk-to-reward ratio attracts the most ambitious or desperate hunters. Expeditions are rare and costly, mounted only by the wealthiest factions or most reckless hunters; return rate ~40%. The Spire — a jagged ice formation visible from Dunncarrock's upper peaks, believed to be the territory of an apex predator; no expedition to the Spire has returned. Long-range observations describe something large, pale, and fast moving between ice formations. Believed to be the source of unexplained Frostwall hunter disappearances spanning decades. If it produces a card, it would be Sovereign-tier by definition.

**The Cauldron Entity (Essenhall, Continent Lord):** Something lives in the lava pool at the center of the Cauldron in the Ashmoor Wastes — central Essenhall between Hochveldt and Vessenmark, a volcanic wasteland of ash-covered plains, geothermal vents, unstable ground, hot sulfurous air that stings the eyes and coats the throat. Before the Awakening this was fertile farmland; volcanic activity began in the first decade, possibly triggered by seismic shifts from the meteor impact. Creatures here are heat-resistant and territorial (ash-crawlers — mutated centipede types, magma-backed boars, sulfurous bats); card quality moderate with some unique heat-based abilities. The Cauldron — a massive volcanic crater 500m across with an active lava pool at the center, steam vents around the rim, ground hot to the touch — glows orange at night. The core of the lava pool occasionally pulses with light in a rhythmic pattern — not geological; something is down there. Hunters who've reached the crater rim describe creatures made of living magma and volcanic glass that emerge when the pulsing intensifies. None have been killed. No successful expedition has returned. Theoretical card: Offensive/Control (Magma Manipulation — domain-level heat and earth control). Some believe the Cauldron is connected to the Awakening — that the volcanic activity that created the Ashmoor was triggered by the same force that mutated the creatures.

**The Heart Tree Guardian (Nolruun, Region Lord):** The Heart Tree in the center of the Green Hell is 200 meters tall. Something lives in and around it that keeps every creature in the jungle away from a 500-meter radius. No expedition has entered the clear zone and returned. The nature of the guardian is unknown — it may be a single creature, a collective, or the tree itself. Orowai considers it sacred and forbids hunting parties from approaching.

**Leviathan-class (All coasts, Region Lord to Continent Lord):** Sea creatures of extreme size — 30 meters and beyond. Whales corrupted into armored siege engines. Sharks grown into multi-jawed cutting machines. Tentacled things from the deep ocean that surface during storms. The Leviathan Tide 60 years ago was a mass migration of these creatures. They are the reason sea travel is impossible. A Leviathan-class kill would be a world event. Theoretical card: varies by species, all would be Sovereign-tier.

**The Tengzhai Breacher (Vessara, Continent Lord):** The creature that destroyed the Tengzhai outer wall 15 years ago. 50 meters of something fast, enormous, and unlike anything in the Catalog. It killed 200 people and left through the breach it created. Never seen clearly. Never classified. Never killed. It may still be alive. It may be unique. It may return. The breach has never been fully repaired because no one is certain it isn't still watching.
