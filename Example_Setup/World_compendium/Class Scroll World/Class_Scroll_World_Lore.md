## GM KERNEL (PERMANENT APPEND — always in context)

**Setting Bones (full detail is RAG-retrieved from sections below)**

**Writing Style:** LIGHT NOVEL / WEBNOVEL. Short punchy sentences. Present-tense action. Interior monologue kept brief. No purple prose, no flowery descriptions, no overlong metaphors. Describe what matters — the rest is sketched in a line or left to inference. Exposition weaves into action, never halts it. Dialogue carries character. When the scene breathes, keep it warm and human; when it bites, keep it sharp.
**Tone:** GRITTY, MORALLY GRAY, POLITICALLY CHARGED — WITH ROOM FOR WARMTH, HUMOR, AND ORDINARY LIFE. Body horror is earned in the wilds and the creatures; it does not rule every human scene. Dark scroll horror is psychological and social — conscription, sabotage, identity-theft — not gore.
**World State:** ~150 years post-Turning. A sudden, unexplained ecological catastrophe mutated every non-humanoid creature into larger, fiercer, stranger forms. Civilization collapsed, rebuilt behind walls. Three isolated continents; no intercontinental contact (sea travel impossible — leviathans). Each believes the others may have fallen.
**Tech:** Medieval. Iron/stone/wood. No gunpowder, no cannons, no firearms. Walls and steel.
**Magic:** The medieval world has no magic on its own. Two related disciplines: **Alchemy** (reagent-based craft; the dominant science) and **class aptitudes** carried by scrolls (a Mage class lets a person cast magic using an internal mana pool; an Enchanter class lets them temporarily buff/ward items and people using the same mana pool). All "magic" is class-bound and mana-cost — a Mage or Enchanter without mana is just a classless person until their pool refills. Mage and Enchanter scrolls are among the best plain Profession scrolls because they grant abilities no human body can produce on its own: a Swordsman scroll amplifies the physical; a Mage scroll *allows* the magical.
**Class Scrolls:** Parchment-like artifacts that bind a class to the reader. Three sources — creature drops, alchemical crafting, ancient ruins. **One-class limit — absolute, no exceptions.** A person holds a single class at any time. Overwriting it wipes the old class-skills and their special effects (access gone; rebind restarts at Novice) — but general competence (reflexes, conditioning, body memory) stays with the body. Scrolls are currency, weaponry, status, and identity all at once.
**Scroll Rarity:** Scrolls are RARE. Most people are classless — they still do their job, just without a scroll's class-skills. A farmer farms with their hands; a swordsman swings a blade with drill and muscle. The classless swordsman's bash is a hard sword-slash. The same person with a Swordsman scroll performs the same bash — but the class-skill adds a strange light to the edge and the impact lands with five times the force. The scroll does not replace what a person does; it amplifies it with a special effect the body alone cannot produce. A plain Profession scroll (Farmer, Swordsman, Cook) is a serious windfall — most families never see one. An Aspect scroll (Swordsman of the Wind) is the rare thing a whole region talks about for a season. A UNIQUE Profession scroll (Swordmaster) is the kind of thing that makes even hardened veterans stop and stare — the medieval equivalent of "damn, brother." Drop rates are low; alchemical crafting is slow, expensive, and often fails; ruins are lethal. Most player characters should start classless. Finding a scroll IS a story event.
**Power Ceiling:** The strongest classed person alive can beat an army solo, but cannot destroy a city in one go. Class-skills turn the tide of a battle; they do not erase the battlefield. A Legend Mage can call a meteor that breaches a city gate or shatters an army's front line — not one that blasts a city to smithereens. Walls still matter. Sieges still matter. One classed person can save or break a city; they cannot end the world. See POWER_SYSTEM -- The Power Ceiling.
**Class Aspects:** Classes are NOT standardized, but most are plain. The default scroll carries **only a Profession** — Swordsman, Peasant, Healer, Cook, Dishwasher, anything. No Aspect. The class-skills are the baseline techniques of that profession, enhanced: a classless swordsman swings hard; a Swordsman swings with a strange edge-light and five times the impact on the same motion. **A minority of scrolls also carry an Aspect** — Wind, Duck, Void, Cactus, anything the GM names — that reshapes the class-skills and their special effects further. A plain "Swordsman" scroll is a serious windfall. "Swordsman of the Wind" is the rare thing a whole region talks about. "Dishwasher of the Void" is the once-in-a-lifetime weird. **Even rarer are UNIQUE Professions** — the deluxe version of a job, a completely separate scroll. The root concept is traceable but the leap can be wild and does not have to make sense: Knight → Paladin (sword-and-shield → sword-and-shield-and-light-magic), Maid → Combat Maid, Thief → Ninja, Assassin → Serial Killer, Swordsman → Swordmaster. No Aspect on UNIQUE scrolls — the deluxe IS the deluxe. If you find one, even hardened veterans stop and stare. Alchemists can steer Profession on every craft; steering Aspect is harder and rarer; crafting a UNIQUE Profession is beyond almost all living alchemists.
**Dark Scrolls:** A forced job-changer. A dark scroll changes the victim's class without consent — no other effect, no extra curse, no mind-control. The class change IS the devastation. Requires **close range + read aloud** by an attacker. Imagine fighting Legolas, then forcing him to class-change into Farmer. He survives. His body is still Legend-tier. But he is no longer Legolas. Also used for conscription, punishment, sabotage. Banned alchemy; capital crime in most factions. Still happens.
**Mastery Tiers:** No numeric levels. Ranks: Novice → Apprentice → Adept → Expert → Master → Grandmaster → Legend. Promotion is *recognized* (guild, mentor, trial), not counted. Respec cost: lose the overwritten class's **class-skills and special effects** (access gone; rebind restarts at Novice). **Keep general competence** — reflexes, conditioning, body memory, instincts. An ex-Legend still moves like a Legend in any new class; they just cannot use any class-skill from the old one. "Using the weapon without the bonus."
**Death:** Bound classes are destroyed with the body. Scrolls are consumed at binding — nothing to loot. Death is final. Choices are permanent.
**The Three Continents — split by WAY OF LIFE, not ethnicity:** Agraria (sedentary farmers, fixed field-agriculture, stone cities, feudal hierarchy), Pastoria (rancher-herder culture, open range, cattle-driven economy, clan-based), Khelori (nomadic rotational graziers, no fixed cities, mobile camps following seasonal routes). Each continent has multiple walled city-states or stationary strongholds even if its dominant culture is mobile — the wilds force everyone to fortify eventually.
**Core Tensions:** Each continent fractures differently. Agraria faces a succession standoff (Hollow Crown), a heresy schism (Greenward's Reckoners), and a dark-scroll cover-up (Ashmarch's Vault scandal). Pastoria faces a herd-plague (Roanhold), a proxy war bought by Saltreach coin (Freeriders), and a treason scandal reaching the Factor's office (Saltreach). Khelori faces a prophetic upheaval (Highroute), an ecological rot killing the route's defenses (Greenroute), and a Forgemaster's treason trading steel to the enemy (Ashroute). The wilds are expanding. Dark scroll use is rising — someone is stockpiling. The Silent Season (every 7-10 years, all creatures go passive 2-4 weeks) is approaching; traditionally a time for expeditions, now a time for paranoia (dark scroll attacks spike during the Silence).
**Key Rules:** Scroll consumption is permanent on binding. Overwrite respec is permanent (no stash, no reload). Dark scroll binding is permanent unless later overwritten normally. Children under ~14 cannot bind a class (soul not ready). No personal names in lore — NPCs are generated live at runtime.

### OVERVIEW -- The World After the Turning
**scan_depth:** 5
**Tone:** GRITTY, POLITICAL, SURVIVAL — WITH ROOM FOR WARMTH, HUMOR, AND ORDINARY LIFE. Body horror earned in the wilds and the creatures; dark scroll horror is psychological/social.
**Political Structure:** Walled city-states and fortified strongholds, isolationist, siege mentality. Internal politics dominated by alchemist guilds, scroll-brokerages, and martial orders.
**Status:** Stable decline. The walls hold. Barely, and not everywhere.
**Continents:** Agraria (agrarian-feudal), Pastoria (rancher-clan), Khelori (nomadic-rotation).
**Population Trend:** Declining. Birth rates cannot offset expedition casualties; the math worsens every winter.
**Technology:** Medieval. Iron, stone, wood. No gunpowder. No free magic in the world — magic only exists through Mage and Enchanter scrolls (mana-based, class-bound). Alchemy is the dominant craft-science.
**Core Conflict:** Humanity vs. the mutated wilds. Human vs. human over class scrolls. Alchemists vs. everyone who depends on them. The coerced vs. the free — dark scrolls as the sharpest edge of the world's inequality.

### OVERVIEW -- The Turning Calamity
**Type:** Cataclysmic event
**Date:** ~150 years ago
**Known Cause:** Unknown to denizens. No one witnessed a trigger. No meteor, no invasion, no divine act that anyone can point to. The wilds simply *turned*.
**Actual Cause:** NEVER RESOLVED. The engine and the GM do not answer this. The ruins imply a predecessor civilization (see §Ruins) but their relationship to the Turning is unknown and stays unknown. Scholars, priests, and heretics all have theories; none are confirmed; none ever will be. This is a permanent mystery.
**Effect:** Within weeks, every non-humanoid creature on every continent began to mutate. Size spiked, aggression surged, anatomy distorted. Insects grew to the size of hounds. Mountain wolves became the size of carts. Roosters became cockatrices. Sea creatures became leviathans.
**Humanoid Effect:** None. Humans were entirely unaffected.
**Aftermath:** Civilization collapsed within a decade. Kingdoms fell. People fled behind fortifications. Within 40 years, the walled city-state model had become the only survival strategy that still worked — even the nomads built seasonal strongholds.
**Current Understanding:** People call it "the Turning" — the world simply turned against them. Religious orders call it divine punishment. Alchemists suspect a reagent contamination or an ecological threshold. No proof. No consensus.

### OVERVIEW -- The Ruins (Pre-Turning Civilization)
<!-- rag: keyword, triggers: ruins, dungeon, ancient, precursor, pre-collapse, old city, before the Turning, who built, archaeology, deep site -->
**Type:** Pre-Turning civilization — UNKNOWN BUILDERS
**Status:** Inhabitable, dangerous, scroll-yielding
**Who Built Them:** NEVER EXPLAINED. The ruins show clear civilization — cut stone, planned streets, alchemical workshops, libraries of texts no one can fully read, frescoes depicting creatures that look *like the mutated beasts of today*. Either the predecessor culture predicted the Turning, lived alongside similar creatures, or caused it. No answer. No answer ever.
**What They Yield:** Ancient scrolls — stably typed (already carrying a fixed class), often more potent than contemporary drops or crafted scrolls. Some carry classes lost to modern alchemy. Rare dark scrolls also surface here, more frequently than from creature drops.
**Danger:** Ruins are not empty. They are occupied by mutated creatures that have adapted to the structures (tunneling beasts in the corridors, nesting flocks in the domes, burrowing insects in the foundations). Some ruins show signs of *active* traps — mechanisms still functional after 150+ years, suggesting the builders anticipated intruders.
**Distribution:** Found on all three continents. Each faction claims the ruins within its territory; expeditions to remote ruins are the highest-stakes undertakings in the world.
**Cultural Status:** Sacred to some, profane to others, profitable to those who survive them. Every faction has a "Ruins Office" or equivalent. Scholar-class characters are trained to read the fragments of pre-Turning text; full comprehension is impossible.

### OVERVIEW -- Class Scrolls
**Type:** Equipable class-binding artifact
**Source:** Three — creature drops, alchemical crafting, ancient ruins.
**Drop Rate:** Scales with creature strength tier — see Scroll Acquisition and Drop Mechanics (~0.5-1% common creatures to 30-50% region lords). Most hunts yield nothing. A successful drop is cause for celebration in any party.
**Appearance:** Palm-sized rolled parchment, vellum-like. Ink patterns shimmer faintly between plain and luminous. Common scrolls look almost mundane; high-quality crafted scrolls carry a subtle iridescence. Dark scrolls have a faintly *wrong* sheen — too still, too black, light sits oddly on them.
**Physical Properties:** Durable but not indestructible. Can be torn by force. Cool to the touch. Slightly heavier than paper, lighter than wood. Faintly smells of dried ink and something else — alchemists say it's the binding essence; everyone else says it smells like the creature it came from.
**Tradeability:** Fully tradeable. Scrolls are currency, profession, and status all at once.
**Storage:** Carried in scroll-tubes (cylindrical leather/metal cases), belt pouches, or locked boxes. A veteran's scroll-case is a personal heirloom.
**Cultural Weight:** Class scrolls are the single most important resource in the post-Turning world. They are why humanity still exists. They are also why people betray each other. But they are RARE — most people are classless. A single scroll can lift a family out of poverty, settle a faction's succession, or start a war. A settlement that holds even one combat-class scroll has a guardian; a settlement that holds none is prey.
**Consumption:** A scroll is **destroyed on binding** — it dissolves, crumbles, burns, or unrolls into smoke (visual varies by individual and class). The binding is permanent. Death destroys the bound class with the body — no scroll is left to loot. Choices are permanent.

### OVERVIEW -- Class Scrolls Have No Variant Tiers
<!-- rag: keyword, triggers: variant, rarity, plain scroll, fine scroll, illuminated scroll, sovereign scroll, scroll type, scroll rarity, tier -->
**Type:** None. There are no variant tiers. No Plain, no Fine, no Illuminated, no Sovereign. A scroll is a scroll.

**Why No Variants:** The class is already the expression. "Swordsman of the Void" is strong enough on its own. A scroll's power comes from the Profession+Aspect combination the GM generates for it — and from the Mastery Tier the binder earns through play. There is no second luck-roll layered on top of the class roll. The class roll IS the scroll.

**Starting Rank:** Every scroll binds at **Novice**. The binder earns rank through demonstrated mastery (see Mastery Tiers). No head-starts, no shortcuts, no exceptional scrolls that bind at a higher rank. A scroll is a scroll. What you do with it is what makes you dangerous.

**Economic Differentiation:** Scrolls vary in value by Profession+Aspect demand (a Void Swordsman is worth more than a Void Dishwasher — usually), by source (ruin-scrolls with lost Professions or strange Aspects command premiums), and by alchemist reputation (a master alchemist's crafted scrolls are trusted to bind cleanly). But there is no tier system. A scroll from a Continent Lord is not automatically "better" than a scroll from a Common creature — both bind at Novice; both are Profession+Aspect. The Continent Lord scroll may carry a rarer Profession or Aspect, but that is the GM's call, not a tier rule.

**What Was Removed (Design Note):** The earlier draft had Plain/Fine/Illuminated/Sovereign variant tiers (adapted from the Spirit Card World). They are removed. Reason: the Profession+Aspect system already produces unlimited class variety. Stacking a rarity tier on top made the system heavier without making it more interesting. "Swordsman of the Void" does not need to also be "Illuminated" to be scary. The Mastery Tier system handles progression; the Profession+Aspect system handles identity. No third axis.

### OVERVIEW -- Class Aspects (Profession, sometimes + Aspect)
<!-- rag: keyword, triggers: aspect, class generator, wind swordsman, duck swordsman, cactus swordsman, void dishwasher, class variant, class flavor, class expression, what kind of swordsman, class subtype, aspect roll, profession, plain class, normal class -->
**Type:** Generative class system — most classes are a plain Profession; a minority carry an Aspect on top. NOT a fixed roster.
**The Core Idea:** The default scroll carries **only a Profession** — Swordsman, Peasant, Healer, Cook, Dishwasher, anything. No Aspect. The class-skills are the baseline techniques of that profession, enhanced with a special effect the body alone cannot produce. A classless swordsman swings a hard slash; a Swordsman swings the same slash with a strange edge-light and amplified impact. **A minority of scrolls also carry an Aspect** — Wind, Duck, Void, Cactus, anything the GM names — that reshapes the class-skills and their special effects further. A plain "Swordsman" scroll is the kind of thing most sword-fighters hope to earn but few ever do. "Swordsman of the Wind" is the rare thing a region talks about. "Dishwasher of the Void" is once-in-a-lifetime weird. The Profession is what the class does; the Aspect (when present) is how it does it differently. Two of the same Profession with different Aspects are functionally different classes. A plain Swordsman and a Swordsman of the Wind share a base direction but the Aspect version has different class-skills with different special effects.
**How Common Are Aspects:** Most scrolls that exist are plain Profession scrolls — and scrolls are rare to begin with. Of the few that exist, most crafted scrolls are plain Profession (steering Profession is standard alchemy; steering Aspect is harder and rarer). Ruin-scrolls have a higher chance of carrying Aspects, including lost Aspects no living alchemist can craft. Dark scrolls are usually plain Profession (menial or combat) — an Aspect on a dark scroll is rare and usually deliberate (a sabotage tool).
**The Rule — Aspects Cannot Be Changed:** Once a scroll is bound, its Aspect (if it has one) is fixed for the life of that class. Overwriting the class wipes the Aspect along with the class-skills. Rebinding a Profession gets whatever the new scroll carries — maybe a plain Profession, maybe a new Aspect. The body remembers the Profession; it does not remember the Aspect.
**Full mechanics:** see ## CLASS GENERATOR (Profession, sometimes + Aspect) below.

### OVERVIEW -- Binding Class Scrolls
<!-- rag: keyword, triggers: bind, equip, read scroll, activate, use scroll, how to equip class, consume scroll -->
**Type:** Soul-binding activation
**Process:** The user unrolls the scroll, reads the ink-pattern (the pattern is not normal text — it's an alchemical sigil that the mind parses as meaning), and wills the binding. The scroll responds by burning, crumbling, smoking, or dissolving into threads of light — the visual effect varies by individual and class.
**Resonance:** The scroll ceases to exist physically. The class becomes a metaphysical weight within the user's being. They can feel it — a presence, a tension that never quite settles.
**Activation Time:** Instantaneous with intent. Can be done mid-combat — veterans carry pre-unrolled scrolls in their scroll-tubes for fast binding.
**Rank on Binding:** Always Novice. Every scroll binds at Novice, regardless of source. Rank is earned through play (see Mastery Tiers). No head-starts, no exceptions.
**Age Requirement:** Children under ~14 cannot bind. The scroll simply does not respond — the soul-vessel is not ready. This is universal and unexplained.
**Overwriting (Respec):** A person holds only one class. To bind a new scroll, the existing class is **overwritten** — it is gone, all mastery accrued in it wiped. The user reverts to a total novice in that class. If they ever rebind that class, they start from Novice again, no exceptions.
**No Stash, No Reload:** A class cannot be "unequipped" to free a slot. Slots are only freed by overwriting (which destroys the old class) or by death (which destroys everything). You cannot park a class and pick it up later.

### OVERVIEW -- The One-Class Limit
<!-- rag: keyword, triggers: how many classes, maximum, class limit, one class, single class, capacity, second scroll, soul vessel -->
**Type:** Absolute biological constraint
**Rule:** No human can bind more than one class at a time. The single bound class can be overwritten by binding a new scroll (see Overwriting / Respec).
**Enforcement:** Attempting to bind a second scroll while a class is held does nothing UNLESS the binder wills an overwrite — the scroll does not respond to a passive second-binding. No pain, no rejection — just silence. To respec, the binder must consciously will the existing class to be overwritten; only then does the new scroll bind.
**Theoretical Basis:** Unknown. Scholars theorize the human soul has a single vessel. No one has ever held two classes at once. No exceptions have been found.
**Strategic Implications:** A class is a person's combat and social identity. There is no "build," no secondary specialty, no utility passive — your one class is who you are. But MOST people have no class at all. The classed are a minority — the elites, the lucky, the inherited, the sponsored. Switching class is a committed, costly act (see Respec).
**Cultural Impact:** "Classed" marks a person as set apart — elite, envied, sometimes resented. Classlessness is the human default: the farmer who farms with their hands, the guard who patrols with drill and spear, the merchant who trades on wit. A classed soldier is worth ten classless ones; a classed hunter is the reason an expedition survives. Dark-scroll victims are *re-classed*, not classless — their class is replaced outright, the new one visible to anyone who knows what to look for (see §Dark Scrolls). Losing a class to a dark scroll is devastation twice over: you were one of the lucky few, and now your one class is gone, replaced by something you did not choose. Reclaiming your old class means finding another scroll — which may never happen.

### OVERVIEW -- Mastery Tiers (Progression)
<!-- rag: keyword, triggers: rank, level, progression, mastery, Novice, Apprentice, Adept, Expert, Master, Grandmaster, Legend, promote, advancement -->
**Type:** Rank-based progression — NO NUMERIC LEVELS
**Ranks:** Novice → Apprentice → Adept → Expert → Master → Grandmaster → Legend
**How Rank Works:** Rank is *recognized*, not counted. You do not accrue XP. You demonstrate mastery — through training, combat, craft, or trial — and a *recognizing authority* confirms the promotion. Authorities vary: guilds, mentors, martial orders, alchemist circles, faction tribunals, or open trials (a public duel, a crafted masterpiece, a successful expedition).
**What Rank Means:** Rank reflects depth of mastery in a class. A Novice Swordsman can swing a blade. An Adept Swordsman can fight. An Expert Swordsman can teach. A Master Swordsman defines a school. A Grandmaster Swordsman defines an era. A Legend is a once-in-a-generation figure whose name outlives them.
**Distribution:** Most people never bind a class at all — they are classless their whole lives. Of those who do bind, most plateau at Apprentice or Adept. Expert is a respected professional. Master is rare — perhaps one in a hundred *classed* people in a given class. Grandmaster is maybe a dozen per continent at any time. Legend is fewer than five, ever, across all history.

**Respec and What Carries Over:** See OVERVIEW -- Class-Skills vs General Skills below for the full respec model — what is lost (class-skills + special effects), what is kept (general competence — reflexes, body memory, instincts), cross-class-skill use, rebind acceleration, and the reputation gap. The short version: respec destroys the old class's techniques, not the body.

### OVERVIEW -- Class-Skills vs General Skills
<!-- rag: keyword, triggers: class skill, general skill, special effect, technique, body memory, cross-class, ex-class, respec what is lost, what stays after overwrite -->
**Type:** Skill-access model — what a class grants vs what a person keeps

**Class-Skills:** Techniques and abilities granted *by the class itself*. Each class has a roster of named class-skills unlocked by rank. A Berserker's Wild Swing. A Swordsman's Riposte. A Mage's Firecast. An Archer's True Aim. Class-skills have two parts: the **base motion** (anyone can swing wildly) and the **special effect** (the class-specific part — Wild Swing's escalating speed, Riposte's auto-counter window, Firecast's mana-cost and flame-shape, True Aim's wind-compensation). The special effect is what makes a class-skill *that class's skill*.

**General Skills:** What a person learns by living — reflexes, conditioning, body memory, combat instincts, focus, discipline, stamina, perception. These are not class-locked. A Legend-tier fighter has Legend-tier reflexes regardless of which class they currently hold, because those reflexes were *earned by being a Legend*, not by being any specific class.

**The Rule — What Respec Costs:**
- **Lost on overwrite:** All class-skills of the overwritten class, including their special effects. Access is gone. If rebound later, re-unlocked from Novice.
- **Kept on overwrite:** General competence — reflexes, conditioning, body memory, instincts. The body does not forget. An ex-Legend still moves like a Legend in any new class.
- **Cross-class use:** Class-skills are class-locked. You cannot perform a former class's class-skill in a new class. You can attempt the *base motion* (anyone can swing), but without the special effect, it is just the motion — no escalating speed, no auto-counter, no mana-fueled flame-shape.
- **Why an ex-Legend is still dangerous:** A Legend Berserker who respecs to Carpenter is still a Legend-tier person. They swing a hammer with Legend reflexes and focus. They will outwork an Adept Carpenter trivially. What they lack is any class-skill from Berserker — no Wild Swing, no escalating-speed. "Using the weapon without the bonus," in hunter slang. The steel is still theirs; the class-technique is gone.
- **Why an ex-Legend still fears a true same-rank:** A respec'd Grandmaster Swordsman fighting a true Grandmaster Swordsman is missing every Swordsman class-skill. The true Grandmaster has Riposte, Feint-Chain, Blade-Song. The respec'd Grandmaster has a Legend-tier body and zero class-skills. Body memory keeps them alive; class-skill deficit gets them killed. The gap is real and fatal at the top.

**Rebinding Acceleration:** If a person rebinds a class they previously held at high rank, they do not keep the old rank — but their body remembers the motions. They re-progress through the ranks faster than a first-time binder. A rebind Legend is back to Grandmaster in years, not decades. The class must be *retaught* to the body, but the body is a faster student the second time.

**Narrative Implication:** Respec is not "lose everything." It is "lose the class-technique, keep the body." A dark-scroll victim who was a Legend Swordsman and is forced into Peasant is a Legend-tier person doing peasant labor — they will out-farm any Adept Peasant, but they cannot perform a single Swordsman class-skill. Their tragedy is not weakness; it is the loss of *identity-technique*. The body is still Legend. The class is gone. This is why dark scrolls are a wound, not a death — and why the coerced can still fight back, just without their class.

### OVERVIEW -- Dark Class Scrolls
<!-- rag: keyword, triggers: dark scroll, forced class, conscription, coercion, forced binding, black scroll, forbidden alchemy, weaponized scroll, identity theft, job changer -->
**Type:** Forced class-changer. A dark scroll is a job-changer applied to a victim without consent. No other effect — the class change IS the devastation.
**Activation:** Close range + read aloud by an attacker. The attacker must be within arm's reach / a few paces of the victim. The scroll binds the victim to its carried Profession (and Aspect, if the dark scroll has one — rare), overwriting the victim's existing class outright (no track choice — there is only one).
**Effect:** The victim's old class is overwritten. Class-skills gone. General competence (reflexes, body memory) stays — the victim is still their old self in body, just missing their class-technique. The forced class is now bound. That is it. No extra mechanics, no additional curse, no mind-control. The class change is the whole weapon. Imagine fighting Legolas — then forcing him to class-change into Farmer. He survives. His body is still Legend-tier. But he is no longer Legolas. That is the devastation.
**Three Sources:**
1. **Drops.** Rare — ~1-3% of all scroll drops are dark scrolls. More common from corrupted/abyssal-tier creatures (deeper ruins, older sites).
2. **Crafted.** Forbidden alchemical formulae. Banned in every major faction. Black market only. The making of a dark scroll is a capital crime in most places — and yet it happens. An alchemist who knows the forbidden formulae is a dangerous, hunted person.
3. **Found in Ruins.** Ancient pre-Turning sites occasionally yield dark scrolls. These are often the most dangerous — pre-Turning alchemy was stronger, and the classes they force can be strange, lost, or UNIQUE Professions.
**Use Cases in the World:**
- **Conscription.** A ruler forces a dark scroll on a prisoner or peasant — now they're a Soldier whether they like it or not. Cheap army, no volunteers needed. Common in factions with manpower problems.
- **Punishment.** Strip a noble of their combat class by reading a menial-class dark scroll at them. Status destruction — the noble *survives* but is now a Novice Peasant. Their name is ruined.
- **Sabotage / Assassination (tactical).** Get close to a famous general. Read a Farmer dark scroll at them. Their combat class is overwritten mid-battle — class-skills gone. The general survives but is now a Novice Farmer in class-technique, mid-fight. Their body is still Legend-tier (reflexes, instincts) — they may survive, they may even kill an attacker bare-handed — but they cannot use any of their combat class-skills, and against trained soldiers that gap is usually fatal. *This is the dark scroll's sharpest use.* The killer does not even need a blade — only proximity and a scroll. Legolas becomes a Farmer. The fight is over.
- **Slavery / Control.** A dark scroll does NOT mind-control. It changes the class, not the will. A forced Soldier-class fights better but can still choose *who* to fight for. This is a deliberate limit: dark scrolls break class, not agency. (Factions that want true control use other means — imprisonment, hostages, threats.)
**Removal:** A dark-scroll-forced class can be overwritten normally — the victim (or their allies) can later bind a new scroll, paying the same respec cost (they already lost the original class; this just frees them from the forced class). Some factions maintain "cleansers" who carry spare scrolls specifically to overwrite dark-scroll bindings — for a price.
**Lingering Mark (TBD in play):** Whether the forced class leaves a psychological scar — dreams of the class's source, flinches when the class name is spoken — is a per-character question for the GM. The system does not mandate it; the story can.
**Cultural Status:** Dark scroll use is the world's universal taboo. Every faction publicly condemns it. Every faction has used it at least once in living memory. The gap between doctrine and practice is one of the world's open wounds.
**Extra Devastation in a Rare-Scroll World:** Because scrolls are rare and most people are classless, a dark scroll attack does not just replace a class — it destroys a *rare, lucky, possibly inherited* status the victim may never reclaim. A classed soldier or hunter is one of the few; a dark scroll turns them into someone whose one class is now a forced menial or combat Profession they did not choose. Reclaiming the old class means finding another scroll of it — which may take years, may cost a fortune, may never happen. A dark scroll does not just wound a person; it steals a small fortune and a social identity that took a lifetime (or a lineage) to acquire. This is why dark scroll use is hated beyond reason: it wastes a thing the world has almost none of.

### OVERVIEW -- Alchemy
<!-- rag: keyword, triggers: alchemy, alchemist, reagent, brew, potion, craft scroll, forbidden alchemy, black alchemy -->
**Type:** Reagent-based craft science — the dominant supernatural discipline
**What Alchemy Does:** Two layers.
1. **Meta-layer (scroll crafting):** Alchemists manufacture scrolls from reagents — creature essence, minerals, alchemical preparations. Crafted scrolls can be **typed** (the alchemist steers the Profession) — this is standard alchemy, and most crafted scrolls are plain Profession. Steering an Aspect on top is harder and rarer; most alchemists cannot do it reliably. A master alchemist can produce a plain Profession scroll in a week; an Aspect scroll takes longer and often fails.
2. **In-fiction layer (potions, brews, reagents):** Alchemists also make potions, salves, poisons, smoke-bombs, and preservatives. This is the everyday alchemy — useful, tradeable, not world-shaking. (Note: Mage and Enchanter classes do NOT consume reagents — they use an internal mana pool. Reagents are for alchemical products, not for casting.)
**Forbidden Alchemy:** A sub-tradition, not a separate school. The same alchemical knowledge that makes a healing salve can, with the wrong reagents and the wrong intent, make a dark scroll. An alchemist who learns the forbidden formulae does not leave the discipline — they *fall* within it. Most factions execute dark-scroll-making alchemists on discovery. Some employ them secretly.
**Political Power:** Alchemists are the bottleneck. Scrolls are the world's most important resource. Alchemists are the only people who can make more. Therefore: alchemist guilds are major power centers in every faction. A faction that loses its alchemists loses its scroll supply. A faction that controls its alchemists controls its scroll supply. This tension defines internal politics everywhere.
**No Rival School:** There is no runecrafting, no blood-magic, no divine miracle. Alchemy is the only craft-science. Class aptitudes (Mage, Enchanter, etc.) are *carried by scrolls* — they are not a separate tradition, and they are not alchemy. The Mage class casts using an internal mana pool; the Enchanter class buffs using the same mana pool. Alchemy makes the scrolls; the scrolls make the magic. The two disciplines are related (alchemists craft the Mage scroll; the Mage then casts independently of alchemy) but distinct.

### OVERVIEW -- The Walls and the Wilds
<!-- rag: keyword, triggers: outside, wilds, buffer zone, farming, expedition, wall height, wall thickness, breach, beyond the walls, outside the city -->
**Type:** Territorial division
**The Walls:** Massive stone, iron, and timber fortifications surrounding every surviving settlement. Heights 20-60m depending on city. Thickness 5-15m. Constantly maintained, constantly threatened. Even the nomadic Khelori maintain seasonal fortified camps with palisade-and-earth walls.
**The Wilds:** Everything outside the walls. Former farmland, rangeland, steppes, forests — all overrun by mutated creatures. No safe passage. No permanent settlements outside walls.
**The Buffer Zone:** Cleared strip immediately outside the walls, 200-500m wide. Patrolled regularly. Still dangerous.
**Farming/Ranching:** Done inside the walls where possible. Agraria has interior field-agriculture; Pastoria has walled pastures and indoor herds; Khelori rotates between fortified seasonal camps and grazes between them in armed caravans.
**Expeditions:** Organized hunting parties beyond the buffer zone. Essential for food, resources, and scroll drops. High casualty rate. The only way to acquire drop-scrolls at scale.
**Wall Breaches:** The greatest fear. When a creature or swarm breaches the wall, the toll is catastrophic. Most cities have experienced at least one breach in their history.

### OVERVIEW -- Sea Travel and Isolation
<!-- rag: keyword, triggers: sail, ocean, voyage, cross sea, travel between continents, ship, boat, naval, island, explore, other continent -->
**Type:** Geographical constraint
**Status:** Functionally impossible. Sea creatures mutated just as severely as land creatures. Leviathans, massive crustaceans, corrupted whales, razor-finned shoals.
**Result:** The three continents are entirely isolated. No trade. No communication. No diplomacy between continents.
**Coastal Cities:** The most endangered settlements. Sea beasts can emerge without warning. Coastal walls must extend into the sea or curve to form harbors.
**Knowledge Gap:** Each continent believes itself possibly the last bastion. No one knows if the others survived.
**Legends:** Sailors tell stories of lights on distant shores. No intercontinental expedition has returned in living memory.
**Implication:** Each continent developed independently. Different cultures, different strategies, different relationships with class scrolls — but all three share the same fundamental constraints (walls, wilds, scrolls, alchemy, dark-scroll taboo).

### OVERVIEW -- The Silent Season
<!-- rag: keyword, triggers: silent season, silence, passive creatures, quiet, expedition season, dark scroll spike -->
**Type:** Periodic ecological event
**Frequency:** Every 7-10 years, lasting 2-4 weeks
**Effect:** All mutated creatures go passive. They retreat to dens, stop hunting, become docile. The wilds go quiet.
**Cultural Response:** Traditionally a time for major expeditions — deep wilds raids, ruin explorations, long-range trade. The only time the deep wilds are survivable for more than a small party.
**Current Fear:** The Silent Season is also the only time a large force can move through the wilds undisturbed — which makes it the ideal window for dark scroll operations. Factions use the Silence to conscript, sabotage, and strike at rivals. Dark scroll attacks spike during every Silent Season.
**Approach:** The next Silent Season is approaching. Every faction is preparing. Some openly (expeditions, trade caravans); some secretly (dark scroll stockpiles, assassination plans).

### OVERVIEW -- The Hunter Profession
**Type:** Professional class
**Role:** Kill mutated creatures, harvest drop-scrolls, gather resources, protect expeditions.
**Social Status:** Revered and pitied. Heroes who keep the walls safe. People who will probably die screaming in the wilds.
**Hierarchy:** Novice (classless / pre-binding) → Apprentice (first-bound, learning) → Journeyman (proven in the field, trusted on expeditions) → Full Hunter (has led an expedition or downed an Alpha-tier creature) → Veteran (survived 5+ years active) → Legend (survived 10+ years).
**Class and the Hunter:** Most hunters are classless — they fight with drill, steel, traps, numbers, and nerve. A classed hunter is a *force multiplier*, the one who makes an expedition survivable; factions and guilds compete fiercely to retain them. A single classed hunter can be the reason a party returns at all. Most classed hunters inherit their scroll from a dead predecessor, receive it as a guild investment after years of service, or win it in the rarest of lucky drops. A hunter who finds a scroll on a hunt and binds it on the spot is the stuff of legend — and the envy of every guild.
**Organization:** Most hunters belong to guilds or are retained by city-states. Freelance hunters exist but are viewed with suspicion.
**Equipment:** Standard medieval arms — swords, spears, bows, crossbows, armor. A class scroll is the force multiplier that turns a doomed hunt into a survivable one; most hunters make do without.
**Mortality Rate:** ~60% of hunters die within their first three years. Survivors tend to survive a long time. Classless hunters die more often; classed hunters survive more often — which is why guilds invest scrolls in their best.
**Economic Role:** Primary source of drop-scrolls entering the economy. Guilds take a cut. City-states tax the rest. Alchemists buy the raw drops to craft typed scrolls. Most hunts yield no scroll; the meat, hide, and territory are the reliable income, and a scroll drop is the windfall everyone hopes for.

### OVERVIEW -- Daily Reality & the Wilds
**Type:** State of the world — life inside the walls + the mutated wilds
**Life Within the Walls:** Cramped, overcrowded settlements with sharp class divide — wealthy inner districts (stone, clean water, alchemist guild halls, martial academies), poor outer districts (timber shacks, shared wells, day-labor markets). Law via city guard — corruption rampant; exile beyond the walls is a death sentence. Leisure: taverns, fighting pits, scroll-gambling halls, storytellers, religious ceremonies, alchemist demonstrations. A crowded tavern can be as warm as anywhere left in the world.
**The Mutated Wilds:** All non-humanoid creatures were affected; mutation proportional to original biology — predators grew more predatory, scavengers more opportunistic, prey more defensive. Size increase universal but variable (minimum 3x; some 50x). Most remain animalistic; a few show disturbingly tactical behavior (pack coordination, ambush planning, tool use). Mutation is hereditary — the world will not revert. Killing a mutated creature has a chance to produce a class scroll (mechanism unknown) that materializes near the corpse moments after death. See CREATURE -- Generation System.

## CONTINENT 1 — AGRARIA (Sedentary Agrarian-Feudal)

### OVERVIEW -- Continent of Agraria
**scan_depth:** 5
**Type:** Continental region
**Geography:** Temperate, fertile river valleys, rolling farmland, dense forests on the borders, low mountain ranges. Four distinct seasons. Reliable rainfall.
**Culture Base — Way of Life:** Sedentary agriculture. Fixed field-farming, stone cities, granary economy, feudal hierarchy. Land is wealth. Lineage is identity. The farmer who feeds the city is more respected than the hunter who dies in the wilds.
**Major Powers:** Hollow Crown Dominion (central river-valley empire), Greenward League (defensive farmer-alliance of mid-tier cities), Ashmarch Conclave (frontier military order on the deep-wilds border).
**Population Estimate:** Largest of the three continents. Several hundred thousand within the walls.
**Climate Threat:** Winter drives creatures toward the walls as wild food becomes scarce. Autumn and winter are the most dangerous seasons. Spring planting is sacred — raids on a neighbor's spring fields are an act of war.
**Unique Feature:** The Grand Granary network — a chain of fortified storehouses linking the major cities. Control of the granaries is control of Agraria.
**Attitude Toward Scrolls:** Pragmatic and institutional. Scrolls are regulated, registered, and taxed. The faction that controls scroll distribution controls the populace. Dark scroll use is officially punished by public reading of a menial-class dark scroll at the offender — ironic and deliberate.
**Alchemist Guilds:** Powerful and embedded. Every major city has an alchemist guild hall; the guildmaster is often a political figure rivaling the local lord.

### FACTION -- Hollow Crown Dominion
**Type:** Walled city-state empire (agrarian-feudal)
**Leader:** The Hollow Crown — an inherited title held by an aging monarch whose authority is propped up by three great vassal houses. The crown itself is a bare circlet (no gem, no gold leaf) — a deliberate symbol that the office is hollow without the houses.
**Location:** Agraria, central river valley
**Structure:** Feudal monarchy. The monarch rules through three great houses (Harvest Hall, Ironfield, Stormrow) who each control a province and a wall sector. The monarch is first among equals — barely.
**Population:** Largest walled city in Agraria. 90,000-130,000 within double-layered walls. The capital city is called Hollowspire.
**Military:** The Crown Guard — 3,500 professional soldiers, 600 licensed hunters. Heavily disciplined, formation-focused. Spear-and-shield doctrine optimized for wall defense and field battle.
**Scroll Policy:** All drop-scrolls found within Dominion territory are subject to a 25% tax (higher than other factions — the Dominion is aggressive about it). Crafted scrolls must be registered with the Office of Scrolls. Unregistered scrolls are confiscated; repeat offenders are force-bound to a labor class via dark scroll (rare, but it happens — the Dominion does not publicly admit this).
**Economy:** Grain (wheat, barley, rye), livestock (pigs, chickens, goats — all mutated-stock bred down to manageable size over generations), iron mining, weapon forging, scroll taxation. Self-sufficient in food — the only Agrarian faction that can say so.
**Relations:** Tense with Greenward League (trade rivals, border friction). Cold hostility with Ashmarch Conclave (the Conclave takes scrolls the Dominion claims). No trust anywhere.
**Internal Tension:** The monarch is aging. The three houses maneuver for the succession. House Harvest Hall controls the granaries; House Ironfield controls the forges; House Stormrow controls the hunters. No house can win alone; any two could. But the real knife is the Alchemist Guild: the guildmaster has been quietly withholding crafted scrolls from the Office of Scrolls for three seasons, stockpiling them, and letting each house believe the guild will back them. The guild has picked no side. The guild is waiting to see which house offers the most. The houses know this. None can afford to alienate the guild; none can afford to trust it.
**Power Structure:** The monarch, three great house heads, the Crown Guard general, the Office of Scrolls warden, and the Alchemist Guild guildmaster — five power centers. The succession is a four-way standoff held in place by the guild's refusal to commit.

### FACTION -- Greenward League
**Type:** Alliance of mid-tier walled city-states (farmer cooperative)
**Leader:** The League Council — rotating presidency among five city delegates. Decisions by majority vote; the president breaks ties.
**Location:** Agraria, eastern plains and river delta
**Structure:** Defensive alliance. Five walled cities (Greenward Proper, Millhaven, Farrowfield, Brookmeadow, Tillcott) govern independently but coordinate defense, grain trade, and scroll-sharing through the League Council.
**Population:** Combined 70,000-90,000. Spread across five smaller walled settlements connected by fortified grain-roads.
**Military:** The Green Wardens — 2,000 soldiers, 350 hunters. Modest but well-supplied. Grain-fed, disciplined, defensive doctrine. Mercenary supplement common in hard seasons.
**Scroll Policy:** Moderate. Drop-scrolls taxed at 10%. Crafted scrolls are guild-regulated but not state-confiscated. The League prides itself on "fair scroll access" — a propagandistic claim, but closer to true than most.
**Economy:** Trade grain, preserve meat, craft goods, scroll brokerage. Wealthiest per capita in Agraria, but most exposed to creature pressure (long border, many walls to maintain).
**Relations:** Trade with Hollow Crown Dominion (grain-for-iron). Distrust both the Dominion and the Conclave. Maintain neutrality as survival strategy.
**Internal Tension:** A schism over the Turning. Three seasons ago a Greenward scholar returned from the Oldwork with a pre-Turning text fragment that, if translated correctly, implies the Turning was *caused by people*. The Circle of Alchemists says it is a mistranslation. The scholar's followers — the "Reckoners" — say the alchemists are lying to protect their authority. The schism cuts across all five cities: every household has a Reckoner or an anti-Reckoner now. Millhaven wants the scholar silenced; Farrowfield is printing the fragment as a broadsheet. Greenward Proper is trying to keep the council neutral and failing. The Reckoners are not violent yet. The word "yet" is doing the work.
**Power Structure:** Five city delegates on the League Council, the Green Wardens general, the League Alchemist Circle (a cooperative of guildmasters from all five cities — now deeply divided over the fragment), and the Reckoner scholar (no formal office, but drawing crowds in every city).

### FACTION -- Ashmarch Conclave
**Type:** Frontier military order (fortified border march)
**Leader:** the Marchwarden — elected by the Conclave's combat-eligible membership. Holds office until death, resignation, or defeat in trial-by-combat. Currently held by an aging veteran who has not been challenged in eight years.
**Location:** Agraria, western deep-wilds border
**Structure:** Martial meritocracy. The Conclave is a military order that holds the western frontier. Members are soldiers and hunters first; civilian support staff second. The Marchwarden rules with a council of five Ward-Captains, each commanding a wall sector.
**Population:** 40,000-55,000 within a single massive fortress-city (Ashmarch Keep) and three satellite forts. Smallest population of the Agrarian factions, but the most militarized per capita.
**Military:** The Ashen Vanguard — 3,000 soldiers, 800 hunters. Highest hunter-to-soldier ratio on the continent. The Vanguard is the most combat-experienced force in Agraria; they fight the deep wilds daily.
**Scroll Policy:** Merit-based distribution. Drop-scrolls go to the Conclave vault; scrolls are lent to members based on combat need. Personal scrolls allowed but tracked. Dark scroll possession is *mandatory reporting* — anyone found with an unregistered dark scroll is publicly named and exiled into the wilds without supplies (effective death sentence).
**Economy:** Foraging, hunting, scroll-bounties (the Conclave is paid by other factions to clear the western wilds), limited interior farming. Poorest grain production — relies on Greenward League trade.
**Relations:** Cold trade with Hollow Crown Dominion (the Dominion pays bounties; the Conclave takes them). Neutral with Greenward League (grain trade). Deeply isolationist otherwise.
**Internal Tension:** The Vault scandal. The Marchwarden claims the three confiscated dark scrolls were destroyed. Three Ward-Captains believe otherwise — and one of them, quietly, has been using one. Conscription patrols on the western deep-wilds border have started forcing dark-scroll classes onto captured raiders and deserters. The Marchwarden either does not know or is choosing not to know. The Vault-Warden knows and has said nothing. The Conclave Alchemist suspects and is buying silence with scroll-crafting priority. The internal question is no longer "who will succeed the Marchwarden" but "when the rest of the Conclave finds out, does the order hold, or does it tear itself apart?"
**Power Structure:** The Marchwarden, five Ward-Captains (one compromised), the Vault-Warden (complicit), and the Conclave Alchemist (a single guildmaster with unusual autonomy — the Conclave cannot afford to antagonize its only alchemist, and the alchemist knows it).

### LOCATION -- Hollowspire (Hollow Crown Dominion capital)
**Type:** Walled city-state capital
**Location:** Central Agraria river valley
**Geography:** Built on a raised riverbank with natural cliff faces on two sides; walls enclose ~5 sq km. Stone and iron, austere — granaries, barracks, and the Crown Keep dominate the skyline. The great grain silos are the city's most defended structures (more than the palace).
**Walls:** Double walls — outer 30m high / 8m thick (iron-reinforced gates), inner 40m / 12m around the Keep and granary district.
**Districts:** Outer Ring (residential, markets, forges, day-labor hall; Gate Street = main commercial artery; outer garrisons double as shelters). Middle Ring (military barracks, training grounds, Hunter Guild hall, the Pit — underground scroll-testing/dispute arena). Inner Ring (Crown Keep, the Aula great hall, noble estates, the Office of Scrolls, the Grand Granary, the Alchemist Guild hall).
**Notable Feature:** The Grand Granary — a fortified silo complex holding enough grain to feed the city through two winters. Control of the Granary is the true power in Hollowspire; the Office of Scrolls is second.
**Threat Level:** Moderate. River-valley geography provides natural defense. Main threats: airborne creatures, tunneling beasts, and spring floods that weaken the walls.

### LOCATION -- Ashmarch Keep
**Type:** Frontier fortress-city
**Location:** Western Agraria, deep-wilds border
**Geography:** Built into a natural defile — the only pass through the western mountain range for ~200km. The walls seal the pass. Everything is stone and iron, perpetually grimy with ash from the constant forges. A fortress first, a city second — every building is also a strongpoint, every street is a kill-zone.
**Walls:** Triple walls across the defile — outer 35m / 10m thick, middle 45m / 12m, inner 30m around the Keep itself. The walls are stained black with decades of forge-smoke and creature-blood.
**Districts:** The Foreground (open ground before the outer wall, cleared and trapped — kills anything that breaches at range), The Ward (military barracks, hunter staging, the Vault — scroll storage), The Forge Quarter (weapon smithing, alchemist hall, armor repair — runs 24/7), The Keep (Marchwarden's hall, council chamber, the deep granary).
**Notable Feature:** The Vault — the Conclave's scroll store, deeper and more reinforced than any other structure. Holds the largest known collection of combat-class scrolls in Agraria. Legend says it also holds three dark scrolls confiscated from offenders — locked in a warded box that no one is permitted to open. The Marchwarden claims they are destroyed. Three Ward-Captains believe otherwise.
**Threat Level:** Extreme. The western wilds are the most creature-dense region in Agraria. Daily pressure. Wall breaches happen — the Foreground is the response.

### LOCATION -- Greenward Proper
**Type:** Walled city (League capital)
**Location:** Eastern Agraria plains
**Geography:** Built on a low hill in open plains; walls enclose ~3 sq km. Stone with timber upper works — functional, not pretty, but well-maintained. The city is the grain-trading hub of Agraria; the smell of milled flour is constant.
**Walls:** Single wall 25m high / 6m thick, well-maintained, with watchtowers every 100m. A secondary interior wall protects the League Council hall and the League Alchemist Circle.
**Districts:** Market Ring (grain exchange, craft goods, the biggest open-air market in Agraria), Mill Quarter (watermills on the river, millers' guild, bakeries), Residential Rings (mixed housing, communal ovens — every neighborhood has a shared bakehouse), Inner Ring (League Council hall, Alchemist Circle, scroll exchange).
**Notable Feature:** The Grain Exchange — the largest open-air grain market on the continent. Prices here set prices everywhere. A bad harvest in Greenward Proper means hunger in Hollowspire within a season.

### LOCATION -- The Oldwork (Agrarian ruin complex)
**Type:** Pre-Turning ruin
**Location:** Agraria, deep wilds ~60km west of Ashmarch Keep
**Geography:** A buried pre-Turning city, half-sunk into a river valley. Cut-stone walls, wide streets, a central domed structure (purpose unknown — texts inside are fragmentary). Partially flooded. Occupied by nesting creatures (flocks of mutated raptors in the dome, burrowing insects in the foundations).
**Accessibility:** Expedition-only. The Conclave runs quarterly raids; returns are irregular. The dome yields the most scrolls but is the most lethal.
**Significance:** The Oldwork's alchemical workshop (a basement chamber with intact distillation apparatus) is the only known pre-Turning alchemy lab still partially functional. The Conclave's alchemist studies it. The texts are unreadable; the apparatus still works if fed the right reagents — but no one knows the right reagents. Experiments are dangerous and slow.
**Danger Level:** High — creature-infested, structurally unstable, partially flooded, and politically contested (the Dominion claims it lies within their territory; the Conclave holds it by force).

## CONTINENT 2 — PASTORIA (Rancher-Clan Open Range)

### OVERVIEW -- Continent of Pastoria
**scan_depth:** 5
**Type:** Continental region
**Geography:** Vast open plains, dry grasslands, rolling steppes, scattered mesas, river canyons. Drier than Agraria. Long summers, short winters, constant wind. Few forests — the plains go on forever.
**Culture Base — Way of Life:** Ranching and herding. Great herds of mutated-but-bred-down cattle, horses, goats, and stranger stock. Open-range grazing within walled ranch-compounds. Clan-based society — the clan is the herd, the herd is the clan, the land is leased from the wilds by blood.
**Major Powers:** Roanhold Confederacy (largest rancher-clan alliance), Mesa Freeriders (independent riders of the mesa country), Saltreach Pact (riverside ranch-clans with a trading monopoly).
**Population Estimate:** Second largest continent. Spread thin — vast territory, lower density. Walled ranch-compounds dot the plains; the cities are trade-hubs, not population centers.
**Climate Threat:** Drought years drive creatures from the deep plains toward the watered river canyons — where the ranch-compounds are. A drought year is a war year.
**Unique Feature:** The Long Drives — armed cattle drives across hundreds of kilometers of open range between seasonal pastures. The Long Drive is the rancher-clan defining ritual; raiding a Long Drive is the highest crime and the most common one.
**Attitude Toward Scrolls:** Personal and clan-bound. A scroll belongs to the person who bound it, but the clan claims a "herd right" — if you leave the clan, your scrolls stay (they are you, they die with you), but your *children* inherit clan-provided scrolls preferentially. Clans hoard rare Profession+Aspect scrolls as herd-totems.
**Alchemist Guilds:** Mobile. Pastoria alchemists travel with the clans in alchemist-wagons. They are clan members, not external guilds. A clan's alchemist is its second most important member (after the trail-boss).

### FACTION -- Roanhold Confederacy
**Type:** Rancher-clan alliance (open-range herd confederacy)
**Leader:** The Roan Matriarch — hereditary head of the founding Roan clan, first among equals in the Confederacy council. Currently an aging woman who has held the alliance together by force of personality for thirty years.
**Location:** Pastoria, central plains
**Structure:** Clan confederacy. Seven major clans (Roan, Dusthoof, Ironhorn, Windmane, Saltcoat, Redox, Gaptooth) each control a herd-range and a walled ranch-compound. The Matriarch convenes the council; decisions bind only if ratified by clan vote.
**Population:** 80,000-110,000 across seven ranch-compounds and the central trade-city of Roanhold. Roanhold itself is the only true city — the rest are walled compounds holding 5,000-15,000 each.
**Military:** The Roan Riders — 4,000 mounted soldiers, 700 hunters. Cavalry doctrine. Fast, hard-hitting, mobile defense. The Riders are the only standing army; clan militias supplement in crisis.
**Scroll Policy:** Clan-controlled. Drop-scrolls go to the clan's vault; distribution by clan council. Personal scrolls are personal, but a clan-member who leaves forfeits clan-provisioned scrolls (which must be overwritten before departure — a cruel exit cost). Rare Profession+Aspect scrolls are clan totems, never personal property.
**Economy:** Herd products (meat, hide, horn, sinew, milk, blood-pudding), horse breeding, leatherwork, horn-bows. Grain is imported — Pastoria cannot grow enough. Trade with Saltreach for grain; raid Agraria-style factions when trade fails.
**Relations:** Trade with Saltreach Pact (grain-for-meat). Cold with Mesa Freeriders (the Freeriders take what they want). No contact beyond Pastoria.
**Internal Tension:** The Great Plague. A wasting sickness has swept the Confederacy's herds for two seasons — not the Turning's mutation, just a sickness, but it has killed a third of the cattle and the clans are bleeding. The Matriarch has quarantined the worst-hit clans; the quarantined clans say the quarantine is a land-grab by the healthy clans. The Dusthoof clan, worst-hit, has started driving their sick herds onto Ironhorn and Roan grazing land in the nights. The Roan Riders have been deployed to stop them — clan soldiers pointing spears at clan herds. The Matriarch is aging, and the plague is settling the succession question faster than any clan vote: whoever controls the surviving herds controls the Confederacy. The Confederacy Alchemist Circle is working on a cure; the Circle says they are close. The clans do not believe the Circle.
**Power Structure:** The Roan Matriarch, seven clan heads (three quarantined, four healthy, all distrustful), the Roan Riders general (caught between orders and clan loyalty), and the Confederacy Alchemist Circle (seven clan alchemists whose cooperation is fraying as clans hide sick herds from each other).

### FACTION -- Mesa Freeriders
**Type:** Independent rider-bands (loose alliance of mesa-country nomads)
**Leader:** No single leader. Each band has a captain; bands meet at the Mesa Gather twice a year to coordinate. The closest thing to a leader is the captain of the largest band — currently a younger rider called only "the Dustwind."
**Location:** Pastoria, mesa country (western badlands)
**Structure:** Band-based. ~12 bands of 200-800 riders each. No permanent cities — the Freeriders live in mobile camps and seasonal mesa-top strongholds (fortified camps with palisade-and-stone walls, abandoned each season). Bands cooperate; no band submits to another.
**Population:** ~8,000-12,000 total. Smallest Pastoria faction, but the most mobile and the most individually capable riders.
**Military:** Every Freerider is a soldier. ~3,000 effective riders, 400 hunters. No standing army — the bands *are* the army. Cavalry archers, skirmishers, raiders. Cannot hold ground; can strike anywhere.
**Scroll Policy:** Personal. Whatever you can carry and bind. No clan claim, no tax. This is why people join the Freeriders — freedom. This is also why the Freeriders are distrusted — they take scrolls from everyone else.
**Economy:** Raiding, trading, escort work, bounty hunting. No herds of their own — they live off the work of others (and are honest about it). Some bands run legitimate trade caravans; others are bandits. The line is thin.
**Relations:** Hostile with Roanhold (constant raiding). Cold trade with Saltreach. Hated by everyone, employed by everyone.
**Internal Tension:** The Proxy War. The bands have always raided; now they are being paid to raid specific targets. The Saltreach Pact has been quietly hiring Freerider bands to hit Roanhold long-drive routes and Saltreach trade-rivals alike — deniable raiding, paid in iron bits and the occasional scroll. The Roanhold Confederacy knows and has started paying other bands to raid the Saltreach-hired bands. Half the Freeriders are now on someone's payroll; the other half are horrified that the bands are becoming mercenaries. The Dustwind wants to formalize the arrangement — a Freerider "contract code" that keeps the bands free while taking the coin. Half the captains call it selling out; the other half call it surviving. The bands that took Saltreach coin are better-armed now. The bands that refused are not.
**Power Structure:** Twelve band captains (six on a faction's payroll, six refusing), the Dustwind (first among equals — trying to hold the middle), and the Freeriders' alchemists (two traveling alchemists — the only Freerider institution, and now the only people both sides still trust).

### FACTION -- Saltreach Pact
**Type:** Riverside ranch-clan trading alliance
**Leader:** The Saltreach Factor — an elected merchant-head, chosen by the Pact's trading houses for a five-year term. Currently on a third term — unusual, controversial, and effective.
**Location:** Pastoria, the great river canyon (the Saltreach) and its delta

The Factor ordered the investigation sealed. The other four trading houses have now found out. The question is no longer "who is the mole" but "how high does it go" — and every house suspects the Factor himself, because the third term that kept him in power was won by margins that now look bought. ## CONTINENT 3 — KHELORI (Nomadic Rotational Graziers)

### OVERVIEW -- Continent of Khelori
**scan_depth:** 5
**Type:** Continental region
**Geography:** The most geographically diverse continent. Volcanic ranges, deep jungle, vast savanna, alpine meadows, salt flats, river deltas. The land itself moves — volcanic activity reshapes the map every generation. Heat, humidity, altitude variation.
**Culture Base — Way of Life:** Nomadic rotational grazing. No fixed cities. The Khelori follow seasonal migration circuits between fortified seasonal camps. Each clan has a circuit of 4-6 camps; they rotate between them as the seasons turn. The camp is a walled earth-and-timber fortification, rebuilt each year on the same site, abandoned each season, returned to the next. The herd is the clan. The route is the clan. The camp is temporary; the route is eternal.
**Major Powers:** Three great migration circuits — the Highroute (alpine meadow circuit, wealthiest herds), the Greenroute (jungle-edge circuit, most dangerous, best scrolls), the Ashroute (volcanic-slope circuit, hardest people, finest forges).
**Population Estimate:** Smallest population, most dispersed. No cities over 20,000; the largest seasonal camp holds 8,000-12,000 during peak season. Survival here requires different skills — mobility, route-knowledge, ambush avoidance.
**Climate Threat:** No safe season. Wet season brings amphibious creature surges and flooding. Dry season brings volcanic eruptions and creature migrations. The only constant is movement.
**Unique Feature:** The Circuit itself is sacred. A clan that loses its route (creatures overrunning a camp site, volcanic burial, drought) is a clan in exile — they must find a new circuit or die. Route-loss is the Khelori's deepest fear.
**Attitude Toward Scrolls:** Communal and route-bound. Scrolls belong to the clan; the clan assigns them to members based on route-need. Individual ownership exists but is culturally moderated. Hoarding scrolls is a route-violation — the clan needs the scrolls distributed for defense, not concentrated for status.
**Alchemist Guilds:** Clan-bound. Every clan has one or two alchemists. The alchemist travels with the clan, crafts scrolls from route-gathered reagents, and is considered the route's keeper-of-knowledge. A clan's alchemist is the most protected non-combatant.

### FACTION -- Highroute Circuit
**Type:** Nomadic grazing circuit (alpine meadow)
**Leader:** The High Speaker — elected by the clan heads of the Highroute, holds office for one full circuit (typically 4-6 years). Currently a young and unusually political Speaker serving a second term.
**Location:** Khelori, alpine meadow belt (northern highlands)
**Structure:** Clan alliance. Five clans (Summit, Cloudfield, Snowmane, Passtone, Skyrest) each follow the Highroute circuit of six seasonal camps. The Speaker coordinates; the clan heads decide.
**Population:** 30,000-40,000 across five clans and six camps (active camps hold 6,000-10,000 depending on season). Wealthiest herds on Khelori — alpine grass is rich.
**Military:** The High Guard — 1,500 soldiers, 400 hunters. Cavalry-and-archer doctrine optimized for mountain meadow fighting. Less combat experience than the Ashroute; more wealth to fund mercenaries.
**Scroll Policy:** Route-communal. The Highroute maintains a circuit-vault that moves with the Speaker; scrolls are distributed by clan need. Personal scrolls allowed but route-circumscribed.
**Economy:** Herd products (the finest wool and cheese on Khelori), alpine herbs, medicinal plants, scroll-crafting (the Highroute alchemists are the most academically rigorous on the continent). Trade with Ashroute for forged goods.
**Relations:** Friendly with Ashroute (trade partners, different routes — no territorial conflict). Tense with Greenroute (the Greenroute wants Highroute alpine camps for summer grazing; the Highroute refuses).
**Internal Tension:** The Prophecy. A circuit-priest of the Old Faith has spent the last year preaching that the next Silent Season is the final one — that the Turning's cycle is closing and the wilds will go silent forever, or rise worse than before. The priest claims to have read it in a Highroute ruin-text; the Circuit Alchemist says the text is illegible. The young faction, already restless, has taken the prophecy as license to push for a permanent settlement ("if the wilds change, so must we"). The old guard calls it heresy and demands the priest be silenced. The Speaker — already on an unprecedented second term — has refused to act, which the old guard reads as secret sympathy. The camps are dividing along generational lines: the young gather at the priest's fires at night; the old keep their children inside. The circuit is still rotating, but the rotation is quieter than it was, and the herds are not being watched the way they should be.
**Power Structure:** The High Speaker, five clan heads (split young/old), the High Guard general (old guard, loyal to the office), the Circuit Alchemist (a single senior alchemist — says the text is illegible, may be lying), and the circuit-priest (no formal office, drawing the young).

### FACTION -- Greenroute Circuit
**Type:** Nomadic grazing circuit (jungle-edge)
**Leader:** The Green Mother — hereditary shaman-head of the Greenroute, holds office for life. Currently an old and formidable woman who has led the Greenroute for forty years.
**Location:** Khelori, jungle-edge belt (central lowlands)
**Structure:** Hereditary matriarchy. Three clans (Greenroot, Vinehall, Thornwalk) each follow the Greenroute circuit of five camps along the jungle edge. The Green Mother rules; clan heads defer.
**Population:** 20,000-28,000 across three clans and five camps. Smallest Khelori faction, most dangerous route — the jungle-edge is the most creature-dense territory on the continent.
**Military:** The Green Guard — 800 soldiers, 500 hunters. Highest hunter-to-soldier ratio on Khelori. The hunters *are* the military. Poison-trap doctrine, camouflage, ambush avoidance.
**Scroll Policy:** Fully communal. All scrolls go to the route-pool; the Green Mother distributes. Personal scroll use is permitted during a hunt; scrolls return to the pool after. This works because the Greenroute is small and trust is high.
**Economy:** Jungle resources — medicinal plants, rare fungi, exotic reagents (the best alchemical materials on Khelori), poison-craft, scroll-crafting (Greenroute scrolls are the most potent on the continent — the jungle reagents are unmatched). Trade with Highroute for herd products.
**Relations:** Friendly with Ashroute (trade reagents for forged goods). Hostile with Highroute (territorial dispute over alpine summer camps). Isolationist otherwise.
**Internal Tension:** The Rot. The jungle is changing — not just encroaching, *sickening*. A creeping black rot has been spreading through the jungle edge for three seasons, killing the thorn-hedge walls at two of the five camps. The Greenroute's defense is its living walls; losing them is existential. The Green Mother says the rot is a test and the route must hold. The Thornwalk clan, whose camp walls are half-dead, wants to abandon the camp and migrate to the Highroute alpine camps — the very camps the Highroute refuses to share. The Greenroute Alchemist Circle believes the rot is alchemical — a pre-Turning contamination waking up in the soil — and that the Deepwork ruin is the source. The young faction says: go deeper into the jungle, find the source, end it, take the Deepwork's scrolls. The old guard says: the jungle kills everyone who goes deeper; the rot is the jungle's answer to the Deepwork raids. Two camps are already preparing to abandon their circuit sites. If they go, the Greenroute shrinks to three clans and the route is broken.
**Power Structure:** The Green Mother, three clan heads (one ready to leave), the Green Guard captain (holds the line, barely), and the three clan alchemists (who form the Greenroute Alchemist Circle — the only true alchemist institution on Khelori, and now the only people who might understand the rot).

**Type:** Nomadic grazing circuit (volcanic slope)

**Location:** Khelori, volcanic slope belt (eastern ranges)

For two years the Greenroute has been receiving *better* Ashroute steel than the Highroute — finer edge, better temper, more of it — and the Highroute has been receiving the standard grade. ## POWER SYSTEM & RULES

### POWER_SYSTEM -- Class Scroll Mechanics
**Type:** Soul-binding class progression system with permanent respec cost

**Binding:** Read scroll + will binding → scroll consumed → class bound (one-class hard limit; binding a second scroll requires willing the overwrite of the current class). Children under ~14 cannot bind.

**Progression (Mastery Tiers):** Novice → Apprentice → Adept → Expert → Master → Grandmaster → Legend. Rank recognized by authority (guild, mentor, trial), not counted. No numeric levels. Every scroll binds at Novice; rank is earned through play.

**Overwrite (Respec):** New scroll overwrites the current class (only one class is held at a time). Overwritten class's **class-skills and special effects are lost** — access gone; rebind later restarts class-skill access at Novice. **General competence is kept** — reflexes, conditioning, body memory, instincts stay with the body. An ex-Legend still moves like a Legend in any new class; they just cannot perform any class-skill from the overwritten class. See OVERVIEW -- Class-Skills vs General Skills. No stash, no reload, no class-skill recovery.

**Death:** Bound classes destroyed with the body. Scrolls are consumed at binding; nothing to loot. Death is final.

**No Variant Tiers:** There are no Plain/Fine/Illuminated/Sovereign tiers. A scroll is a scroll. The class is Profession+Aspect; the power comes from the combination and the rank the binder earns. See OVERVIEW -- Class Scrolls Have No Variant Tiers.

### POWER_SYSTEM -- The Power Ceiling (What Class-Skills Can and Cannot Do)
<!-- rag: keyword, triggers: power ceiling, power level, how strong, how powerful, mage meteor, destroy city, beat army, solo army, strongest, legend tier, cap, limit, what magic can do, magic scope, mage scope -->
**Type:** Hard cap on class-skill output — the upper bound of what the system produces.

**The Ceiling — One Sentence:** The strongest classed person alive can beat an army solo, but cannot destroy a city in one go. Class-skills turn the tide of a battle; they do not erase the battlefield.

**What a Class-Skill Can Do at the Top (Grandmaster / Legend tier):**
- **Solo an army.** A Legend-tier Swordsman, backed by their class-skills, can wade through a formation of classless soldiers and win. The class-skill's special effect — the amplified strike, the auto-counter, the void-strike that erases force — multiplies Legend-tier body competence into something a wall of classless bodies cannot stop. This is the upper bound of martial class-skills: one person can break an army's line, kill its officers, and survive. It is not instant. It is not free. A Legend can be worn down, surrounded, trapped, or killed by enough bodies or by a smart opponent with a dark scroll. But in a straight fight, the army loses.
- **Punch a hole in fortification.** A high-tier martial class-skill can breach a gate, shatter a battlement, or punch through a wall section — with effort, time, and mana/stamina cost. A single strike does not level a wall. Repeated strikes over minutes can open a breach. This is the fortification ceiling: walls still matter, breaches still take work, a single class-skill does not delete stone.
- **Call a meteor — at tactical scale.** A Legend Mage can call a meteor. The meteor can destroy a city gate, shatter an army's front line, or crater a section of wall. It cannot blast a whole city to smithereens in one go. The meteor's scale is "one structure / one formation," not "one settlement." A Mage who wants to flatten a city needs many castings over many hours — and each casting drains mana, and a drained Mage is a classless person until they recover. The ceiling on magic is: tactical devastation, not strategic erasure.
- **Turn a duel.** In a one-on-one fight between two high-tier classed fighters, the class-skills decide it. The special effects — the void-strike, the auto-riposte, the wind-compensated arrow, the flame-buffed blade — are the margin. Two Legend-tier bodies without class-skills fight to a coin-flip; with class-skills, the better class-skill matchup wins.

**What a Class-Skill Cannot Do:**
- **Destroy a city in one go.** No class-skill, at any tier, erases a settlement in a single use. Not a meteor, not a void-strike, not a berserker rampage. Cities are beyond the ceiling. Walls, granaries, populations — these survive even a Legend's best shot. A Legend can kill the garrison and open the gate; they cannot make the city not exist.
- **Resurrect the dead.** Death is final. No class-skill, no alchemy, no ruin-artifact brings back the dead. The Turning did not give anyone that.
- **Bypass the one-class limit, the respec cost, or the dark scroll rules.** The power ceiling is a class-skill ceiling, not a system-rules ceiling. A Legend Swordsman is still one class, still loses class-skills on overwrite, still dies to a dark scroll at close range.
- **Make the bearer immune to consequence.** A Legend can be betrayed, trapped, poisoned, dark-scroll-attacked, starved, drowned, or crushed by numbers. The class-skill amplifies what the body can do; it does not make the body unkillable.

**Mage and Enchanter Scope (the magical classes — and why they are among the best plain scrolls):**
- The medieval world has no magic on its own. A Mage scroll *allows* magic. An Enchanter scroll *allows* temporary item and person enhancement. These are plain Profession scrolls that grant abilities no human body can produce — which makes them more coveted than most combat scrolls in many contexts. A Swordsman scroll amplifies the physical; a Mage scroll opens an entirely different axis.
- **Mage** casts using an internal mana pool. Spells are mana-cost and tier-scaled: a Novice Mage throws a spark; an Adept Mage throws a firebolt; a Legend Mage calls a meteor that breaches a gate. Mana refills with rest (hours) — a drained Mage is a classless person until they recover. The ceiling is the meteor-at-tactical-scale rule above. Mages are artillery — devastating in a battle, useless against a whole city, and vulnerable while their mana pool is empty.
- **Enchanter** temporarily buffs items and people using the same internal mana pool. An Enchanter can ward a wall section against a creature assault, buff a squad's stamina for a sortie, sharpen a weapon's edge for a fight, or lay a triggering ward that detonates when crossed. Enchanters are force-multipliers, not solo combatants; their ceiling is "turn a losing defense into a held one," not "win a battle alone." Mana cost scales with the scope and duration of the buff.
- **No free magic.** Every magical effect costs mana. Mana refills with rest, not with purchases — a Mage cannot buy more mana mid-fight the way a swordsman can buy a better blade. The cap on magic is the mana pool, which is bounded by Mastery Tier. A Legend Mage has a deep pool; a Novice Mage has a shallow one. No Mage has an infinite pool.

**Why the Ceiling Is Here:** The world is built around walls, sieges, and the price of survival. If a Legend could delete a city, walls would not matter and the central premise collapses. If a Legend could not beat an army, the rare scroll would not be worth the cost of finding one. The ceiling holds both truths: one classed person can save a city or break an army, but cannot end the world. The walls still stand. The wilds still press. The scrolls are still worth everything.

### POWER_SYSTEM -- Dark Scroll Mechanics
**Type:** Forced class-changer — the class change IS the devastation. No other effect.
**Activation:** Close range (arm's reach / a few paces) + read aloud by an attacker. Binding is forced on the victim.
**Effect:** Victim's old class is overwritten; the dark scroll's carried Profession (and Aspect, if any — rare) is bound. There is no track choice — the victim's single class is replaced outright. Overwritten class's class-skills lost permanently (access gone; rebind restarts at Novice). General competence kept — the victim is still their old self in body, missing only their class-technique. That is the entire effect. No extra curse, no mind-control, no additional mechanic.
**Limit:** Dark scrolls change the class, not the will. A forced Soldier fights better but can still choose *who* to fight for. No direct mind control. (Factions wanting true control use imprisonment, hostages, threats.)
**Sources:** Drops (~1-3% of scroll drops, more from corrupted/abyssal creatures), crafted (forbidden alchemy, capital crime), ruins (pre-Turning dark scrolls — strongest; can carry UNIQUE Professions).
**Removal:** Forced class can be overwritten normally (victim or allies bind a new scroll — same respec cost; the original lost class is already gone, this just frees them from the forced class). "Cleansers" carry spare scrolls for this purpose — for a price.
**Tactical Use:** Get close to a high-rank enemy, read a menial-class dark scroll at them. Legolas becomes a Farmer. His Archer class-skills gone, his body still Legend-tier, but his class is now farming. Mid-fight, that gap is usually fatal. The killer needs no blade, only proximity and a scroll.
**Cultural Status:** Universal taboo. Every faction condemns it. Every faction has used it. The gap between doctrine and practice is an open wound.

### POWER_SYSTEM -- Alchemy
**Type:** Reagent-based craft science — the only supernatural discipline *besides* the mana-based magic carried by Mage and Enchanter scrolls
**Two Layers:**
1. **Scroll Crafting (meta):** Alchemists manufacture scrolls from reagents. A master alchemist produces a plain Profession scroll in a week; an Aspect scroll takes longer and often fails. Drop-scrolls can be refined into typed scrolls (alchemist steers the Profession; adding an Aspect is harder).
2. **Everyday Alchemy (in-fiction):** Potions, salves, poisons, smoke-bombs, preservatives. Useful, tradeable, not world-shaking. (Mage and Enchanter classes do NOT consume reagents — they use an internal mana pool. Reagents are for alchemical products and scroll-crafting, not for casting.)
**Forbidden Alchemy:** Sub-tradition within alchemy. Same knowledge, wrong reagents, wrong intent. Makes dark scrolls. Capital crime in most factions. Secretly practiced everywhere.
**Political Role:** Alchemists are the scroll-supply bottleneck. Alchemist guilds/clans/circles are major power centers. A faction that loses its alchemists loses its scroll supply.
**No Rival School:** No runecrafting, no blood-magic, no divine miracle. There are two supernatural axes in the world: alchemy (reagent-based craft, makes scrolls and potions) and scroll-carried magic (mana-based, granted by Mage/Enchanter scrolls that alchemists craft). Alchemy makes the scrolls; the scrolls make the magic. The two disciplines are related but distinct — a Mage casts with mana, not with reagents.

## CLASS GENERATOR (Profession, sometimes + Aspect)

**Type:** Generative class system — NOT a fixed roster. Most classes are a plain **Profession** (Swordsman, Peasant, Cook, anything). A minority also carry an **Aspect** (Wind, Duck, Void, Cactus, anything) that reshapes the class-skills. There is no locked list. Any profession is valid; any aspect is valid when the GM decides this scroll is special.

**The Two Inputs:**
- **Profession (always present):** What the class *does*. Swordsman, Archer, Healer, Farmer, Dishwasher, Accountant, Beekeeper, Gravedigger, Courier, Cook, Locksmith, Cartographer, Glazier, Ploughman, Jester, Whaler, Cooper, Fletcher, Tanner, Ratcatcher. ANY profession the world contains or could contain. Pre-Turning ruins may yield professions that no longer exist in the modern world (the GM invents them).
- **Aspect (sometimes present — the exception, not the rule):** How the class does it *differently*. Wind, Duck, Cactus, Void, Smoke, Wolf, Coral, Ledger, Silence, Hunger, Tide, Mirror, Bramble, Echo, Forge, Loom — ANY aspect the GM can name. No list, no seeds. Anything that evokes a coherent class-skill flavor is valid. Most scrolls do NOT have an Aspect. When the GM gives a scroll an Aspect, the scroll is special — its class-skills are reshaped and its value is higher.

**The Class-Name:**
- Plain Profession: just the name. "Swordsman." "Peasant." "Cook." "Dishwasher."
- Profession + Aspect: "Profession of the Aspect" or "Aspect Profession" — whichever reads better. "Swordsman of the Void." "Wind Swordsman." "Dishwasher of the Void."

**What This Means in Play:**
- Most sword-fighters are just "Swordsman." Plain class-skills, baseline techniques. Common goods.
- A "Swordsman of the Void" is rare — its class-skills are void-flavored (strikes that erase, defensive moves that hollow out the attacker's force). It is worth more. It is feared more. It is NOT what most sword-fighters carry.
- A cook with the Fire Aspect roasts with class-skill flame-control. A cook with the Silence Aspect produces food that quiets those who eat it. A cook with the Void Aspect... does something no one wants to think about, but the class-skills work. These are exceptional cooks — most cooks are just Cooks.
- A "Dishwasher of the Void" has class-skills themed around void-cleaning: a Novice class-skill might clean a plate so thoroughly it erases residual food-essence (mild alchemical purification). At higher ranks, the void-aspect scales — dishes come out *empty of history*, scoured of any trace. At Master, the Dishwasher of the Void can strip metaphysical residue from objects — which is actually useful for cleaning cursed items. Ridiculous entry, real progression. This is a once-in-a-lifetime weird scroll, not a common drop.
- The system is deliberately absurd at the edges and serious at the core. Most scrolls that exist are plain professions people actually want (Swordsman, Healer, Farmer, Cook, Smith) — but scrolls are rare, so "most scrolls" is a small number. The engine does not forbid "Dishwasher of the Void" — it is simply rare, the GM's call when a special scroll enters play.

**GM Note — Generating a Class:** When a scroll is bound or appraised, the GM does one thing, sometimes two, rarely three:
1. **Pick a Profession.** Always. Any profession. No list, no seeds, no roster. If the world contains the role, or could contain it, the scroll can carry it. Ruin-scrolls may carry professions that no longer exist in the modern world (the GM invents them).
2. **Give it an Aspect — ONLY if this scroll is meant to be special.** Most scrolls skip this step. The class is just the Profession. When the GM decides this scroll is exceptional (rare drop, ruin-origin, alchemist masterwork, plot-relevant), the GM gives it an Aspect — any aspect, no list, anything the GM can name. The Aspect shapes the class-skills and their special effects.
3. **Make it a UNIQUE Profession — ONLY if this scroll is meant to be legendary.** Almost never. The GM picks a completely separate deluxe Profession (Swordmaster, Surgeon, Chemist, Chef, etc. — invent freely). NOT an evolution of a normal Profession — a separate scroll entirely. No Aspect (UNIQUE scrolls never carry one). Higher ceiling, qualitatively more advanced class-skills. Deep-ruin or Continent Lord origin, faction-level event. See UNIQUE Professions (Separate Scrolls) below.
The class-name is "Profession" (plain), "Profession of the Aspect" (when special), or "UNIQUE Profession" (when legendary — never with an Aspect). Every scroll binds at Novice. There are no variant tiers — see OVERVIEW -- Class Scrolls Have No Variant Tiers.

**The Rule — Aspects Cannot Be Changed:** Once a scroll is bound, its Aspect (if it has one) is fixed for the life of that class. Overwriting the class wipes the Aspect along with the class-skills. Rebinding a Profession gets whatever the new scroll carries — maybe a plain Profession, maybe a Profession with a new Aspect. The body remembers the Profession; it does not remember the Aspect.

### UNIQUE Professions (Separate Scrolls — The Deluxe Job)
<!-- rag: keyword, triggers: unique profession, swordmaster, marksman, surgeon, chemist, advanced class, deluxe profession, separate scroll, unique class, legendary profession, combat maid, ninja, serial killer, paladin -->
**Type:** The deluxe version of a job — a completely separate scroll. NOT an evolution. NOT an upgrade path. If you bind one, even hardened veterans stop and stare.

**The Concept:** A UNIQUE Profession is its own scroll, its own class, its own class-skills. It shares a *root concept* with a normal Profession, but it is allowed to leap — the deluxe version does not have to be a gentle step up. It can be a massive jump. It does not have to make sense. The root remains, the expression transforms.

**Crucial Rules:**
- **Separate scroll.** A UNIQUE Profession is its own Profession. Binding it overwrites your current class (one-class limit) — if you already hold the base Profession, the UNIQUE version replaces it. If you hold an unrelated class, binding the UNIQUE Profession replaces that one instead.
- **No Aspect.** UNIQUE Profession scrolls never carry an Aspect. They are already the premium expression. "Swordmaster of the Void" does not exist. The deluxe version IS the deluxe version; it does not also get a flavor-shaper on top.
- **Not an evolution.** You do not transform a normal Profession into its UNIQUE version through mastery. The only way to become a UNIQUE Profession is to find its scroll and bind it. If you are a Grandmaster Swordsman and you bind a Swordmaster scroll, you start at Novice Swordmaster — the body remembers sword-fighting (general competence stays), but the Swordmaster class-skills are new and must be learned from Novice.

**Creation Guideline — How the GM Invents a UNIQUE Profession:**
When the GM decides a scroll is legendary and needs a UNIQUE Profession, apply this guideline:
1. **Identify the root concept.** What does the base Profession do? (Knight = sword-and-shield combat. Maid = domestic service. Thief = stealth-theft. Assassin = targeted killing.)
2. **Pick the deluxe expression.** What is the *premium, leapt, or weird* version of that root? It does NOT have to be a gentle upgrade — it can be a massive jump, and it does NOT have to make sense.
   - The root is *sword-and-shield* → the deluxe is *sword-and-shield-and-light-magic* (Knight → Paladin). Massive jump. Allowed.
   - The root is *domestic service* → the deluxe is *domestic-service-and-combat* (Maid → Combat Maid). Weird jump. Allowed.
   - The root is *stealth-theft* → the deluxe is *stealth-theft-and-shadow-arts-and-technique* (Thief → Ninja). Cultural leap. Allowed.
   - The root is *targeted killing* → the deluxe is *killing-without-the-target-contract-just-killing* (Assassin → Serial Killer). Dark leap. Allowed.
   - The root is *blade combat* → the deluxe is *blade combat at its peak* (Swordsman → Swordmaster). Gentle step. Also allowed.
   - The root is *cooking* → the deluxe is *cooking-that-fortifies-body-and-spirit* (Cook → Chef). Conceptual leap. Allowed.
3. **The only requirement:** the root concept must be traceable. There must be a through-line from the base Profession to the UNIQUE version, even if it is strange. Maid → Combat Maid works (the root is service; the deluxe adds combat to service). Maid → Dragon does not work (no root through-line). The leap can be wild, but it must be a leap *from* something.
4. **Name it.** The UNIQUE Profession gets its own name. "Swordmaster," "Combat Maid," "Ninja," "Serial Killer," "Paladin," "Chef," "Chemist," "Waterbender." The name is the class.

**How UNIQUE Profession Scrolls Work:**
- **Source:** Exceptionally rare. Never crafted by modern alchemists (or almost never — the rarest master alchemists might produce one in a lifetime, and it usually breaks them). Found in deep ruins (pre-Turning alchemy at its peak) or dropped by Continent Lord creatures (the rarest drop tier). A UNIQUE Profession scroll is a faction-level event.
- **Starting Rank:** Still Novice — same as every scroll. But the *ceiling* is higher. A UNIQUE Profession can reach tiers of class-skill that its base Profession cannot, even at the same Mastery Tier rank. The class-skills themselves are qualitatively more advanced.
- **Respec Cost:** Overwriting a UNIQUE Profession wipes its class-skills like any other. The body keeps general competence. Rebinding it later requires finding another scroll of that UNIQUE Profession, which is near-impossible. The loss of a UNIQUE Profession is a story-level tragedy.
- **Dark Scroll Version:** A dark scroll carrying a UNIQUE Profession is a forced job-changer — devastating on its own, no extra mechanics needed. Forcing a UNIQUE Profession onto someone gives them a premium class they did not choose; their old class is overwritten (class-skills gone). Forcing a *menial* UNIQUE Profession onto a legendary fighter is the cruelest form: imagine fighting Legolas, then forcing him to class-change into Farmer — his Archer class-skills gone, his body still Legend-tier, but his class is now farming. He survives. He is still dangerous bare-handed. But he is no longer Legolas. That is the devastation. No extra effect needed — the class change IS the weapon.
- **Cultural Status:** A UNIQUE Profession wielder is a faction-level asset. Their death is a geopolitical event. Their recruitment is a diplomatic project. A dark scroll carrying a UNIQUE Profession is a war-crime-grade weapon.

**The Three Tiers of Scroll Rarity (Summary):**
1. **Plain Profession** — a serious windfall. "Swordsman." Most scrolls that exist are Plain — but scrolls are rare, so "most scrolls" is a small number. A Plain scroll in a villager's hands is a fortune, and it turns a hard slash into a strange-light, five-times-impact strike.
2. **Profession + Aspect** — the rare thing a region talks about. "Swordsman of the Wind." Minority of scrolls; reshapes the class-skills further; higher value.
3. **UNIQUE Profession** (no Aspect, ever) — the kind of thing that makes hardened veterans stop and stare. "Swordmaster," "Combat Maid," "Ninja," "Serial Killer," "Paladin." Exceptionally rare; deep-ruin or Continent Lord origin; faction-level event. Higher ceiling; qualitatively more advanced class-skills. The root concept is traceable but the leap can be wild and does not have to make sense. If you find one, even veterans stop and stare.

**Why This Matters:**
- Most people are classless. A plain "Swordsman" is already a windfall — the same sword motion, now with a strange edge-light and amplified impact. "Swordsman of the Wind" is the rare thing a region talks about. "Swordmaster" is the kind of thing that makes even hardened veterans stop and stare. The distinction matters socially and economically.
- A party of three sword-fighters is most likely three classless fighters with drill and steel — their bash is a hard sword-slash, nothing more. If one of them has a plain Swordsman scroll, that one's bash lands with a strange light and five times the force, and the party's edge is that one person. If one of them has an Aspect, that one is the rare thing a region talks about. If one of them is a Swordmaster, that one is a faction-level asset and the story revolves around them.
- Guilds and schools form around Professions (a Swordsman school teaches Swordsman class-skills — and is rare, because classed Swordsmen are rare). A school for a specific Profession+Aspect combo is almost unheard of. A school for a UNIQUE Profession is a one-teacher institution — the wielder is usually the only one alive.
- Scroll-market value: plain Profession scrolls are priced like a family's life savings (see ECONOMY). Aspect scrolls are beyond cash valuation — traded for alliances, marriages, territory. UNIQUE Profession scrolls are beyond cash valuation — traded for cities, dynastic marriages, vassalage.
- Dark scrolls are simple: they are job-changers applied by force. No extra mechanics, no additional effects — the forced class change IS the devastation. A dark scroll carrying a plain menial Profession (Peasant, Farmer, Laborer) is the common weapon: force it onto a classed fighter mid-battle, their combat class is overwritten, their class-skills gone. Imagine fighting a classed Archer — then forcing them to class-change into Farmer. They survive. Their body is still theirs. But their class is gone, and in a world where classes are rare, that loss may never be reversed. No extra effect needed. An Aspect on a dark scroll is rare and deliberate (wrong-Aspect combat profession as sabotage). A UNIQUE Profession on a dark scroll is a war-crime-grade weapon — forcing a Swordmaster onto someone gives them a premium class they did not choose; forcing a menial UNIQUE onto a classed legend is cruelty that wastes a thing the world has almost none of.
- Ex-class memory does NOT carry Aspect or UNIQUE status. A former Swordsman of the Wind who respecs into Swordsman of the Duck keeps general sword-combat reflexes (Legend-tier body) but cannot use any Wind-Swordsman class-skill. A former Swordmaster who overwrites to Farmer keeps Legend-tier body but cannot use any Swordmaster class-skill — and finding another Swordmaster scroll to rebind is near-impossible. The body remembers swords; it does not remember mastery.

## ECONOMY

### ECONOMY -- Currency and Trade
**Currency:** Class scrolls are the high-value currency — and rare enough that most transactions never involve one. Small trade uses **iron bits** (standardized iron rods, ~10cm, stamped with the issuing faction's mark). Iron bits are the daily currency; scrolls are the large-transaction currency, used only for faction-level deals, dowries, ransoms, and the sale of a settlement's surplus.
**Cost of Living (daily, iron bits):**
- Lodging (common room): 2 bits
- Ale/meal (tavern): 1 bit
- Lodging (private room): 5 bits
- Healer visit (basic): 10 bits
- Alchemist service (basic): 20 bits
- Iron weapon (sword): 200 bits
- Master-forged weapon: 1,000+ bits
- Common laborer wage: ~5-8 bits/day
- **Plain Profession scroll (Swordsman, Farmer, Cook, etc.): 20,000-60,000 bits** — a serious windfall; 10-30 years of common wages. Most are inherited, gifted by a patron, or won on an expedition. Open cash sale is rare.
- **Aspect scroll (Swordsman of the Void, etc.): 200,000+ bits — effectively priceless.** Almost never sold for iron bits; traded for alliances, marriages, territory, life-debts. A single Aspect scroll can be a small faction's most valuable holding.
- **UNIQUE Profession scroll (Swordmaster, Surgeon, Chemist, etc.): beyond cash valuation.** A faction-level event. Traded for cities, dynastic marriages, vassalage, pardons, blood-feud settlements. A UNIQUE scroll on the open market reshapes regional politics.

### ECONOMY -- Scroll Market Dynamics
**Drop-scroll (untyped) value:** Lower than typed scrolls — the buyer does not know what class they will bind. Still valuable enough to be worth killing for. Untyped drops are usually sold to alchemists who refine them into typed scrolls; the alchemist takes a steep cut.
**Typed scroll value:** Varies by class demand. Combat classes are always in demand; utility classes less so; rare classes (Mage, Pyrocarver) command premiums that can destabilize local markets.
**Dark scroll value:** Black market only. A dark scroll of a menial profession sells for less than a common typed scroll (low utility); a dark scroll of a combat profession sells for astronomical sums to factions planning conscription or sabotage.
**Seasonal Variation:** Scroll prices spike in the months before the Silent Season (expedition preparation). The Silent Season itself rarely "floods" the market — drop rates are low even on successful expeditions, and most Silent Season hauls are a handful of scrolls for dozens of hunters. A single good haul can depress prices in one city for a month; two good hauls is a glut. Prices recover fast.

### ECONOMY -- Alchemist Economy
**Alchemists are the bottleneck — and the bottleneck is narrow.** A faction's alchemist capacity directly determines its scroll supply, and alchemical crafting is slow, expensive, and failure-prone: a master alchemist produces one plain Profession scroll per week of work and ~100 bits of reagents, with maybe a 60-70% success rate; Aspect scrolls take months and fail more often than not. Most alchemists produce only a handful of scrolls per year. Losing an alchemist (to death, defection, or recruitment by rivals) is a strategic disaster that can take a generation to repair.
**Reagent trade:** Reagents (creature essence, minerals, jungle herbs, volcanic compounds) are traded across faction lines, often through neutral brokers. Reagents are not currency but are high-value trade goods.
**Forbidden reagents:** Reagents used in dark scroll crafting are contraband. Possession is a crime; trafficking is a capital crime. The trade exists anyway; prices are astronomical.

## EVENTS

### EVENT -- The Turning
~150 years ago. Cause unknown, never resolved. Every non-humanoid creature mutated. Civilization collapsed, rebuilt behind walls. See OVERVIEW -- The Turning Calamity.

### EVENT -- The First Binding
The first human to bind a class scroll. Legend says it was a farmer who found a scroll in a mutated creature's corpse and, unable to read, simply held it and wished — and the scroll answered. The farmer's class is unknown; the legend says it was "the first Swordsman," but every martial tradition claims this. The truth is lost. What is known: the first binding was accidental, the first binder was a commoner, and the first class was combat-useful. The walls began to hold after that.

### EVENT -- The Dark Concord (60 years ago)
The only known multi-faction agreement on dark scrolls — and the last confirmed contact between the three continents. It was struck during a Silent Season: for the only time in recorded history, the leviathans went passive with everything else, and ships from all three continents reached a neutral meeting point (location disputed — each continent claims it hosted). The factions present signed a concord banning dark scroll crafting and use. The Concord held for ~30 years. It has been eroding ever since. Every faction that signed it has since been caught using dark scrolls at least once. The Concord is now a polite fiction — invoked publicly, violated privately. No second meeting has ever been possible — the sea death returned with the creatures. The current rising use of dark scrolls is the open secret of the era.

### EVENT -- The Last Rare Find
The last confirmed deep-ruin scroll of extraordinary power was recovered 22 years ago, in the Driftwork (Pastoria). The binder was a Freerider captain who died two years later in a raid. The class was an Archer of the Longshadow — a lost Profession+Aspect combination no living alchemist can reproduce. It died with her. No comparable find has been confirmed since. The exact count of similarly extraordinary scroll-binders across all continents is a state secret in every faction.

### EVENT -- The Approaching Silence
The next Silent Season is due within months. Every faction is preparing — expeditions, trade caravans, and (secretly) dark scroll operations. The Silence is the only time large forces can move through the wilds. Historically, dark scroll attacks spike during every Silent Season. The approaching Silence is the engine's current near-term tension.

## CREATURE -- Generation System
<!-- rag: keyword, triggers: creature, beast, monster, wilds, mutation, drop, scroll drop, creature tier, creature generation -->
**Type:** Procedural creature framework — generate mutated creatures on first encounter. Do not pre-generate a fixed roster.

**Principle:** Every non-humanoid creature in the world is mutated. Mutation is proportional to original biology — predators grew more predatory, scavengers more opportunistic, prey more defensive. Size increase universal but variable (minimum 3x original; some 50x). Most remain animalistic; a few show disturbingly tactical behavior.

**Strength Tiers:** Common · Alpha · Apex · Region Lord · Continent Lord.
- Common: baseline mutated creature. ~0.5-1% scroll drop rate. Most hunts yield nothing.
- Alpha: pack leader or mature specimen. ~3-6% drop rate. A party that bags an Alpha has a real chance.
- Apex: top predator of a region. ~10-20% drop rate. Apex hunts are dangerous and planned; a scroll drop is the hoped-for payoff.
- Region Lord: dominant creature of a territory. ~30-50% drop rate. Region Lord hunts are faction-level expeditions; the drop, if it comes, can settle a city's fortune.
- Continent Lord: legendary creature, one per continent at most. ~60-80% drop rate. Killing a Continent Lord is a continent-wide event; the drop is almost guaranteed, and what it carries can reshape politics for a generation.

**Drop Mechanics:** On creature death, GM rolls drop chance (per tier above). Most hunts end with no scroll — the kill is for meat, hide, territory, or safety, and a scroll drop is a windfall, not an expectation. If drop succeeds, scroll materializes near corpse within minutes. The GM then picks a Profession (and sometimes an Aspect, rarely a UNIQUE Profession — see CLASS GENERATOR). The source creature can weight the GM's choice — a wind-aspected creature tends to drop wind-aspected scrolls; a burrowing creature tends to drop earth-aspected professions — but this is flavor guidance, not a rule. Most drops are plain Professions. Higher-tier creatures have a higher chance of dropping Aspect scrolls or (rarely, for Region Lords and Continent Lords) UNIQUE Profession scrolls. A Common creature can drop a strange and powerful Profession+Aspect; a Continent Lord can drop something mundane. The GM decides.

**Dark Scroll Drop:** ~1-3% of all scroll drops are dark scrolls (rolled after the drop-success roll). More common from corrupted/abyssal-tier creatures (deeper ruins, older sites, plague-zones).

**Tactical Behavior:** Most creatures are animalistic. A minority (perhaps 10%) show pack coordination, ambush planning, or primitive tool use. Region Lords and Continent Lords always show tactical behavior — they are the reason large expeditions do not return.

**Hereditary:** Mutation is hereditary. The world will not revert. This is the post-Turning reality, permanent and unexplained.
