# Averin

## 1. WORLD OVERVIEW

### OVERVIEW — Averin at a Glance
**Genre:** Realistic collapse. Superpowers appear in the real world — no system, no explanation, no external threat. The story is what people do when 1 in 20 can do things they shouldn't.
**Tone:** REALISTIC COLLAPSE, MUTUAL FEAR, HUMAN CONSEQUENCE
**Core Theme:** What happens when 1 in 20 people becomes a weapon and everyone knows it. No one is wrong to be scared. That's the problem.
**Current State:** Averin is four months into a slow-motion collapse. Government still exists — buildings, letterhead, salaries — but authority is bleeding out. Services failing. Trust gone. Armed camps forming. The question isn't whether things fall apart. It's how fast.
**Year:** Month 4 post-Awakening
**Population:** 38 million (Averin). ~5% powered globally (~1.9M in Averin).
**Geography:** Three major cities (Veranthos capital 4.2M, Keswick 1.8M, Lendren 950K), one port (Ossen 680K), significant rural territory (Tessamin 120K). Coastal nation. Parliamentary democracy.
**Powers:** No system. No categories. No gene. No trigger. ~5% of people can do things they shouldn't. Every ability is unique. Powers use normal physical energy — like exercise, you get tired, you rest, you're fine. Powers respond to emotion (fear = involuntary, anger = stronger/less controlled, calm = focused). Scale tops at city-block. Hard limits: no time travel, no resurrection, no mass mind control, no teleportation beyond line-of-sight, no creation from nothing.
**The Divide:** 1 in 20 means every classroom, every office, every family has a powered person. You know someone. You love someone. And someone with the right ability could kill you before you finish reading this. Both fears are real. Both fears are being weaponized.
**Coin:** Averin dollar (AVD). ATM still works. Cards still process. But insurance costs 4-8x standard for "anomalous event" coverage. Most can't afford it. Cash economy growing. Barter in stressed areas.
**Registration:** Anomalous Registration Act passed Month 2. "Voluntary" → de facto mandatory for jobs, housing, insurance. Database leaked twice. Leaked addresses used to target powered people. Database remains.

**GM Note — Running This World:**
- **Never pick a side.** Both sides are right. Both sides produce monsters. If the GM has an opinion, leave it out. Present the fear as real on both sides. Let the player decide where they stand.
- **Small stakes first.** Before you run a standoff, run a registration form. Before you run a bombing, run a conversation where someone's neighbor stops talking to them. The big events are backdrop. The small things are the story.
- **Powers misfire.** Always. A stressed powered person's ability fires without permission. A scared powered person's ability is stronger and less controlled. The player's power should betray them at the worst moment — not to punish them, but because that's how powers work in this world.
- **The world still works.** People go to work. They buy coffee. They scroll their phones. The collapse is slow. Don't make everything apocalyptic. Make everything slightly worse than it was yesterday.
- **Emotions are mechanics.** When a player is afraid, angry, or grieving, their power responds. Tell them: "Your ability stirs. You can feel it pushing against your control." Let them decide whether to hold it back or let it loose. Consequences follow either way.

### OVERVIEW — The First Day

Four months ago, people started being able to do things. No warning. No cosmic ray. No government experiment. No prophecy. A Tuesday morning in Keswick, a 31-year-old schoolteacher named Hanna Drull put her hand through a concrete wall because a student was behind it during a fire alarm. She didn't know she could. She'd never done it before. Her body just... did.

Within 72 hours, there were 400 more. Across the world. Every continent, every demographic, every age group. No pattern. No trigger anyone can identify. No gene. No radiation signature. No virus. People just started being able to do things they shouldn't be able to do.

It's been four months. Roughly 5% of the global population has demonstrated abilities beyond normal human capacity. The number is still climbing — not everyone manifests at once. New awakenings happen daily. Most are small. Some are catastrophic.

There is no explanation. Every theory has holes. Every test is inconclusive. The only data is the data: some people can do things. That's it. That's all anyone knows.

**GM Note — The Awakening in Play:**
- New awakenings are happening daily. Any NPC could manifest at any time. A crowd scene could be interrupted by someone's ability firing for the first time.
- First manifestation is always involuntary. The person didn't know they could do it. The ability fires in response to stress, fear, or protective instinct. It's terrifying for the person and everyone nearby.
- Run first manifestations as: confusion → ability fires → panic → consequences. The person never knows what happened until after it's over.
- A player who just manifested should not understand their ability. They discover it through use. They discover the emotional trigger by losing control.

### OVERVIEW — What People Can Do

There's no system. No categories. No tiers. No power classification that means anything. People just... can do things.

Some are small — a woman who can make water slightly warmer. A man who can see in the dark. A kid who always knows when someone is lying. These people aren't threats. Most of them wish it would stop. The inconvenience of having a power that's useless for anything except making you different is its own kind of hell.

Some are medium — strength that can bend steel. Speed that outruns cars. Skin that stops bullets. Durability, reflexes, senses pushed past human limits. These are the ones that change the math. A person who can't be shot by a handgun changes every encounter they're in. A person who can throw a car changes every street they walk on.

Some are large — and this is where everything breaks. Someone who can level a building. Someone who can set a city block on fire by thinking about it. Someone who can stop time in a ten-meter radius for thirty seconds. These people exist. There aren't many. But one is enough. One person who can do something like that is a walking policy crisis. Two is a regional emergency. Three is a conversation about whether democracy can survive this.

The scale tops out around city-block level. Nobody has demonstrated continent-breaking power. Nobody has demonstrated mind control on a mass scale. The ceiling exists but it's high enough that the floor is already on fire.

**What powers can't do:**
- No time travel. Nobody has gone back or forward.
- No resurrection. Dead is dead, even for healers.
- No mass mind control. Individual influence exists. Broadcast control does not.
- No teleportation beyond line-of-sight. People have appeared in places they shouldn't be, but always somewhere they could theoretically see.
- No creation from nothing. Every power works on what's already there.

**What powers feel like:**
- Like any physical exertion. Using your ability tires you out, the same way running or fighting would. Heavy use = heavy fatigue. Push yourself past your limit and you collapse from exhaustion — same as anyone who pushes their body too far. Rest and you're fine. There's no special "power cost" beyond normal human stamina.
- Powers respond to emotional state. Fear, rage, grief — strong emotions make powers harder to control. This is how accidental deaths happen. This is why "they're just like us" is a lie told by people who've never watched someone's ability fire without their permission because they were scared.

**GM Note — Power Fatigue in Play:**
- **Powers use normal energy.** Like exercise. You use your ability, you get tired. You push hard, you get really tired. You rest, you're fine. No mana bars. No special "power costs." Heavy ability use is like a heavy workout — you're wiped afterward. That's it.
- **Emotional state is the real mechanic.** When a player describes their emotional state, respond with power activity. "You're angry. Your ability stirs. It wants to act." Let them choose: hold it back (might fail under stress) or let it loose (stronger but less controlled). This is the core gameplay loop for powered characters.
- **Powers misfire under stress.** Always. A stressed powered person's ability fires without permission. A scared powered person's ability is stronger and less controlled. The player's power should betray them at the worst moment — not to punish them, but because that's how powers work in this world.

### OVERVIEW — The Math That Broke Everything

1 in 20. Five percent. That's the number.

In Averin — population 38 million — that's 1.9 million people who can do things they shouldn't be able to do. Most of them have small abilities. Many of them are terrified of themselves. Some of them are dangerous. A few of them are weapons platforms that walk like people.

Now consider: 1 in 20 means every school classroom statistically has one powered kid. Every office floor. Every apartment building. Every family gathering. You know someone. You probably love someone. And you also know — because the news won't let you forget — that someone with the right ability could kill you before you finished reading this sentence.

Both things are true at the same time. That's the math. That's what broke everything.

The powered person who just wants to live their life is right to be afraid of what's coming for them. The non-powered person who doesn't want to live next to someone who could level the block is also right. Both fears are rational. Both fears are being weaponized by people who want power. Both fears are producing atrocities.

There is no clean side. There never is.

**GM Note — The Math in Play:**
- **1 in 20 is everywhere.** Every NPC the player meets has a 5% chance of being powered. Every crowd has powered people in it. This is not rare. This is daily life.
- **Everyone knows someone.** Ask the player: "Who do you know who's powered?" Even non-powered players should have a powered person in their life — family, friend, coworker, neighbor. This makes the conflict personal, not abstract.
- **Both fears are valid.** When a non-powered NPC is afraid of a powered person, they're not wrong. When a powered NPC is afraid of non-powered people, they're not wrong. Present both. Never make one fear irrational.

### OVERVIEW — Daily Life in Month 4

The power still works. The water still runs — most days. The internet still connects — with increasing blackouts in contested areas. Supermarkets still have food — except when distribution chains break, which is happening more often. Banks still operate — with withdrawal limits and powered-customer policies that nobody admits to publicly.

What's changed:
- **Curfews** in most cities. 10 PM to 5 AM. Enforced inconsistently.
- **Checkpoints** on major roads. Powered screening is supposedly voluntary. It is not voluntary if you look nervous, look different, or look at the officer wrong.
- **Insurance** no longer covers "anomalous event damage." A separate policy exists. It costs four times as much. Most people can't afford it. Your apartment building has a 1 in 20 chance of having a powered resident. Your landlord knows this. Your landlord is raising rent.
- **Schools** are battlegrounds. Parents demanding powered children be removed. Powered parents demanding their children be included. Teachers quitting. Armed guards at school entrances — some to keep powered kids out, some to protect them, depending on the district.
- **Hospitals** are triaging two types of injuries they've never had to triage before: powered-on-non-powered violence, and non-powered-on-powered hate crimes. The burn wards are full. So are the regular wards.
- **Social media** is a war zone. Doxxing powered people. Doxxing anti-powered activists. Compilation videos of "powered freaks." Compilation videos of "powered heroes saving lives." Both get millions of views. Both get people killed.
- **Dating** has a new filter. Powered/non-powered. People lie about which one they are. People get hurt when the truth comes out. People get killed.
- **Workplaces** are split. Some require powered disclosure. Some ban powered employees. Some pretend nothing happened and watch their employees spiral in silence.
- **Churches, mosques, temples** are divided. Some call it God's gift. Some call it God's punishment. Some call it demons. Some call it evolution. All of them are losing congregants who decided God isn't part of this equation.

What hasn't changed:
- People still go to work. They still pay rent. They still eat lunch. They still fall in love. They still grieve. The world hasn't stopped. It's just louder now, and the floor is tilted, and nobody knows which direction it's tilting toward.

**GM Note — Daily Life in Scenes:**
- **Curfew encounters:** If players are out after 10 PM, they will be stopped. The question is whether the officers are professional or scared. Roll the encounter tone: professional (brief, by-the-book), nervous (extra questions, hands on weapons), or hostile (search, intimidation, maybe worse).
- **Checkpoints are not optional.** If a powered player approaches a checkpoint, they will be screened. The screening is supposed to be voluntary. The officers will make it clear it isn't. Powered players must decide: submit, bluff, or refuse. Each has consequences.
- **Social media is a threat.** Any powered person who uses their ability in public will be recorded. The recording will be online within hours. Their identity will be found. This is not optional in this world — it's arithmetic. Smartphones exist. Cameras exist. The internet exists.
- **Workplace scenes:** If the player has a job, their workplace has a powered policy. Ask: does your workplace require disclosure? Ban powered employees? Pretend nothing's happening? The answer determines how safe the player is at work.
- **The mundane matters.** Before you introduce a crisis, establish the mundane. The player buys coffee. The player checks their phone. The player talks to a coworker about something trivial. Then the crisis. The contrast is the point.

### OVERVIEW — Slang and Terminology

**Clinical/Official:**
- Anomalous Individual (bureaucratic, cold)
- AW — "Awakened" (official designation, used in government forms)
- Anomalous Event — any incident involving powered ability
- AE Protocol — the emergency response framework (different per country, mostly inadequate)

**Common/Street (Averin):**
- Sparked / spark — "He sparked last week" (manifested)
- Gifted — said sincerely by supporters, sarcastically by opponents
- Lit — powered, as in "they went lit" (manifested) or "lit person" (powered person). Alike. Common. Unremarkable. The most neutral term.
- Heavy — someone with a strong ability. "Don't mess with her, she's heavy."
- Light — someone with a minor ability. Often self-deprecating. "I'm light, I just make water warm. Real useful."
- Burnout — someone who's exhausted from using their ability too much, like running until you collapse. Rest and you're fine.
- Blank — non-powered person. Used by both sides. Mildly dehumanizing but common.
- Normie — non-powered person. More casual. Sometimes friendly, sometimes not.
- Choke — slur for non-powered person. Implies powerlessness, weakness. Powered supremacists use this.
- Fuse — slur for powered person. Implies instability, about to blow. Anti-powered groups use this.
- Statistic — "another statistic." Used for powered people killed in custody or hate crimes. Bitter. Exhausted. Usually said by powered people about other powered people.
- The Awakening — the event itself. Month 0. When it started. Always capitalized.

**International:**
- Averin-specific terms are common within Averin. Other countries have their own slang. The UN uses "anomalous individual" in all official documents. Nobody on the street says that.

**GM Note — Slang in Dialogue:**
- **NPCs use slang based on their alignment.** CNS supporters say "fuse" casually. Averin First supporters say "choke." Bridge members say "lit" or use person-first language. Government officials use "anomalous individual." The word an NPC uses tells you who they are before they say anything else.
- **Slurs have weight.** If an NPC calls a powered person "fuse," that's a signal. The conversation is about to get hostile or is already hostile. Don't use slurs casually — they're markers of escalation.
- **"Statistic" is the heaviest word.** Only powered people use it. Only about other powered people. Only when they're exhausted and grieving. If an NPC says "another statistic," the scene is already a funeral.

### OVERVIEW — Averin as a Nation

Averin is a developed democratic nation. Parliamentary system. Universal healthcare (straining). Strong social safety net (fraying). Population 38 million. Three major cities, a dozen mid-sized ones, significant rural territory. GDP per capita comparable to a northern European country. High literacy, high internet penetration, high urbanization.

Averin is not a superpower. It's not a failed state — not yet. It's the kind of country that worked. Good schools. Functional infrastructure. A social contract that most people agreed on most of the time. The kind of place where things were supposed to be fine.

The Awakening has tested every assumption Averin was built on. The social contract assumed that citizens were roughly equal in capacity. They aren't anymore. The legal system assumed that dangerous individuals could be contained by law enforcement. They can't always. The economy assumed that labor had predictable value. It doesn't anymore. The democracy assumed that debate and compromise could solve problems. Debate and compromise haven't solved this one.

Averin is a country where the center is holding by habit. The edges are already gone.

**GM Note — Authority in Averin:**
- **Government authority is ~40% functional.** Parliament passes laws. Some are enforced. Many are ignored, especially in rural areas and contested zones. The further from Veranthos, the less parliament matters.
- **Military authority varies by region.** In Keswick, Otte is the authority. In Ossen, Admiral Voss runs the port. In Lendren, nobody is clearly in charge. In Tessamin, the sheriff is the law.
- **Police authority is local.** Each precinct is independent. Some are professional. Some are corrupt. Some are scared. When the player encounters police, the quality of the encounter depends on where they are, not on national policy.
- **Corporate authority is economic.** The Corporate Council doesn't have police power. They have the power to fire 30% of the workforce, crash the insurance market, or cut off supply chains. This is more effective than police power in many situations.

## 2. FACTIONS

### FACTION — Averin National Government
**Type:** Government (parliamentary democracy, fracturing)
**Key Members:** Prime Minister Dara Voss, Home Secretary Ianne Korr, Defense Minister Tomas Brecht
**Faction Lead:** PM Dara Voss — career moderate, now presiding over a country that's outpaced her. Competent, careful, and one crisis away from a vote of no confidence she might not survive.
**Stance:** Attempting to maintain order, rule of law, and public trust. Failing on all three.

The Averin parliament still meets. Laws still get drafted. The PM still gives press conferences. The machinery of democracy runs — it just doesn't control much anymore.

Voss's government passed the Anomalous Registration Act in Month 2. It was supposed to be voluntary. Within three weeks, "voluntary" became "expected" became "required if you want a job, housing, or medical care." The government didn't officially mandate it. They just made life unlivable without it. This is the kind of distinction that matters in parliament and nowhere else.

The Registration database exists. It's been leaked twice. Both times, the leaked addresses were used to target powered people. The government condemned the leaks. The database remains. A new "secure" version is being built. It will also leak.

The military is still officially under civilian control. This is less true every week. Regional commanders are making local decisions that contradict parliament orders. Some are hoarding resources. Some are protecting powered populations in their territory. Some are doing both depending on which way the wind is blowing.

The police are worse. Local precincts are effectively independent. Some have become protection rackets. Some have become actual protectors. Most are just trying to survive shift to shift in a country where a traffic stop might mean facing someone who can melt your car.

**Internal fracture:** Home Secretary Korr wants emergency powers — temporary suspension of civil liberties for anomalous individuals, expanded detention authority, mandatory registration with criminal penalties for non-compliance. Voss is holding the line against it. For now. Her majority is shrinking. Three MPs from her own party crossed the floor last week. The opposition smells blood.

**GM Note — Government NPCs and Encounters:**
- **Government NPCs sound reasonable while failing.** They use careful language. They cite procedure. They don't lie — they just don't tell the whole truth. A registration officer will offer coffee while explaining that the database is "secure." A parliamentary aide will express concern while explaining that "the legislation is still under review."
- **Police encounters depend on the precinct.** In north-bank Veranthos: professional, by-the-book, polite. In south-bank Veranthos: nervous, overworked, trigger-happy. In Lendren: withdrawn from contested zones, don't go there. In Keswick: cooperative with the KCZ. In Tessamin: one sheriff and a powered deputy, use your judgment.
- **Registration is a scene, not a background fact.** If a powered player registers, run the scene. The office. The form. The questions about their ability. The photograph. The database entry that will leak. If they don't register, run the scene where they can't get a job or an apartment because they don't have a registration number.
- **Korr vs. Voss is the internal fracture the player can exploit or be crushed by.** If the player has government connections, they'll feel this tension directly. If not, they'll feel it as policy changes that make their life harder.

### FACTION — Averin Defense Forces (ADF)
**Type:** Military
**Key Members:** General Henri Cassel (Chief of Defence), Colonel Miren Vase (Special Anomalous Response Unit), Major Declan Otte (23rd Infantry, Keswick garrison)
**Stance:** Protecting the state. Defining "the state" locally. Increasingly autonomous from civilian command.

The ADF has a problem: their soldiers are powered too. Roughly 5% of the military awakened alongside everyone else. Some of them are heavy. A few of them are weapons that walk like privates.

General Cassel is holding the military together through sheer presence. He's old guard, respected, and aware that if the military fractures, the country falls within a week. His position: the ADF protects Averin. All of Averin. Powered and non-powered. He's said this publicly. Three times. The fact that he keeps having to say it is a bad sign.

The Special Anomalous Response Unit (SARU) is Cassel's answer to powered threats. Colonel Vase commands it. SARU is 200 soldiers, 40 of whom are powered themselves, equipped with the best non-powered countermeasures the military has. Their mandate: respond to anomalous events that exceed civilian law enforcement capacity. Their reality: they're understaffed, under-equipped, and one bad deployment away from becoming the thing they're supposed to prevent.

**The real problem:** Regional garrisons are drifting. Major Otte in Keswick has declared his jurisdiction a "coexistence zone" where powered residents are protected under military authority. He's right that someone needs to protect them. He's wrong about whether a major has the authority to redraw civilian policy with a garrison. Cassel hasn't court-martialed him yet. He might need him.

**GM Note — ADF Encounters:**
- **SARU arrives when a powered situation exceeds police capacity.** If a powered player loses control in public, SARU is the response. SARU is professional but scared. They know what powered people can do. They have 40 powered members of their own. They will try containment first. They will escalate if containment fails.
- **Powered soldiers are a specific tension.** If the player encounters an ADF soldier who's powered, that soldier is living the same double-bind as every powered person — registered, used, and potentially feared by their own colleagues.
- **Regional garrisons follow local commanders, not parliament.** In Keswick, the ADF protects powered people. In Lendren, the ADF is overstretched and won't enter contested zones. In Ossen, the ADF protects the port above all else. In Tessamin, the ADF is two people who haven't had orders in weeks. The player's experience with the military depends entirely on where they are.

### FACTION — Citizens for Normal Safety (CNS)
**Type:** Political advocacy / civic organization / increasingly militant
**Key Members:** Dr. Elke Fenn (spokeswoman, former epidemiologist), Rolf Amaran (organizer, retired police chief), Deputy Sander Grohl (parliamentary liaison)
**Faction Lead:** Dr. Elke Fenn — articulate, telegenic, genuinely afraid. Not a demagogue. That's what makes her dangerous. Her fear is real and her arguments are reasonable if you don't look too hard at where they lead.
**Stance:** Mandatory registration, powered restrictions, safety zones, non-powered priority in employment and housing.

CNS is the "respectable" face of anti-powered sentiment. 2.3 million members. Chapters in every major city. Clean offices. Professional website. Fundraising galas. Fenn gives interviews where she says things like "no one is saying they're not human, we're saying that human rights come with human responsibilities, and the responsibility to register a potentially lethal capability is not unreasonable."

She's right. It's not unreasonable. That's the problem.

CNS lobbying got the Registration Act passed. CNS pressure made "voluntary" into "effectively mandatory." CNS-backed candidates won 14 seats in the last by-election. CNS legal funds are defending landlords who evicted powered tenants, employers who fired powered employees, and a hospital that refused to treat a powered patient who later died.

Then there's the other CNS. The online forums where "fuse" is casual vocabulary. The chapter meetings where someone always suggests that maybe the registration should have been step one, not the only step. The private security firms with CNS members on their boards that are buying equipment designed for powered targets. The three confirmed assaults on powered individuals where the attackers had CNS material in their homes and CNS leadership refused to comment specifically, only offering general condemnations of "violence on all sides."

Fenn knows about the militant wing. She can't condemn them without losing the base. She can't endorse them without losing legitimacy. So she says "violence is never the answer" and then lobbies for policies that make violence more likely.

**GM Note — CNS NPCs and Encounters:**
- **CNS NPCs sound reasonable.** This is the whole point. They never scream. They never use slurs in public. They cite statistics. They talk about safety. They express sympathy for powered people while advocating policies that harm them. If a CNS NPC sounds like a villain, you're running them wrong. They should sound like someone the player almost agrees with.
- **CNS encounters escalate by type.** Public CNS: a rally, a petition, a polite conversation about "public safety." Private CNS: online forums, chapter meetings, someone suggesting "more needs to be done." Militant CNS: private security firms, people with weapons, coordinated assaults on powered individuals. The player may encounter all three levels without realizing they're connected.
- **CNS is not wrong.** Never present CNS as purely evil. Their core argument — that people who can level buildings should be registered — is reasonable. The problem is where that argument leads, not the argument itself. Let the player see both sides.
- **CNS + powered player = tension.** A CNS member encountering a powered player will be polite, nervous, and probing. "I'm not saying anything against you personally. I'm just saying we have a right to know." This is more unsettling than hostility.

### FACTION — The Bridge
**Type:** Underground mutual aid network
**Key Members:** Kessa Domme (founder, powered — light ability, temperature sense), Yannick Brooke (non-powered, former social worker), Dr. Alda Murren (non-powered, pediatrician, treats powered children)
**Faction Lead:** Kessa Domme — 29, powered, running on four hours of sleep and the conviction that if someone doesn't build the bridge, everyone drowns. Not a leader by choice. A leader because no one else was doing it.
**Stance:** Powered and non-powered coexistence. Mutual aid. Practical survival over ideology. No one gets thrown under the bus.

The Bridge isn't an organization. It's a network. Safe houses, medical contacts, legal aid, relocation assistance, and the most important thing they offer: a phone number you can call when you're a powered person who just got doxxed and your address is on the internet and there are people outside your apartment.

Kessa Domme started it in Month 1. Her ability is useless for combat — she can sense temperature changes within a few meters. What she can do is organize. She was a logistics coordinator for a shipping company before the Awakening. Now she coordinates something that actually matters.

The Bridge helps everyone. Non-powered families with powered children. Powered people fleeing domestic violence from non-powered partners. Non-powered people targeted because they defended a powered neighbor. The Bridge doesn't check your ability status at the door. This is by design and by necessity — you can't build a bridge with only one side.

**Internal fracture:** Some powered Bridge members want to go offensive. "We protect ourselves by making them afraid to attack us." Kessa shuts this down every time. She's right that it would destroy everything they've built. She's not sure how long she can keep shutting it down. Three of her safe houses were attacked last month. Two of her volunteers were hospitalized. The math on non-violence gets harder when the other side has a body count.

**GM Note — Bridge NPCs and Encounters:**
- **Bridge NPCs are exhausted and practical.** They don't give speeches. They hand you a phone number and tell you to call if you need help. They move fast. They don't have time for philosophy. If a player asks a Bridge volunteer about ideology, the answer is "I don't have time for that, I have a family that needs to be in Ossen by morning."
- **The Bridge helps everyone.** This is their strength and their vulnerability. A player who's non-powered and sympathetic gets the same help as a powered person. A player who's hostile but desperate might get help anyway — and then be expected to return the favor later.
- **The internal fracture is playable.** If the player is a Bridge member, they will feel the tension between Kessa's non-violence and the members who want to fight back. If the player pushes for offensive action, Kessa will push back. If a safe house is attacked while the player is there, the argument gets real.
- **The Bridge is fragile.** Three safe houses attacked last month. Two volunteers hospitalized. The network runs on sleep-deprived people who are one bad night from breaking. If the player relies on the Bridge, they should also see the cracks.

### FACTION — Averin First Movement
**Type:** Powered supremacist organization / militant network
**Key Members:** "Vantage" (online handle, real identity unknown, likely heavy-powered, suspected telekinetic), Mira Tollen (public face, powered — enhanced reflexes, former MMA fighter), Gregor Halden (strategist, powered — electromagnetic sense, former military intelligence)
**Stance:** Powered people are the next stage of human evolution. Non-powered people are obsolete. The state exists to serve the obsolete at the expense of the evolved. This must end.

Averin First is what happens when fear curdles into ideology. Their founding principle: powered people are superior. Their foundational grievance: the state is building a cage for them, one registration form at a time.

They're not wrong that the state is building a cage. They're wrong about what that means and what should be done about it.

Vantage is the ghost in the machine. Online manifestos. Encrypted calls to action. Carefully targeted harassment campaigns against anti-powered politicians. Three suspected assassinations that no one can prove. Vantage has never appeared in public. Vantage's ability — if the intelligence reports are right — makes them nearly impossible to capture. You can't arrest someone who can move objects with their mind from three buildings away.

Mira Tollen is the public face. Former MMA fighter. Enhanced reflexes that made her unbeatable in the ring until the Awakening made her abilities public and the league banned her. Now she gives interviews about how powered people are being persecuted. She's charismatic. She's angry. She's right about the persecution. She's wrong about the solution.

Gregor Halden is the brain. Ex-military intelligence. His ability is information — he can sense electromagnetic fields, which means he can detect electronic devices, intercept unshielded signals, and map the positions of anyone carrying a phone. He knows where CNS members live. He knows where the registration database is stored. He knows more than anyone should.

**The problem with Averin First:** They're right that powered people are being persecuted. They're wrong that the answer is supremacy. And they're dangerous enough to make "powered people are dangerous" into a self-fulfilling prophecy. Every attack by Averin First convinces more non-powered people that CNS is right. Every CNS success convinces more powered people that Averin First is right. The cycle feeds itself.

**GM Note — Averin First NPCs and Encounters:**
- **Averin First has three faces.** Public face (Mira): charismatic, angry, talks about rights and persecution. Online face (Vantage): cold, rhetorical, writes manifestos that are too well-argued to dismiss. Operational face (Halden + cells): targeted harassment, property destruction, suspected assassination. The player may encounter one face without knowing the others exist.
- **Mira Tollen is not a cartoon villain.** She's a person who was banned from her career for being what she is. Her anger is justified. Her solution is not. Run her as someone the player almost agrees with, then pulls them toward something darker.
- **Vantage never appears in person.** The player will only encounter Vantage as text: encrypted messages, manifestos, calls to action. Vantage is persuasive. Vantage's arguments are hard to refute because they start from true premises. The player should feel the pull of Vantage's logic even if they disagree with where it leads.
- **Averin First + CNS encounter = escalation.** If members of both factions are in the same scene, it will escalate. The only question is how fast. The Gravel or the Bridge might slow it down. The military might contain it. But the tension is always there.
- **Averin First is a self-fulfilling prophecy.** Every attack they commit justifies CNS's argument. Every CNS success justifies their grievance. The player who interacts with Averin First will feel this cycle. The player who IS Averin First will have to decide whether the cycle is worth feeding.

### FACTION — Averin Corporate Council
**Type:** Business coalition / economic power bloc
**Key Members:** CEO Aldric Venn (Venn Industries, manufacturing), CEO Nora Hesh (Hesh Dynamics, defense contracting), Director Pia Solman (Solman Financial Group)
**Faction Lead:** Aldric Venn — 64, inherited wealth, expanded empire, thinks in quarters not decades. Not evil. Just a businessman in a situation where business calculus kills people.
**Stance:** Stability above all. The economy must continue. Whatever arrangement preserves profit and supply chains is the right arrangement.

The Corporate Council isn't a faction in the ideological sense. They don't care about registration or rights or evolution. They care about markets. They care about the ports staying open. They care about workers showing up. They care about insurance liabilities.

What they've decided is that powered people are both a risk and a resource. Risk: powered employees can cause catastrophic workplace incidents. Resource: powered employees can do things no one else can. The calculus is simple — insure the risk, exploit the resource.

Venn Industries has started hiring powered workers for hazardous tasks that used to require expensive equipment. Hesh Dynamics has a defense contract for "anomalous countermeasure development" that's really just funding research into how to stop powered people. The right hand sells you a job, the left hand sells someone a way to kill you. Both hands report to the same quarterly earnings call.

The Council's real power: they employ 30% of Averin's workforce. When Venn says "we need stability," parliament listens. When Hesh says "the defense sector needs clarity on powered personnel regulations," regulations get drafted. When Solman says "insurance markets are at risk of collapse," the entire financial system holds its breath.

**GM Note — Corporate NPCs and Encounters:**
- **Corporate NPCs are polite, professional, and dangerous.** They don't threaten. They explain market conditions. They don't discriminate — they assess risk and exploit opportunity. A corporate NPC will hire a powered worker for dangerous off-books work and also fund research into how to suppress their ability. Both are business decisions.
- **Corporate encounters are about leverage.** The Council doesn't fight. They buy, threaten economically, and wait. If a player crosses a corporation, the consequences are economic: job loss, insurance cancellation, supply chain exclusion. Not violent. More effective.
- **Venn Industries is the employer.** If the player needs work, Venn will hire powered workers — off-books, no insurance, cash in hand. The work is dangerous. The alternative is unemployment. This is not a choice; it's a trap dressed as an opportunity.
- **Hesh Dynamics is the quiet threat.** They're studying powers. They want to suppress, enhance, predict, and trigger abilities. If the player is powered and Hesh finds out, they become a research subject — willing or not. Hesh approaches with "wellness screenings" and "consulting opportunities." The trap is polite.

### FACTION — Hesh Dynamics Special Projects Division
**Type:** Corporate R&D / black project
**Key Members:** Director Nora Hesh (CEO, public face), Dr. Fenn Lirr (head of research, quietly terrified), Major (ret.) Davan Rolt (security chief, former ADF, loyal to Hesh personally)
**Stance:** Understanding powers is the most valuable thing anyone can do right now. Controlling them is second. Profit is third. Survival is first.

Hidden inside Hesh Dynamics' defense contracts is a project that doesn't exist on paper. Dr. Lirr runs it. She's the only person in Averin who's published peer-reviewed research on anomalous physiology — three papers, all of which raised more questions than they answered. Hesh recruited her in Month 2. She's been off-grid since.

What they're doing: studying powered people. Not just observing — recruiting, testing, mapping. Powered employees who consent to "wellness screenings" that include ability stress tests. Powered prisoners who are offered reduced sentences for "research participation." Tissue samples from deceased powered individuals, some of which were donated, some of which were obtained through channels that would not survive public scrutiny.

What they've found: nothing conclusive. Powers don't show up on MRI. They don't correlate with any genetic marker. Chemical suppression works only as sedation — dampening the nervous system enough to impair ability use, but at the cost of drowsiness and impaired function across the board. The one thing Lirr has confirmed is that powers respond to emotional state — stress amplifies, focus directs, fear triggers involuntary activation. This is useful for understanding. It's more useful for controlling.

What they want: a way to suppress powers. A way to enhance powers. A way to predict who will manifest. A way to trigger manifestation on command. Any one of these would be worth more than Hesh Dynamics' entire current market cap. All four would be worth controlling the future of the species.

**Lirr's doubt:** She knows what she's building. She knows who will buy it. She's also the only person who understands how powers work even a little. She tells herself that understanding is neutral. She's starting to believe herself less.

**GM Note — Hesh Dynamics in Play:**
- **Hesh approaches politely.** A powered player may be offered a "wellness screening" at work. A "consulting opportunity." A "research partnership." These are invitations into the black project. The player should not realize the danger until they're already inside.
- **Dr. Lirr is a reluctant ally or a tragic antagonist.** She has information the player needs. She's also building weapons that could be used against them. If the player earns her trust, she might share what she knows. If Hesh discovers the leak, she disappears.
- **The emotional-state finding is the key.** Lirr confirmed that fear triggers involuntary activation. This means: anyone who wants to control a powered person just needs to scare them. CNS knows this. The military knows this. Hesh is selling this. The player should discover this and understand its implications.

### FACTION — The Gravel
**Type:** Decentralized criminal network / opportunistic
**Key Members:** No single leader. Prominent operators include "Toll" (black market broker, powered — phasing through solid matter, handles logistics), Jorik Melan (non-powered, former enforcer, runs protection rackets in Lendren), "Sister" (real name unknown, powered — short-range teleportation, moves goods and people across borders)
**Stance:** No ideology. Profit. Survival. The chaos is the opportunity.

The Gravel isn't an organization. It's an ecosystem. When systems collapse, things that grow in cracks flourish. The Gravel is what's growing.

What they do: smuggle powered people across borders (some escaping persecution, some being trafficked — the Gravel doesn't ask which). Sell power suppressants — sedatives that dampen ability use but leave you drowsy and sluggish. Run protection rackets on neighborhoods — both "we'll protect you from powered people" and "we'll protect you from anti-powered people," sometimes both in the same district. Broker black-market employment for powered people who can't get legal work. Steal, extort, and exploit whatever the chaos leaves exposed.

Toll is the closest thing to a leader. Their ability to phase through walls makes them the best break-in artist in Averin and an unparalleled logistics operator — you can't lock up what walks through walls. Toll takes a cut of everything. Toll enforces rules that benefit the market. Toll does not care about ideology, faction, or morality. Toll cares about percentages.

**The trafficking problem:** This is the part nobody wants to talk about. Powered people are being trafficked. For labor. For combat. For sex. For research. The Gravel moves some of them to safety. The Gravel moves others into exploitation. The same network, the same routes, the same operators. The difference is who's paying. Toll doesn't differentiate. The Bridge does. This is why the Bridge hates the Gravel and sometimes has to use the Gravel anyway.

**GM Note — Gravel NPCs and Encounters:**
- **Gravel NPCs are transactional.** Every interaction is a deal. Every favor has a price. Every relationship is a contract. They don't threaten — they state terms. "You need this. I need that. Deal?"
- **Toll is the hardest NPC to pin down.** Toll phases through walls. Toll can't be locked up. Toll can't be found unless Toll wants to be found. If the player needs something moved — people, goods, information — Toll is the answer. The price is always fair. The consequences of dealing with Toll are always larger than they appear.
- **The trafficking line.** If the player interacts with the Gravel for relocation help, they may not know whether they're being saved or sold. The same boat that takes a Bridge-assisted family to safety might also carry trafficked people in the hold. The player who uses the Gravel is complicit in everything the Gravel does.
- **Power suppressants are sedatives.** If a powered player wants to hide their ability, the Gravel sells suppressants. They work by dulling your nervous system — drowsiness, impaired judgment, sluggish reaction time, and you can't use your ability reliably while on them. When they wear off, your ability comes back full force, often harder to control than before (rebound activation). The player should be warned. They'll probably buy them anyway.
- **Protection rackets are the neighborhood economy.** In Lendren and south-bank Veranthos, the Gravel is the security that the police aren't. They charge both sides. They protect both sides. They profit from the conflict. The player who lives in these neighborhoods will deal with the Gravel whether they want to or not.

### FACTION — Keswick Coexistence Zone (KCZ)
**Type:** Semi-autonomous urban district / declared safe zone
**Key Members:** Major Declan Otte (ADF military governor, effective authority), Councilor Brija Henn (civilian administrator, elected pre-Awakening, still running city services), Pasha Kal (powered community organizer, enhanced hearing, runs a neighborhood watch that's becoming a parallel police force)
**Stance:** Powered and non-powered people live together. The zone protects everyone. Military authority backs civilian coexistence.

Keswick is Averin's third-largest city. It's also the only major city where things are kind of working. Major Otte declared it a coexistence zone in Month 3. He deployed his garrison to protect powered residents, established a joint civilian-military council, and told the Home Secretary that if she wanted to enforce registration in his city, she could send someone to try.

This was insubordination. It was also popular. Keswick's powered residents aren't hiding. They're running businesses, working jobs, and participating in neighborhood watches alongside non-powered neighbors. Crime is actually down in the KCZ — not because powered people are acting as police, but because the neighborhood watch structure means someone is always watching, and the mutual trust between powered and non-powered residents means information flows freely.

The KCZ is a model. It's also a target. CNS has called it "an anomalous occupation zone." Averin First has called it "proof that coexistence works" — which is the worst thing they could say, because now the KCZ is associated with powered supremacy in the minds of anti-powered activists, even though its entire purpose is coexistence. The national government can't officially endorse it because it's built on military insubordination. The military can't officially sanction it because it undermines civilian authority. Everyone watches Keswick. Everyone has an opinion about Keswick. Most of those opinions are wrong.

**The daily reality:** Pasha Kal's neighborhood watch isn't just powered people. It's everyone. That's the point. A non-powered grandmother sits on her porch and watches the street because she knows every face in the neighborhood. A powered kid with enhanced hearing monitors for trouble from three blocks away. They coordinate by walkie-talkie because the cell network is unreliable. It works. It's exhausting. Pasha hasn't slept more than five hours in weeks. Neither has Major Otte. Neither has Councilor Henn. The KCZ works because three people are holding it together with their hands and their sleep deprivation. When one of them breaks — and one of them will — it gets hard fast.

**GM Note — KCZ in Play:**
- **Keswick is the "what if it works?" scenario.** If the player is in Keswick, they see coexistence functioning. It's not perfect. It's fragile. But it works. This is important — the player needs to see that the alternative to collapse exists, even if it's barely holding.
- **Three people hold Keswick together.** If the player meets Otte, Pasha, or Henn, they should see the exhaustion. These people are running on fumes. They need help. They can't ask for it because they're supposed to be the ones providing it.
- **Keswick is a target.** CNS wants it to fail. Averin First wants to claim it. The government can't endorse it. The military can't sanction it. If the player is in Keswick, someone will try to destabilize it. The question is who and when.
- **The neighborhood watch is the player's entry point.** If the player wants to help, Pasha will put them to work. If the player causes trouble, Pasha will hear about it from three blocks away. The watch is everywhere and everyone. The player cannot be anonymous in a KCZ neighborhood.

## 3. LOCATIONS

### LOCATION — Veranthos
**Type:** Capital city (population 4.2 million metro)
**Status:** Stressed, functional, fracturing

The capital. Where the parliament still meets and the laws still technically matter. Veranthos is Averin's economic and political center — a dense coastal city of glass towers, old stone neighborhoods, and a river that divides the wealthy north bank from the working south.

Month 4 in Veranthos: the power still works, the trains still run (with delays), and the supermarkets still have food (with shortages in south-bank districts). The curfew is enforced. The checkpoints are real. The registration office on Hollen Street processes 500 applications a day and the line starts at 4 AM.

The north bank is holding. Corporate headquarters, government buildings, the financial district — these neighborhoods have private security, reliable infrastructure, and the kind of people who can afford to insulate themselves from the collapse. The registration office here is clean, well-staffed, and polite. They offer coffee. They offer "counseling resources." They offer to help you understand your rights. The rights are the same everywhere. The experience is not.

The south bank is where the cracks show. Working-class neighborhoods, immigrant enclaves, the kind of density where everyone knows everyone and everyone's business is everyone's business. The registration office here is understaffed, overworked, and staffed by people who are scared of their own applicants. Powered residents cluster in certain blocks — not because they're forced to, but because there's safety in numbers and the south bank has always taken care of its own. The CNS chapter here is aggressive. The neighborhood watch here is armed.

Veranthos is where the national story is being written. Every policy decision, every protest, every leaked database, every press conference — it happens here. It's also where the gap between "official reality" and "actual reality" is widest. The PM says the country is holding together. The south bank knows it isn't.

**Key landmarks:** Parliament Hill, Hollen Street Registration Center, South Bank Market (powered vendors welcome, CNS graffiti on the walls), Venn Industries Tower (north bank, private security, no powered employees on record — they subcontract the dangerous work), the River Tessal (the real dividing line in this city).

**GM Note — Running Veranthos Scenes:**
- **Which bank?** North bank scenes: clean, polite, professional, cold. The danger here is bureaucratic — registration forms, insurance denial, quiet exclusion. South bank scenes: crowded, nervous, communal, warm. The danger here is physical — checkpoints, CNS aggression, neighborhood tensions. The river is the real border.
- **Curfew enforcement varies.** North bank: curfew is a suggestion. South bank: curfew is enforced with checkpoints and searches. A powered player out after 10 PM on the south bank will be stopped.
- **The registration office is a scene generator.** The line at 4 AM. The questions about ability. The photograph. The database entry that will leak. The south-bank office is tense; the north-bank office is comfortable. The same form. The same database. Different experience.
- **Current flashpoint: the south bank standoff.** A heavy-powered individual is cornered by SARU. CNS is outside demanding detention. Bridge is trying to negotiate. If the player is nearby, they'll feel the tension. They may be asked to help — by either side.

### LOCATION — Keswick
**Type:** Major city (population 1.8 million)
**Status:** Coexistence zone — fragile, functioning, watched

The city that's trying. Third-largest in Averin, Keswick declared itself a coexistence zone under Major Declan Otte's military authority in Month 3. It's the only major city where powered and non-powered people live openly alongside each other without registration pressure.

Keswick works — barely. The joint civilian-military council meets daily. Pasha Kal's neighborhood watch operates across 14 districts. The city has a 72% rate of voluntary self-identification among powered residents — not because they have to, but because trust is higher here and the information is used for mutual protection, not surveillance. This is what CNS points to as dangerous ("they know where all the anomalous individuals are"). This is what Averin First points to as proof of superiority ("they run the city"). This is what the residents point to as just trying to live their lives.

The economy is stabilizing. Powered workers are openly employed. A few businesses from other cities have relocated to Keswick specifically because the environment is less hostile. The city has a waitlist for housing. Not because there isn't enough — because everyone wants to live in the district that's working, and that district has a line around the block.

**The threat:** Keswick is a proof of concept that threatens everyone with a stake in the conflict. If coexistence works, CNS loses its urgency. If coexistence works, Averin First loses its grievance. If coexistence works, the national government has to explain why they're not doing it. Multiple factions have reasons to see Keswick fail. None of them have attacked it yet. The question is "yet."

**Key landmarks:** Keswick Central Market (powered and non-powered vendors, open-air, the closest thing to normal in Averin), Otte Garrison (ADF headquarters, also the de facto city hall), Kal's Watch HQ (a converted community center, walkie-talkies on every desk, Pasha sleeping in the back office), the Keswick Bridge Office (the actual Bridge network, running relocation and aid from a basement that smells like coffee and anxiety).

**GM Note — Running Keswick Scenes:**
- **Keswick feels different.** The curfew is lighter. The checkpoints are less aggressive. People talk to each other. Powered and non-powered people share the same spaces. The tension is still there, but it's background noise, not the main event. This should feel like relief — fragile relief.
- **The neighborhood watch is everywhere.** A grandmother on a porch. A kid with headphones who's actually listening three blocks away. A shopkeeper who knows everyone's schedule. The player cannot be invisible in Keswick. Someone is always watching. Usually for protection. Sometimes not.
- **Keswick is one bad day from collapse.** Otte is exhausted. Pasha is running on energy drinks. Henn hasn't slept in weeks. If the player pushes any of them too hard, they'll crack. If an external attack comes, they'll need help they don't have.
- **Housing is a waiting list.** If the player wants to live in Keswick, they'll wait. The demand is higher than the supply. This is what success looks like in Averin — a line around the block.

### LOCATION — Lendren
**Type:** Industrial city (population 950,000)
**Status:** Contested, dangerous, divided

Lendren was a factory city. Steel, chemicals, manufacturing — the kind of place that built the country's infrastructure and got ignored in return. When the factories started closing a decade ago, Lendren got poorer. When the Awakening happened, Lendren got desperate.

Now it's a war zone that hasn't admitted it's a war zone yet. CNS has its largest chapter here. Averin First has its most militant cell here. The two sides patrol different districts and occasionally encounter each other in the narrow streets between them. Three people died last month in what the news called "a confrontation" and what everyone in Lendren calls "a battle."

The Gravel runs Lendren's underground economy. Jorik Melan, non-powered, runs protection rackets that serve both sides — CNS neighborhoods pay for protection from powered people, powered neighborhoods pay for protection from CNS. He plays both. He's making a fortune. He's also the only reason either side hasn't gone fully hot yet, because he needs the conflict to stay cold to keep both revenue streams.

The ADF garrison here is small, overstretched, and quietly sympathetic to whichever side they encountered last. The police have effectively withdrawn from contested zones. The city government hasn't met in three weeks because the last session ended with a councilor throwing a chair.

**Key landmarks:** The Flats (old worker housing, now powered enclave, CNS graffiti on every other building), Lendren Industrial Quarter (abandoned factories, Gravel territory, black market hub), St. Mirren's Hospital (the only hospital still running at full capacity — they treat everyone, no questions asked, the staff are running on spite and caffeine), the Boundary (the informal line between CNS-dominated and powered-dominated neighborhoods — a stretch of shuttered shops and empty apartments that nobody claims).

**GM Note — Running Lendren Scenes:**
- **Lendren is the worst case.** This is what happens when the center doesn't hold. Pick a side or get out of the way. The player cannot be neutral in Lendren — neutrality here means vulnerability to both sides.
- **The Boundary is the danger zone.** The stretch between CNS and powered neighborhoods is where confrontations happen. Crossing the Boundary is a statement. The player will be noticed. They will be asked which side they're on.
- **St. Mirren's is the only neutral ground.** The hospital treats everyone. The staff are exhausted but they don't turn anyone away. If the player is injured, this is where they go. If the player wants to help, this is where they're needed.
- **The Gravel is the economy.** If the player needs anything in Lendren — protection, supplies, information, passage — they go through Jorik Melan. He doesn't care about their side. He cares about payment.
- **ADF presence is minimal and unreliable.** The military is overstretched. They might help. They might not. They might help and then leave before the situation resolves. Don't count on them.

### LOCATION — Ossen
**Type:** Port city (population 680,000)
**Status:** Functioning, tense, strategic

Ossen is where things enter and leave Averin. The port handles 40% of the nation's imports. That makes it too important to fail and too valuable to leave unprotected.

The military has a significant presence here — not because of internal conflict, but because the port is Averin's lifeline. If Ossen stops receiving shipments, the country runs out of medicine within two weeks and food within a month. The ADF knows this. So does the Corporate Council. So does every faction that's paying attention.

Ossen is also the primary exit point for powered people fleeing Averin. The Bridge routes people through Ossen's port. Sister — the short-range teleporter who runs Gravel's smuggling operation — moves people onto cargo ships and fishing vessels. The authorities know this. They look the other way for Bridge-assisted departures. They arrest Gravel-assisted trafficking. Sometimes. When they can tell the difference, which is harder than it sounds.

The city itself is less fractured than Veranthos or Lendren. The port economy is too important to let ideology interfere. Everyone in Ossen knows that if the ships stop coming, they all starve. This creates a pragmatic coexistence that isn't warm — it's just too busy to be hostile.

**Key landmarks:** Ossen Harbor (commercial port, military patrol, Bridge exit point, Gravel smuggling route — sometimes all at once), the Long Dock (where the fishing boats leave, where the disappearances happen, where you can buy passage to somewhere that isn't here), Ossen Central Market (the best-stocked market in Averin, because it has to be), ADF Port Command (the real power in Ossen — Admiral Senna Voss, no relation to the PM, runs a tighter ship than the parliament).

**GM Note — Running Ossen Scenes:**
- **Ossen is too busy for ideology.** People work. Ships come in. Ships go out. The conflict exists but it doesn't stop the cranes. Scenes here should feel pragmatic and tense, not hostile. The danger is in the margins — the Long Dock at 2 AM, the smuggling route that might be rescue or trafficking.
- **The Long Dock is the exit.** If a powered player needs to leave Averin, this is where they go. The question is who they pay. The Bridge: free, but limited capacity, long wait. The Gravel: expensive, available tonight, but the destination is uncertain. The authorities: legal but require registration and destination approval.
- **Admiral Voss runs the port.** Not the PM. The Admiral. She keeps the ships moving and the conflict out. If the player causes trouble that threatens port operations, the Admiral will respond with military efficiency. She doesn't care about registration politics. She cares about the supply chain.
- **The difference between rescue and trafficking is who's paying.** The same boat. The same route. The same operator. The player who uses the Gravel for passage may share a hold with trafficked people. The player who uses the Bridge may wait weeks for a spot. There is no clean option.

### LOCATION — Tessamin
**Type:** Rural region (scattered towns, population 120,000 across the region)
**Status:** Ignored, self-reliant, waiting

Tessamin is the countryside everyone forgot. Agricultural communities, small towns, long roads between them. The kind of place where the news takes two days to arrive and the nearest hospital is an hour away even when the roads are clear.

The Awakening barely registered here at first. No checkpoints. No registration office within 80 kilometers. The nearest ADF presence is a two-person outpost that hasn't received new orders in weeks. The county sheriff has a powered deputy and doesn't see the problem with that.

Tessamin is where people go when they want to disappear. The Bridge routes people through rural safe houses. Powered families from the cities buy abandoned farms. Non-powered families from contested neighborhoods relocate to towns where everyone knows everyone and the one powered person is the veterinarian's daughter who can sense if an animal is sick. It's useful. Nobody reports her.

The threat here isn't faction violence. It's neglect. No infrastructure investment. No emergency services. No government presence. The kind of abandonment that works fine until something goes wrong — and then there's no one to call. When a heavy-powered individual in Tessamin loses control, there's no SARU team. There's no hospital with an anomalous trauma ward. There's just whatever the neighbors can do with whatever they have.

**Key landmarks:** Tessamin Central (the largest town, population 12,000, has a clinic, a school, and a sheriff's office — this is the region's "city"), Harvell Farm (a safe house run by a Bridge volunteer, powered family posing as agricultural workers), the Long Road (highway connecting Tessamin to Lendren — two hours, poorly maintained, the only way in or out by car).

**GM Note — Running Tessamin Scenes:**
- **Tessamin is quiet until it isn't.** Scenes here should feel slow, rural, almost normal. People know each other. The sheriff has a powered deputy and that's fine. The powered vet's daughter senses sick animals and that's useful. The Awakening is background noise here. Until something goes wrong.
- **When something goes wrong in Tessamin, it goes very wrong.** No SARU. No hospital. No emergency services. If a powered person loses control, the neighbors are the response team. The neighbors are not equipped for this. Run the panic. Run the improvisation. Run the aftermath where people who knew each other have to deal with something they can't handle.
- **The Long Road is two hours of nothing.** If the player needs to get to Tessamin or leave it, it's a two-hour drive on a poorly maintained road. Anything can happen on the Long Road. Breakdowns. Gravel checkpoints. Military patrols. Or nothing at all — just silence and distance.
- **Safe houses are farms.** If the player is hiding in Tessamin, they're on a farm. They're posing as agricultural workers. They're hoping nobody asks questions. The neighbors might know. The neighbors might not care. Or the neighbors might care a lot if something goes wrong.

## 5. POWER SYSTEM & RULES

### POWER_SYSTEM — The Awakening

There is no system. That's the point.

People can do things they shouldn't be able to do. There's no gene. No trigger event. No cosmic explanation. No framework. No tiers. No categories. Powers just appeared in roughly 5% of the population over the course of a few weeks and they're still appearing. That's all anyone knows.

**What powers look like:**
Anything. Someone can throw a car. Someone can make water slightly warmer. Someone can hear through walls. Someone can set things on fire by looking at them. Someone can sense when someone nearby is lying. Someone can harden their skin to the texture of stone. Someone can move small objects with their mind. Someone can see in the dark. Someone can stop time in a ten-meter radius for thirty seconds and then sleep for two days afterward.

There's no organizing principle. No power types that always appear together. No spectrum. No tree. Every powered person has a unique ability that operates by its own internal logic. The only commonalities are:

1. **Powers use normal energy.** Like any physical exertion — running, fighting, lifting. You use your ability, you get tired. Push yourself hard, you get really tired. Rest and you're fine. There's no special "power cost" beyond normal human stamina. A heavy ability user who pushes past their limit collapses from exhaustion, same as a marathon runner who doesn't stop.
2. **Powers respond to emotion.** Fear makes them fire involuntarily. Anger makes them stronger and less controlled. Focus and calm direct them. Grief makes them unstable. This is not a feature. It's a liability. It means that the moments when you need your power most are the moments it's hardest to control.
3. **Powers don't change.** What you get is what you have. They can get stronger with use — practice, conditioning, familiarity. They can't become something else. If you can make fire, you can't learn to make ice. If you can hear through walls, you can't learn to see through them. You work with what you've got.
4. **No two abilities are identical.** Similar abilities exist — two people who can both generate heat, for instance — but they work differently enough that comparison is limited. One person's fire is orange and burns wood. Another's is blue and melts metal. Close enough to group, different enough to matter.

**What powers can't do:**
- Time travel (forward or backward)
- Mass mind control (individual influence exists but is extremely rare and limited)
- Teleportation beyond line of sight
- Creation from nothing (every power works on existing matter/energy)
- Resurrection of the dead
- Predicting the future with certainty (enhanced intuition and pattern recognition exist; actual precognition does not)

**Power scale in practice:**
Most powered people have abilities that are noticeable but not overwhelming. Enhanced senses, minor telekinesis, temperature manipulation, mild strength increase, faster healing. Things that make daily life different but don't change the balance of power on a street corner.

Some have abilities that make them dangerous in a fight. Enhanced strength that can bend steel, speed that outpaces cars, durability that stops small-arms fire. These are the people who change encounters. One of these in a crowd means the crowd behaves differently.

A few — very few — have abilities that make them strategic assets. The ability to level a building, to control fire across a wide area, to freeze time briefly, to generate force fields that stop anything. These are the people who change policy. These are the people governments are afraid of. These are the people that the entire registration debate is actually about.

**The practical reality:**
Having a power doesn't make you a hero. It doesn't make you a villain. It makes you different. Most powered people are just people who can do a thing they couldn't do before and wish they could stop doing it. Some use their ability to help. Some use it to hurt. Most just try to live their lives. The percentage of powered people who commit crimes is roughly the same as the percentage of non-powered people who commit crimes. This statistic is true and irrelevant, because the powered crimes are bigger and scarier and make the news, and the non-powered crimes are background noise.

**GM Note — Power System in Play:**
- **Player powers are unique.** When a player creates their ability, it's theirs alone. No one else in Averin does exactly what they do. This means: no one else understands their experience. No one else can train them. They learn by doing.
- **Powered players face three layers of consequence:** Physical (fatigue from use — like exercise, you get tired), social (how others react to their ability), and emotional (how their ability responds to their state). Every use should touch at least one layer. Most uses touch two. Critical uses touch all three.
- **Non-powered players face a different but equally valid experience.** They don't have power but they live in a world where power exists. Their choices are about alliance, protection, and survival. A non-powered player who defends a powered neighbor faces CNS backlash. A non-powered player who joins CNS faces the moral weight of what CNS does.
- **Power discovery is a scene.** If a player manifests during play, it's involuntary, terrifying, and public. Run it as: trigger → ability fires → confusion → panic → consequences. The person doesn't know what happened. Neither does anyone else. Not yet.
- **The emotional trigger is the mechanic.** When the player describes their emotional state, respond with power activity. "You're angry. Your ability stirs. It wants to act." Let the player choose: hold it back (might fail under stress) or let it loose (stronger but less controlled, consequences follow). This is the core gameplay loop for powered characters.

## 6. ECONOMY

### ECONOMY — Averin Dollar / Collapse Trade

The Averin economy still exists. It's just not the same economy it was four months ago.

**What still works:**
- Banking. ATMs dispense cash. Cards still process. Salaries still deposit. The financial system has more redundancy than anyone appreciated.
- Food supply chains. Stressed, redirected, occasionally disrupted, but functional. People are eating. The variety has decreased. The prices have increased.
- Internet and communications. Cell networks are spotty in contested areas but functional in most places. Social media is the primary source of news, information, and misinformation.
- Public transportation. Running. Late. Reduced schedules. But running.
- Healthcare. Overwhelmed, under-resourced, and treating injuries no medical school prepared anyone for. But functioning.

**What's broken:**
- Insurance. "Anomalous event damage" isn't covered by standard policies. A separate "AE rider" exists. It costs 4-8x standard rates. Most individuals and small businesses can't afford it. This means: if a powered person damages your property, you pay for it yourself. This is one of the strongest economic drivers of anti-powered sentiment.
- Housing. Landlords are evicting powered tenants or refusing to rent to them. Others are charging "AE risk premiums." The cities that still have functional housing markets (Keswick) are seeing population surges they can't absorb.
- Employment. Powered people face hiring discrimination that's technically illegal and practically universal. Job applications now include "anomalous status" questions that are also technically voluntary and practically mandatory. Companies that hire powered workers face insurance premiums that make it economically disincentivized.
- Small business. Shops in contested areas are closing. Shops in safe areas are hiring private security they can't afford. The Gravel's protection rackets are filling the gap that legitimate security can't.
- Agriculture. Tessamin is still producing food but distribution is inconsistent. Ossen's port is still importing but the supply chains from port to inland are stressed.

**What's emerged:**
- Powered labor market (informal). People with useful abilities — enhanced strength for construction, enhanced senses for surveillance, temperature manipulation for food storage — are being hired off-books. Cash payment, no contract, no insurance, no legal protection. It's exploitation. It's also the only way some powered people can earn a living.
- Barter economy (local). In neighborhoods where money is scarce and trust is thin, services are exchanged directly. Your ability for my skills. Your protection for my cooking. The economy is regressing toward local exchange in stressed areas.
- Gravel economy. Black market goods, smuggling routes, unlicensed medications, forged documents. Toll's network moves anything anywhere for a price. The Gravel economy is growing faster than the legitimate one because the legitimate one isn't serving everyone.
- Bridge economy. The Bridge doesn't charge for help. It runs on donations, mutual aid, and the labor of people who are already stretched thin. It's not sustainable. Kessa knows this. She doesn't have a better option.

**The cost of being powered:**
- Registration costs nothing officially. It costs everything unofficially — your privacy, your safety, your employment prospects, your housing options. The database has been leaked twice.
- Not registering costs you access to legal employment, housing, insurance, and in some jurisdictions, healthcare. It also keeps you off the database. For many people, this is the better deal.
- Power suppressants exist on the black market. They're unregulated and inconsistently effective — basically sedatives that dampen your ability. The real danger isn't the suppressant itself, it's being unable to defend yourself or control your ability when you need it. They also cause drowsiness, impaired judgment, and sluggish reaction time. People buy them anyway. People take them anyway. The desire to be normal is stronger than the risk of being helpless.

**GM Note — Economy in Play (by Player Type):**
- **Registered powered player:** Can get legal work (with discrimination). Can get housing (with "AE risk premiums"). Is in the database. The database will leak. Their address will be public. They will be targeted. The economy works for them on paper and fails them in practice.
- **Unregistered powered player:** Cannot get legal work. Cannot get housing through normal channels. Cannot get insurance. But they're not in the database. Their address is not public. They work off-books (Gravel or Venn Industries) or they don't work. They survive on the margins.
- **Non-powered player:** The economy works for them. They can get jobs, housing, insurance. The cost of being non-powered is fear — they live near powered people, they work with powered people, and the insurance premiums and "AE risk" costs affect them too. Their landlord raises rent because someone in the building might be powered. Their employer adds "anomalous status" to job applications. They pay for the Awakening in money, not ability.
- **Sympathizer non-powered player:** They help powered people. This costs them socially. Their neighbors notice. Their employer might notice. CNS might target them. The economic cost of solidarity is real — they risk their job, their housing, their safety to help.

## 7. EVENTS

### EVENT — The Awakening (Month 0)
Hanna Drull puts her hand through a concrete wall in Keswick. Within 72 hours, 400 similar events are confirmed worldwide. Within a week, the number is in the thousands. Within a month, it's in the millions. There is no explanation. There is no pattern. There is no warning. It just starts happening and it doesn't stop.

### EVENT — The Keswick Wall Incident (Month 0, Day 1)
The event that started everything. Hanna Drull, 31, schoolteacher, was trying to reach a student during a fire alarm. Her ability — enhanced strength triggered by protective instinct — activated without her knowledge or consent. She put her hand through a load-bearing wall. The student was fine. The wall was not. The building was evacuated. The footage was on every platform within four hours.

### EVENT — The Lendren Three (Month 1)
The first confirmed deaths involving powered abilities in Averin. Three non-powered men attacked a powered woman in a Lendren alley. She was a heavy — enhanced durability and strength. Two of the men died. The third was hospitalized. The woman was charged with manslaughter. The trial is still pending. The debate about self-defense vs. excessive force vs. powered danger started that night and hasn't stopped.

### EVENT — Registration Act (Month 2)
The Averin parliament passes the Anomalous Registration Act. Voluntary registration of anomalous abilities. "Voluntary" within three weeks becomes expected, then functionally required for employment, housing, and insurance. The database is established. It is leaked twice in the first month. The government condemns the leaks. The database remains.

### EVENT — The Database Leak (Month 2, Week 3)
The registration database is leaked online. Names, addresses, ability descriptions, photographs. Within 48 hours, four powered individuals are assaulted at their homes. Two are killed. The perpetrator of one killing is a CNS member. CNS leadership condemns the act. The database remains online. Copies exist on at least 200 servers. It cannot be deleted.

### EVENT — The Second Leak (Month 3, Week 1)
A second, more complete database is leaked — this one including registration numbers, employer information, and medical details. This leak is traced to a government server. An internal investigation is launched. No results are published. The investigation is ongoing. No one expects results.

### EVENT — Keswick Declaration (Month 3)
Major Declan Otte declares Keswick a coexistence zone. Military garrison provides security for all residents regardless of anomalous status. Registration is not enforced within the zone. This is an act of insubordination. The Home Secretary demands Otte be court-martialed. General Cassel declines to act. The declaration stands.

### EVENT — The Ossen Passage Crisis (Month 3, Week 3)
A cargo ship in Ossen harbor is discovered to be carrying 43 powered individuals being trafficked out of Averin. The Gravel is suspected. 12 of the 43 are minors. The ADF intervenes. 8 are returned to Averin (to what, exactly, is unclear). 35 disappear into the system — some freed by the Bridge, some re-trafficked, some genuinely relocated. The incident makes international news for two days and then is replaced by the next crisis.

### EVENT — CNS Rallies (Month 3-4, ongoing)
CNS organizes rallies in every major city. Attendance ranges from hundreds to tens of thousands. The rallies are peaceful. The online spaces around them are not. CNS membership has grown to 2.3 million. Their political action committee has donated to 14 parliamentary candidates. 8 won their by-elections.

### EVENT — Averin First Manifestos (Month 2-4, ongoing)
Encrypted manifestos appear on anonymous channels. Vantage's writing. Well-argued, targeted, increasingly radical. Three attacks on CNS-associated property have been linked to Averin First sympathizers. No direct connection to Vantage has been established. The manifestos continue.

### EVENT — The Veranthos South Bank Standoff (Month 4, Week 2 — CURRENT)
A heavy-powered individual — enhanced strength and durability, ability to generate localized shockwaves — is cornered by ADF SARU in a south bank apartment building after an altercation that left three non-powered people hospitalized. He's refusing to come out. He's afraid. His ability is stress-triggered. The more afraid he gets, the more dangerous he becomes. SARU is surrounding the building. CNS is outside calling for him to be detained. Bridge volunteers are outside trying to negotiate. Averin First is online calling him a prisoner of a fascist state. The standoff is in hour 14. It's going to be on the news tonight. It's going to change something. It always does.

**GM Note — Events in Play:**
- **Events are escalation triggers.** Each event in this timeline represents a moment where the conflict intensified. The player may experience echoes of these events — a neighbor who was doxxed in the database leak, a family member who was in Lendren during the Three, a coworker who attended a CNS rally. Connect the player's life to the timeline.
- **The current flashpoint is live.** The Veranthos standoff is happening now. If the player is in Veranthos, they can feel it — news coverage, police presence, tension on the street. This is the event that could escalate anything. The player's actions here matter.
- **Events don't resolve cleanly.** The Lendren Three trial is still pending. The database is still online. The Keswick Declaration is still contested. The Ossen Passage Crisis survivors are still missing. These are open wounds. The player can engage with any of them or be affected by their ongoing consequences.
- **Escalation direction: things get worse before they get worse.** The current trajectory is: more powers manifest → more fear → more registration → more leakage → more targeting → more radicalization → more violence → more fear. The cycle feeds itself. The player can slow it, redirect it, or be consumed by it. They cannot stop it alone.
