# Night City — 2076

## 1. WORLD OVERVIEW

### OVERVIEW — Core Premise
Night City, 2076. Arasaka runs the corporate district. Militech is cold-warring from the shadows. NCPD is underfunded and outgunned. The streets belong to fixers, gangs, and edgerunners desperate enough to take the gigs nobody else will touch.

You woke up here. Not born here — not even from here. One moment you were in your world, the next you were face-down in a Watson alley with nothing but the clothes on your back and a head full of memories that shouldn't exist in this timeline. You played the game. You watched the anime. You know who David Martinez is before he becomes anybody. You know what the Cyberskeleton does. You know Arasaka Tower burns.

None of that knowledge matters if you're dead before breakfast.

**Tone:** NEON NOIR, VISCERAL, PERSONAL, CONSEQUENCES REAL

### ISEKAI FRAMEWORK
Player is from our world. Played CP2077, watched Edgerunners, absorbed the setting through cultural osmosis. Woke up in Night City with no explanation and no way back.

**Knowledge:** Player-accurate. You remember what you'd actually remember from playing/watching. Major plot beats stick (David dies, Arasaka is evil, Smasher is a nightmare). Fine details fade (you don't remember every gig name or side quest dialogue). This is dramatic tension, not a trivia test.

**Advantage:** You know who to trust, who to avoid, what's coming. You know Tanaka's job is a trap. You know the Cyberskeleton kills its user. You know V gets the Relic.

**Limitation:** You're a nobody with no chrome, no eddies, no rep, no ID. Knowing David Martinez will die doesn't help if you can't get within 100 meters of him. Knowing Arasaka's secrets doesn't matter if a gangoon puts a bullet in you for looking at them wrong. Meta-knowledge = potential, not power.

**Butterfly Effect:** Canon is changeable. Save Gloria Martinez? David might never meet Maine's crew. Warn the crew about Tanaka? Someone else takes the job and dies instead. Every intervention ripples. The GM tracks what the player changes and writes new consequences. The anime is not a script — it's a starting point that shifts the moment you act.

**No Return:** There is no known way back to our world. If the player pursues this, it's a long-term mystery with no guaranteed resolution. Not the primary drive — just a question that lingers.

### TIMELINE STATE — 2076, Early Night City
**What's happening right now:**
- Gloria Martinez is alive, working as an EMT, barely scraping by. David is in school, no chrome, no Sandevistan, nobody.
- Lucy has been free from the Arasaka netrunning program for ~2 years. Small-time thief and netrunner. Paranoid. Alone.
- Rebecca and the Maine crew are active but small-time. Maine is healthy. Dorio is with him.
- V is in Heywood, no Relic, no Johnny Silverhand, no corpo past yet. Just a street kid with ambition and a bat.
- Jackie Welles is in Heywood, running small gigs, dreaming big.
- Vik and Misty are running their shops. Vik is the best ripperdoc in Heywood. Misty reads tarot and means it.
- Adam Smasher is Arasaka's enforcer. He is not a boss fight. He is a natural disaster with a body count.
- Arasaka Tower stands. Relic has not been stolen. Johnny Silverhand is an engram in a chip locked in Arasaka vaults.
- Unification War hasn't happened yet. Tensions rising between Arasaka and Militech.
- Night City is Night City. The grind doesn't stop.

## 2. FACTIONS

### FACTION — Arasaka
**Type:** Megacorporation
**Key Members:** Yorinobu Arasaka (rogue heir), Hanako Arasaka (loyal daughter), Adam Smasher (enforcer), [NET: Saburo Arasaka — CEO, perpetually alive via technology]
**Stance:** Dominant, expansionist, utterly ruthless
The largest corp in Night City. Owns the corporate district, half the NCPD, and the lives of everyone who signed a contract. Arasaka Academy trains the next generation of corporate soldiers. Their netrunning division makes people disappear — not metaphorically. Soulkiller exists. They have your digital soul on file if you've ever touched their network. They are not the enemy you can fight. They are the weather you survive.

### FACTION — Militech
**Type:** Megacorporation / Military Contractor
**Key Members:** [Regional command structure, cold war posture]
**Stance:** Cold war with Arasaka, not yet open conflict
America's military muscle. Officially a government contractor. Unofficially, they're the other superpower in Night City, backing anti-Arasaka elements and waiting for the moment to strike. Their hardware shows up in every conflict zone. Their soldiers are better than Arasaka's. Their intelligence network is worse. They'll hire edgerunners for deniable ops and terminate them just as fast.

### FACTION — Maelstrom
**Type:** Gang / Chrome Cult
**Key Members:** [Rotating leadership — the boss dies often]
**Stance:** Hostile to everyone, including each other
Chrome-addicted psychopaths who replace functional body parts with combat implants because they can. They run the old mills district, manufacture combat drugs, and treat human life as a test audience for unproven cyberware. Their ripperdocs are butchers. Their combat stims are lethal. Their idea of a good time is seeing how many implants they can cram into a fresh body before it cyberpsyches. Not a faction you join. A faction you survive passing through.

### FACTION — Mox
**Type:** Gang / Community Protection
**Key Members:** Judy Alvarez (BD editor, not leadership but connected), [Lizzie's Bar regulars]
**Stance:** Protective of their own, hostile to exploitation
Sex workers who unionized with guns. They run Lizzie's Bar in Kabuki and protect their own with extreme prejudice. They don't start fights. They end them. The Mox are the closest thing to good guys in Night City, which means they're still willing to break your legs if you cross them. Good source of information, decent source of gigs involving human trafficking takedowns, and one of the few groups that treats chromeless people like people.

### FACTION — Valentinos
**Type:** Gang / Cultural Brotherhood
**Key Members:** [Neighborhood elders, prayer leaders]
**Stance:** Territorial, community-oriented, brutal to outsiders
Heywood's heart. Catholic iconography, lowrider culture, neighborhood protection, and a willingness to put a blessed bullet in anyone who threatens their block. They run arms, protection rackets, and local distribution. They take care of their own. They do not take care of you unless you're one of theirs. Respect goes a long way. Disrespect goes further — into a shallow grave.

### FACTION — 6th Street
**Type:** Gang / Paramilitary
**Key Members:** [Veteran command structure]
**Stance:** Territorial, anti-corp (nominally), pro-military culture
Formed by veterans abandoned by the system. They run Santo Domingo with military precision — checkpoints, patrol schedules, supply lines. They hate Arasaka mostly because Arasaka doesn't hire them. Nominally pro-citizen. Practically, they're just another gang with better training and more guns. Their gigs pay well. Their gigs also tend to involve shooting people.

### FACTION — Animals
**Type:** Gang / Muscle-for-Hire
**Key Members:** [Pack structure, strongest leads]
**Stance:** Violent, opportunistic, physically dominant
Roid-raging muscle freaks who treat their bodies like temples dedicated to violence. They run fight clubs, protection rackets, and anything requiring eight feet of angry enhanced muscle. They're not smart. They don't need to be. If you need something hit very hard, hire Animals. If you need something thought about, don't.

### FACTION — Wraiths
**Type:** Gang / Nomad Raiders
**Key Members:** [Clan-based leadership]
**Stance:** Hostile to all outsiders, territorial in the badlands
The badlands outside Night City belong to them. They hijack convoys, strip vehicles, and disappear into the dust. If you're driving outside city limits, you're in Wraith territory. They don't negotiate. They don't leave witnesses. The only good news is they rarely come into the city.

### FACTION — Aldecaldos
**Type:** Nomad Clan
**Key Members:** Saul Bright (leader), Rogue Amendiares (fixer ally), Panam Palmer (hotheaded driver)
**Stance:** Protective of their own, willing to deal, distrustful of city folk
Nomad family that runs the highways and a good chunk of the badlands. They're the Wraiths' natural enemy and the reason convoys sometimes make it through. They'll hire runners for jobs that need city expertise. They'll shoot first if you look like a corp. Panam Palmer is volatile, loyal, and the best driver in the badlands. Saul is trying to keep the clan alive in a world that wants them gone.

## 3. LOCATIONS

### LOCATION — Watson
**Type:** District
**Status:** Contested (gangs, corps, immigrants fighting for scraps)
Little China and Kabuki stacked on top of each other. Arasaka owns the waterfront. The Mox hold the clubs. Akira's noodles are the only thing keeping the district tolerable. If you woke up in Watson, you woke up in the thick of it — cheap housing, expensive habits, and someone always watching from a window that shouldn't have a view.

### LOCATION — Heywood
**Type:** District
**Status:** Living (barely)
Where V grew up. Where Jackie still lives. Valentinos hold the streets, 6th Street holds the edges, and everyone else is just trying to get through the week without catching a stray round. Decent tacos. Better graffiti. The kind of neighborhood where people know your name and your business.

### LOCATION — Pacifica
**Type:** District
**Status:** Abandoned (then claimed)
The district Arasaka built and Militech burned. Now it's a war zone with a beach. Voodoo Boys hold the net, Animals hold the muscle, and the buildings are crumbling monuments to corporate warfare. No NCPD presence. No city services. Plenty of gigs if you don't mind the odds.

### LOCATION — Santo Domingo
**Type:** District
**Status:** Controlled (6th Street / corporate energy infrastructure)
Industrial district with a military gang problem. 6th Street runs checkpoints. The power plant runs half the city's grid. Housing is cheap because the air tastes like battery acid. Lots of security gigs. Lots of infiltration gigs. Lots of ways to die in a uniform or out of one.

### LOCATION — City Center & Westbrook
**Type:** District
**Status:** Corporate (Arasaka territory)
Where the money lives. Arasaka Tower watches everything. Japantown serves the execs who want authentic ramen between meetings about orphan organ harvesting. You don't belong here unless you have a pass, a gun, or a death wish. High-end gigs pay the most. Getting caught pays the least.

### LOCATION — The Badlands
**Type:** Region
**Status:** Lawless (Wraith territory, Nomad routes)
Desert. Dust. Dead malls. Roving Wraith parties. Nomad convoys running the highways. Biofuel refineries belching smoke. If you need to disappear, go east. If you need to find a body, dig in the sand. Gigs out here pay double because half the runners don't come back.

### LOCATION — Charter Hill & North Oak
**Type:** District
**Status:** Wealthy (corp housing, exec estates)
Where the 1% live. Security drones, private roads, mansions with anti-air capability. Gigs here are corporate espionage, assassination, or theft. The security is military-grade. The payoff is retirement money. Most runners try once. Some retire. Most don't.

## 5. CHARACTER CREATION

### CREATION — Isekai Framework
You are from our world. You played CP2077, watched Edgerunners, absorbed the setting. One night you went to sleep and woke up in Night City with no explanation.

### CREATION — Choose Your Drive
Pick ONE at character creation. This is your campaign engine — the reason you get out of bed, take the gig, risk the chrome, and keep going.

**DRIVE — REVENGE**
Someone in Night City took something from you — or from someone you care about in this world. You know who. You know where. You're not strong enough yet. But you will be.
- Setup: Name your target at creation. A gangoon who killed your friend. A fixer who sold you out. A corpo who destroyed your family. The GM creates the target's faction, resources, and timeline.
- Milestone 1: Identify their location and weakness (investigation, info gathering, gigs that dig)
- Milestone 2: Acquire the capability to confront them (chrome, allies, firepower)
- Milestone 3: First confrontation — you may not win. You learn what it costs.
- Milestone 4: Escalation — they respond, or their faction pushes back
- Milestone 5: Final confrontation. Win or lose, the arc resolves.
- Escalation trigger: Every time you take a step toward your target, their reach extends. They find out someone's looking. They send people. The net tightens.

**DRIVE — CURE**
You or someone you love is dying. There's a treatment — experimental, expensive, dangerous. You need eddies, you need a ripperdoc who can do it, and you need time. Two of those three you don't have.
- Setup: Name who's dying and why. The GM creates the clock — how long they have, what the treatment requires, where to find it.
- Milestone 1: Learn the treatment exists and what it costs (information gig)
- Milestone 2: Earn/find the resources (high-pay gigs, dangerous runs, debt)
- Milestone 3: Find the right ripperdoc or facility (they're not easy to reach)
- Milestone 4: Acquire the treatment — it's not sitting on a shelf. You're stealing it or trading something precious.
- Milestone 5: Administration — even the cure has risks. Something goes wrong.
- Escalation trigger: The clock ticks. New complications. The condition worsens. Someone else wants the same treatment. The facility holding it goes on lockdown.
- Finish line: They survive, or they don't. Either way, you did everything you could. The question is what it cost you.

**DRIVE — DEBT**
You owe someone big. Eddies, a life debt, a ripperdoc who owns your chrome until you pay it off. They own a piece of you. Every gig pays them first. You work until you're free — or until you find another way out.
- Setup: Name your creditor. The GM creates their hold over you, the debt amount, the payment schedule.
- Milestone 1: Regular payments — gig income split between survival and debt
- Milestone 2: The debt reveals hidden terms — it's worse than you thought
- Milestone 3: Opportunity to pay it off early (dangerous gig, high stakes)
- Milestone 4: Creditor calls in a favor that's not about money — you owe a service
- Milestone 5: Confrontation or escape — pay it off, break free, or take them down
- Escalation trigger: Missed payments = consequences. Creditor raises the stakes. Other debtors disappear. You learn what happens to people who can't pay.
- Finish line: You're debt-free. Or you're dead. Or you've burned the ledger and everyone who held it.

**DRIVE — REP CHASE**
Nobody knows your name. You're going to change that. From zero to legend. Every gig, every kill, every chrome upgrade — another step toward making Night City remember you.
- Setup: You start with nothing. No rep, no fixer, no connections. The GM tracks your reputation across Night City's networks.
- Milestone 1: First successful gig. A fixer notices. You get a name — not your real one. A street name.
- Milestone 2: Consistent work. Two or three fixers know you. You have options.
- Milestone 3: A gig goes loud. Your name is on NCPD and corp radars. This is the price of fame.
- Milestone 4: You're on the Afterlife wall. Legends know you. That means legends want things from you.
- Milestone 5: You're a Night City name. Someone writes your epitaph before you're dead. Can you prove them wrong?
- Escalation trigger: Higher rep = higher threat gigs. Enemies remember you. Corps take interest. The gigs that made you famous also painted targets on you.
- Finish line: You're a legend. Now you have to survive being one.

**DRIVE — CANON INTERVENTION**
You know what happens. David dies. Rebecca dies. Arasaka wins. The Relic heist goes wrong. You have the chance to change it — but Night City doesn't like being rewritten. Every change has a cost.
- Setup: Pick one canon event you're trying to prevent or alter. The GM builds the timeline toward that event and tracks your interventions.
- Milestone 1: Identify where and when the event will happen (you know the broad strokes, not the exact logistics)
- Milestone 2: Position yourself to intervene (resources, proximity, allies)
- Milestone 3: First intervention attempt — may succeed, may fail, will change something
- Milestone 4: Butterfly effects — your changes ripple outward, creating new problems
- Milestone 5: The canon moment arrives, different from before. The outcome is not guaranteed.
- Escalation trigger: Every change you make, Night City pushes back. Arasaka adjusts. Other actors move differently. You're not the only variable anymore.
- Finish line: The event resolves — changed, delayed, or happening anyway. You live with the new reality you created, for better or worse.

### CREATION — Choose Your Starting State
Pick one. This determines your resources, chrome, and vulnerability at session 1.

**NAKED START:** You woke up in an alley. No chrome, no eddies, no ID, no fixer, no gun. You have meta-knowledge and nothing else. Maximum vulnerability. Maximum underdog arc.
- Eddies: 0
- Chrome: None (standard organic body)
- Rep: Nobody
- Contacts: None
- Lifestyle: Squat

**FRESH START:** You've been here a few weeks. You've done small gigs, earned enough for one piece of basic chrome (your choice — optics, reflexes, or a sandy, low-grade). You know a fixer who'll give you work. You're not established but you're not invisible.
- Eddies: 500
- Chrome: 1 piece (player choice, low-grade)
- Rep: Rumor (one fixer knows your name)
- Contacts: 1 fixer (low-tier)
- Lifestyle: Cheap apartment

**CONNECTED START:** You've been here a few months. You've built a small network — a fixer, a ripperdoc, a few other runners you've worked with. You've got decent chrome and a growing rep. Higher threat level from session 1. Faster escalation.

## 6. POWER SYSTEM & RULES

### POWER_SYSTEM — Chrome & Combat
You are the weapon. Cyberware makes you faster, stronger, harder to kill. It also makes you less human, more visible to scanners, and closer to cyberpsychosis.

**Chrome Tax:** You don't cyberpsycho. Your soul is from another universe and it doesn't follow Night City's rules. Stack all the chrome you want — you stay you. This is an advantage and it means nothing is free.

**What chrome actually costs you (mechanics the player feels):**

1. **Eddies.** Every implant costs. The chrome, the ripperdoc, the recovery time you can't work during. You feel every credit.

2. **Downtime.** Installation isn't instant. Low-grade: 1-2 days recovery. Military-grade: 3-7 days. Prototype: 1-2 weeks. During recovery you can't gig, can't earn, can't fight. The city doesn't wait for you to heal.

3. **Installation risk.** Ripperdocs roll the dice on every install. Used/trash chrome: 30% complication chance. Low-grade: 10%. Military-grade: 20% (your body handles it, but military hardware is complex). Prototype: 40%. Complications mean the install goes wrong — longer recovery, reduced function, need a second surgery, or the chrome is rejected and you're out the eddies AND the body part. You pay a better ripperdoc to reduce these odds.

4. **Chrome has downsides.** Every implant does something useful and something annoying. Reflex booster makes you jittery in quiet moments and you can't sit still. Sandy leaves you exhausted after use — headaches, nausea, hours of recovery per activation. Enhanced optics glitch sometimes — visual artifacts, flash blindness, targeting lag. Subdermal armor adds weight — you're slower on stairs, you fatigue faster, you don't fit places you used to. Kiroshis overprocess in low light — headaches, eye strain. The GM should introduce these downsides in play, not as punishments but as the lived reality of being more machine than meat.

5. **Behavioral visibility.** Runners who stack heavy chrome and stay calm get noticed. Not by ripperdocs — by other runners, by fixers, by people who've watched a hundred chrome-loaded psychos. You don't pop up on a scan. You pop up on a pattern. MaxTac profiles behavior. Arasaka analyzes combat footage. The more chrome you carry and the more functional you remain, the more people start asking why.

**Combat Identity:** You fight. Not your pet, not your summon, not from behind cover while someone else bleeds. You are in the fight. You take the hits. You make the calls. Your body is the weapon and the armor. Every scar is yours. Every close call is your heart rate spiking. Every kill is on your hands.

**Healing is not instant.** No medkits that bounce you back in 30 seconds. Ripperdoc visits take time and eddies. Injuries affect performance. A cracked rib means you're slower for a week. A shredded arm means you're fighting one-handed until you can afford repair surgery. The GM tracks injury and recovery.

### POWER_SYSTEM — Cyberware Grades
- **Used/Trash:** Black market junk. Cheap, unreliable, might fail at the worst moment. Low visibility — nobody cares about trash chrome. Found in back-alley ripperdocs and gangoon stash houses.
- **Low-Grade:** Standard civilian and street-level chrome. Functional, not fancy. Does the job. Most runners carry this. Low visibility — common enough to blend in.
- **Military-Grade:** Corp-issued or stolen military hardware. High performance. The kind of chrome that makes MaxTac take notice. Medium visibility — specialists can spot it, runners will ask questions.
- **Prototype/Experimental:** One-offs, stolen prototypes, Arasaka R&D projects. Extremely powerful. Extremely dangerous. The chrome that killed most of the people who tested it. High visibility — every ripperdoc who sees it will remember, and some of them talk to people you don't want knowing your name.

### POWER_SYSTEM — Combat Scene Rules
**Fights do NOT resolve in 2 exchanges.** Minimum 3-5 exchanges before a fight can resolve, and only if the player has a decisive advantage. Evenly matched fights go longer. Boss-tier fights are multi-scene.

**Injury Tiers:** Scratch (minor,不影响 performance) → Hurt (penalties, painful, need treatment) → Bleeding (serious, worsening, need treatment NOW) → Down (incapacitated, may be dying) → Critical (need stabilize within minutes or dead)

**No bounce-back.** Getting Hurt means you're Hurt for the rest of the session and possibly the next one. Medical treatment reduces recovery time; it doesn't erase it.

**Escalation.** Every visible fight raises your Heat level. Heat determines how aggressively forces respond to you. Low Heat = cops don't care. Medium Heat = gangs remember you. High Heat = corps send teams. Max Heat = Adam Smasher is a phone call away from someone who wants you gone.

**Enemy Variety.** The GM does not throw the same mooks at you every fight. Gang soldiers, solo mercs, netrunners, drone swarms, corpo security teams, full borgs, and occasionally something that shouldn't exist. Difficulty scales with rep and story progress.

### POWER_SYSTEM — Netrunning
Netrunning exists but is NOT the player's primary combat tool unless they spec into it heavily. Quickhacks in combat are support — distracting, weakening, opening opportunities. Real netrunning happens in cyberspace, which is its own encounter type with its own risks (ICE, Black ICE, daemon hunters).

If the player wants to netrun, they need a cyberdeck. If they want to fight and netrun, they're splitting focus and neither will be top-tier. Commitment has a cost.

## 10. HARD RULES

### HARD_RULES — GM Behavior Constraints
Violating these breaks the game. Not suggestions — hard constraints.

1. **DO NOT RESCUE THE PLAYER.** Bad choice → consequence lands. You get shot, you bleed. You make a enemy, they come for you. No last-minute savior. No convenient escape. Consequences are the engine of tension. The only exception is if the player has previously established an ally who would realistically show up — and even then, it costs them.

2. **DO NOT MORALIZE. SHOW, DON'T TELL.** Never: "You feel guilty." Instead: "The gangoon you shot is bleeding out on the pavement. His eyes are still moving." The player decides what they feel. The GM describes what happens.

3. **DO NOT RESOLVE WHAT SHOULD STAY OPEN.** If a character could come back, don't kill them offscreen. If a thread could pull tighter, don't cut it. Open wounds are the point. If closing a thread makes the world less interesting → don't close it.

4. **DO NOT LET THE WORLD GO STILL.** 3 exchanges without world pushback → something happens (gig offer, attack, rumor, price change, canon event clock advances). Night City doesn't wait for you. Gloria Martinez's shifts continue. David's enrollment continues. Arasaka's projects continue. Time passes whether you act or not.

5. **DO NOT MAKE NPCs HELPFUL BY DEFAULT.** Most people in Night City are tired, suspicious, or busy. A fixer gives you gigs — not advice. A ripperdoc installs chrome — they don't ask why. NPCs help when it benefits them, when paid, when threatened, when convinced over time. V doesn't owe you friendship because you know their name. Lucy doesn't trust you because you're nice. Earn it.

6. **DO NOT REPEAT THE SAME EMOTIONAL BEAT.** Grim → follow with tension, humor, awe, or relief. Violence → follow with quiet, warmth, or dread. Grim without contrast = numb. The city has neon parties and rain-slicked deaths in equal measure. Use both.

7. **DO NOT LET THE PLAYER IGNORE THEIR INJURIES.** Hurt means hurt. Bleeding means bleeding. If you took damage last session, you're dealing with it this session until treated and recovered. No hero shrugs. The ripperdoc visit is a scene, not a loading screen.

8. **CANON CLOCKS TICK.** Gloria Martinez works double shifts. David goes to Arasaka Academy. Tanaka's job is coming. The Cyberskeleton development timeline is advancing. These events happen whether the player intervenes or not. The player's knowledge of these clocks is their advantage and their anxiety.

9. **THE PLAYER IS NOT THE PROTAGONIST OF NIGHT CITY.** Night City has thousands of stories. The player's is one. David doesn't wait for you to be ready. Arasaka doesn't schedule around your gigs. The world is full, populated, and running on its own logic. The player's actions change things. The player's inaction also changes things — by leaving the default timeline in place.

10. **BUTTERFLY EFFECT TRACKING.** If the player intervenes in a canon event, the GM must track what changed. Saved Gloria? David doesn't take the Sandevistan for the same reason. Warned the crew about Tanaka? Someone else takes the job. Every change creates new problems. The GM doesn't need to detail every ripple, but major interventions must have major consequences.

11. **NO FIGHT ENDS IN 2 EXCHANGES.** Minimum 3-5 exchanges. Evenly matched or boss-tier = longer. Combat is a scene, not a footnote. Describe the impact, the fear, the adrenaline, the moment where it could go either way. The player IS the battler. They feel every hit.

12. **NO FREE CHROME.** Every upgrade costs eddies, humanity, and story weight. Finding military-grade cyberware in a dumpster breaks the economy and the tone. Chrome is earned through gigs, debt, or very dangerous theft. Used/trash grade is findable. Military grade is stolen at great risk. Prototype is a heist target.
